# Payer-Specific Appeals: Comprehensive Reference for Denials Doctor

## Overview

Each health insurance payer has unique appeal processes, forms, timelines, and requirements. While the general appeal principles are similar across payers, the specific details matter enormously — filing a Medicare appeal correctly does not mean you know how to file a UHC appeal, and a BCBS of Texas appeal is different from a BCBS of California appeal.

This document provides detailed, payer-specific appeal guidance for all major payers, including exact submission procedures, forms, contact information, and strategies unique to each payer.

---

## 1. Medicare Appeals

### Overview of Medicare Structure

Medicare is administered by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) through:
- **Medicare Administrative Contractors (MACs)**: Handle Part A and Part B claims processing and Level 1 appeals
- **Qualified Independent Contractors (QICs)**: Handle Level 2 reconsiderations (C2C Solutions for Part A, Maximus for Part B)
- **OMHA (Office of Medicare Hearings and Appeals)**: Handles Level 3 ALJ hearings
- **Medicare Appeals Council**: Handles Level 4 reviews (under the Departmental Appeals Board)
- **Federal District Courts**: Handle Level 5 judicial review

### Medicare Appeal Strategy Tips

1. **Level 1 is the easiest reversal point**: The MAC will assign a different reviewer than the one who processed the claim. Success rate is 50-60%.
2. **Submit new evidence at Level 2**: The QIC wants to see that you addressed the Level 1 deficiencies. If you submit the same evidence that was rejected at Level 1, the QIC will likely uphold the denial.
3. **Request an in-person or video hearing at Level 3**: Paper reviews at Level 3 have the lowest success rate of the hearing types. In-person or video hearings allow the treating physician to present the case directly to the ALJ.
4. **The $180 minimum for Level 3 is per appeal**: You can aggregate multiple claims with the same issue to meet the minimum controversy amount.
5. **ALJ hearings have the highest success rate**: 60-70% of provider appeals succeed at the ALJ level. Do not give up after Level 2.
6. **Exhaust all levels before going to court**: You cannot skip levels. Each level must be exhausted before proceeding to the next.
7. **Document receipt of all deadlines**: When you receive a denial, immediately calendar the next appeal deadline.
8. **For RAC denials**: The appeal process is the same, but the deadline runs from the RAC demand letter date, not the original remittance date.

### Medicare Part C (Medicare Advantage) Appeals

Medicare Advantage plans (e.g., Aetna Medicare, UHC Medicare Advantage, Anthem Medicare) have a slightly different process:

| Level | Name | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|
| Level 1 | Plan Redetermination | File with the MA plan directly. 60 days from denial. 30-day decision. |
| Level 2 | IRE Reconsideration | Independent Review Entity (Maximus or C2C). 60 days from Level 1 decision. |
| Level 3-5 | Same as original Medicare | ALJ, Appeals Council, Federal Court. Same minimum controversy amounts. |

**Key differences from Original Medicare:**
- Level 1 is 60 days to file (not 120)
- Level 1 decision is 30 days (not 60)
- Plans have specific forms (not CMS-10003)
- Plans use their own medical policies (not LCDs/NCDs exclusively)
- For Part D drug denials, the process is similar but uses Medicare Part D coverage rules

### UHC-Specific Appeal Features

**UHC Gold Card Program:**
- Providers with Gold Card status may be exempt from certain prior authorization requirements
- If a claim for a Gold Card provider is denied for no prior auth, appeal citing Gold Card status
- Gold Card status is reviewed annually based on the provider's compliance rate

**UHC Clinical Peer Review (Level 2):**
- Available for medical necessity denials
- Involves a UHC medical director or physician reviewer in the same specialty
- The treating physician should participate in the phone call
- More effective than a written appeal for complex clinical cases

**UHC Expedited Appeals:**
- Available for ongoing treatment decisions
- Must demonstrate that the standard timeline would cause harm
- Decision within 72 hours
- Call UHC Provider Services to initiate

**UHC State-Specific Rules:**
- California: UHC must comply with the California Department of Managed Health Care (DMHC) appeal standards
- New York: UHC must comply with NY Department of Financial Services (DFS) prompt pay and appeal laws
- Texas: UHC must comply with Texas Department of Insurance requirements

### UHC Appeal Strategy Tips

1. **Use the portal**: UHC's portal provides the fastest processing and real-time status tracking
2. **Include the authorization number**: If prior auth was obtained, always include it in the appeal
3. **For medical necessity denials**: Request a clinical peer review before filing a Level 2 — this informal conversation can resolve the issue faster
4. **UHC may require "differential diagnoses"**: For some medical necessity denials, UHC asks what other diagnoses were considered and ruled out
5. **For UHC Community Plan (Medicaid)**: Each state has separate rules and contacts — check the state-specific UHC provider manual
6. **UHC Optum denials**: Some UHC denials originate from Optum (their clinical review subsidiary). The appeal goes to UHC, not Optum.
7. **Check the plan type**: HMO, PPO, POS, and Medicare Advantage plans each have different UHC appeal contacts

### Aetna Clinical Policy Bulletins (CPBs)

Aetna's medical necessity decisions are based on Clinical Policy Bulletins (CPBs). When appealing an Aetna medical necessity denial:

1. **Identify the specific CPB**: Find the CPB that addresses the denied service
2. **Read the CPB criteria**: Understand exactly what criteria the patient must meet
3. **Map clinical documentation to CPB criteria**: Show how the patient meets each criterion
4. **Cite the CPB in your appeal**: Reference the CPB number, title, and version date
5. **If CPB criteria are outdated**: Cite more recent peer-reviewed literature or specialty guidelines that have evolved since the CPB was published

CPBs are available at: www.aetna.com/health-care-professionals/clinical-policy-bulletins

### Aetna Appeal Strategy Tips

1. **Aetna CPBs are not always updated**: Check the review date on the CPB. If the CPB is more than 2 years old, cite more recent evidence.
2. **Aetna Case Management**: For complex cases (high-cost, chronic conditions), request a case manager who can coordinate across the appeal process.
3. **Aetna Institute for Medical Excellence**: This internal group develops CPBs. In rare cases, appeal to this institute for policy-level disputes.
4. **Expedited appeals**: Aetna offers expedited review for ongoing care. Decision in 72 hours.
5. **Aetna Better Health (Medicaid)**: Each state has separate appeal rules. Contact the specific Aetna Better Health plan.
6. **Retrospective vs. prospective**: Aetna treats retrospective (claim-level) appeals differently from prospective (pre-service) appeals. Claim-level appeals have a 180-day window.
7. **Medical record submission**: Aetna strongly prefers electronic medical record submission through the portal. Avoid faxing medical records if possible.

## 5. Blue Cross Blue Shield (BCBS) Appeals

### Overview: The BCBS System

BCBS is not a single company — it is a federation of 34 independent Blue Cross and Blue Shield companies. Each company:
- Has its own appeal process
- Sets its own timelines and requirements
- Uses its own forms and portals
- Has its own medical policies
- Determines its own coverage criteria

This means there is NO single "BCBS appeal process." Each plan is different.

To appeal a BCBS denial, you must know:
1. Which specific BCBS plan processed the claim (e.g., BCBS of Texas, Anthem BCBS of California, Highmark BCBS of Pennsylvania, CareFirst BCBS of Maryland)
2. The plan type (HMO, PPO, POS, Indemnity, FEP, Medicare Advantage)

### General Workers' Comp Appeal Strategy Tips

1. **State-specific forms**: Most states require specific forms for each appeal level. Do not use a generic letter.
2. **Medical treatment guidelines**: Most states have specific guidelines (ACOEM, ODG, or state-specific). Know the applicable guidelines.
3. **UR vs. claim appeals**: Utilization Review (UR) denials follow a different path than claim/compensability denials. Know which type you are appealing.
4. **Deadlines are strict**: Workers' Comp deadlines are typically short (20-60 days) and are strictly enforced. Missing a deadline can permanently bar the claim.
5. **Physician involvement**: The treating physician must typically request UR reconsideration. The billing office cannot do it alone.
6. **Fee schedule disputes**: Medical fee schedule disputes are separate from clinical necessity disputes and go through different processes.
7. **IME (Independent Medical Examination)**: The carrier may request an IME to challenge the treating physician's opinion. Be prepared to respond with a rebuttal report.

## 9. Medicaid Managed Care Appeals

### Overview

Most Medicaid beneficiaries are now covered by Managed Care Organizations (MCOs). Each MCO has its own appeal process, but all must meet the federal Medicaid "state fair hearing" requirement.

### General Medicaid Managed Care Appeal Process

| Step | Description | Timeline |
|------|-------------|----------|
| MCO Internal Appeal | File with the MCO | Usually 30-180 days from denial |
| MCO Second Level (if available) | Additional MCO review | Usually 30-60 days |
| State Fair Hearing | State administrative hearing | Usually 90-120 days from MCO decision |

### MCO Appeal Differences by State (Examples)

**California (Medi-Cal Managed Care):**
- MCO appeal: 90 days from denial
- State fair hearing: 90 days from MCO decision
- Medi-Cal managed care plans include: Anthem Blue Cross, Health Net, Blue Shield of California

**New York (NY Medicaid Managed Care):**
- MCO appeal: 60 days from denial
- State fair hearing: 120 days from MCO notice
- Managed care plans include: Fidelis Care, Healthfirst, EmblemHealth, MetroPlus

**Texas (STAR and CHIP):**
- MCO appeal: 30 days from denial
- State fair hearing: 120 days from MCO decision
- STAR plans include: Superior, UnitedHealthcare, BCBS, Cook Children's

**Florida (Medicaid Managed Care):**
- MCO appeal: 60 days from denial
- State fair hearing: 120 days from MCO decision

### Medicaid Fair Hearing Tips

1. **Beneficiary rights**: The beneficiary (patient) must be informed of their fair hearing rights in writing.
2. **Aid paid pending**: Beneficiaries can request continued benefits during the appeal (within 10 days of the notice). For providers, this means continued payment for ongoing treatment.
3. **Retroactive denials**: Claim-level denials go through the MCO internal appeal process, not the state fair hearing process.
4. **State-specific rules**: Each state has unique fair hearing procedures. Check with the state Medicaid agency.
5. **Expedited hearings**: Available if delaying the hearing would harm the patient's health. Decision within 3 days.

## Agent Q&A Pairs for Training

**Q: What is the Medicare appeal filing process for a Level 1 Redetermination?**
**A:** Medicare Level 1 (Redetermination) is filed with the MAC that processed the claim. You have 120 calendar days from the date of the remittance notice. You can use the CMS-10003 form or write an appeal letter. Include: (1) Beneficiary name and Medicare number (MBI), (2) Claim number and date of service, (3) Specific denial reason being appealed, (4) Detailed explanation of why the denial is incorrect, (5) Supporting documentation (medical records, clinical notes). Submit through the MAC's online portal (fastest), by fax, or by mail to the MAC's appeals address. The MAC must issue a decision within 60 calendar days. There is no filing fee for Level 1 appeals. Keep copies of everything submitted and the confirmation of receipt. For expedited (urgent) appeals, call the MAC directly — decision in 72 hours.

**Q: How do I appeal a UnitedHealthcare denial?**
**A:** UnitedHealthcare has a 3-level appeal process. Level 1 (Claim Appeal): File within 180 days of the remittance via the UHC Provider Portal (recommended), fax, or mail. Decision in 30-45 days. Level 2 (Clinical Peer Review): File within 60 days of the Level 1 denial. A physician in the same specialty will review the case. Request a peer-to-peer review if available. Level 3 (External Independent Review): File within 60 days of the Level 2 denial. An IRO (like MAXIMUS) conducts the review. Decision is binding on UHC. UHC-specific tips: Use the portal for fastest processing; include authorization numbers if prior auth was obtained; for medical necessity denials, call to request a clinical peer review before filing a formal Level 2; check if the provider has Gold Card status.

**Q: What is the Aetna Clinical Policy Bulletin and how do I use it in an appeal?**
**A:** An Aetna Clinical Policy Bulletin (CPB) is a document that outlines Aetna's medical necessity criteria for a specific service or procedure. Aetna has over 500 CPBs covering medical, surgical, behavioral health, and pharmacy services. To use a CPB in an appeal: (1) Go to www.aetna.com/health-care-professionals/clinical-policy-bulletins, (2) Search for the CPB covering the denied service (by keyword or CPT code), (3) Read the coverage criteria section — these are the specific requirements the patient must meet, (4) Map the patient's clinical documentation to each criterion, (5) Cite the CPB number, title, and version date in your appeal letter, (6) If the patient meets all criteria, explain how. If the CPB is outdated (no recent update), cite more recent peer-reviewed literature that supports the service. CPB-based appeals should focus on proving the patient meets Aetna's own published criteria.

**Q: How do I appeal a Blue Cross Blue Shield denial when there are 34 different BCBS plans?**

Check the member ID card: the card will show the specific BCBS company name (e.g., "BCBS of Texas," "Anthem BCBS of California," "Highmark BCBS," "CareFirst BCBS," "Regence BCBS," "Excellus BCBS"). Then: (1) Visit that specific plan's provider portal — each plan has its own portal, appeal addresses, and forms, (2) Look for the plan's appeal policy on their website, (3) Determine the appeal timeline — most BCBS plans offer 180 days for Level 1, but some offer 365 days, (4) Determine if the plan uses a specific appeal form or accepts written letters, (5) For BCBS FEP (Federal Employee Program): use the national FEP appeal process at P.O. Box 1340, Harrisburg, PA 17105-1340. Common BCBS mistakes: sending to the wrong address, using the wrong form, assuming one BCBS plan's policies apply to another, and missing state-specific deadlines.

**Q: What is the Tricare appeal process for a non-network provider?**
**A:** Tricare non-network provider appeals follow a 4-level process. Level 1 (Contractor Internal Review): File within 90 days of the remittance with the regional contractor (Humana Military for Tricare East, Health Net for Tricare West). Level 2 (National Quality Monitoring Contractor): File within 60 days of the Level 1 decision. Level 3 (Tricare Management Activity/TMA): File within 60 days of the Level 2 decision. Level 4 (Federal Court under the Contract Disputes Act): File within 60 days of the Level 3 decision. Non-network providers: do NOT use the patient's appeal process — use the provider-specific process. Key differences: Tricare uses Medicare coverage rules for many services; prior authorization is required for all inpatient admissions; the 90-day Level 1 window is shorter than most commercial payers; Tricare for Life appeals follow Medicare rules first.

**Q: What is the Workers' Compensation appeal process in California for a UR denial?**
**A:** California Workers' Comp UR (Utilization Review) denials have a specific process: (1) Request for reconsideration: File with the UR agent (the payer's review organization) within 30 days of the UR denial. The treating physician must submit this request. (2) If the reconsideration is denied: File for Independent Medical Review (IMR) within 30 days of the reconsideration denial. The IMR is conducted by a physician in the same specialty. The IMR decision is binding on all parties. (3) If the issue is not subject to IMR (e.g., compensability): File an Application for Adjudication with the Workers' Compensation Appeals Board (WCAB) within 1 year of the injury. **Q: How do I appeal a claim from a self-funded (ERISA) plan?**
**A:** Self-funded (ERISA) plans have their own appeal processes defined by the plan document (Summary Plan Description). The reviewer must be someone not involved in the initial decision. (2) Level 2 — Second Internal Appeal: Many ERISA plans require a second internal appeal before external review. Strategy: (1) Get a copy of the plan document (SPD) from the plan administrator or TPA, (2) Follow the appeal process EXACTLY as described in the document, (3) ERISA has strict "full and fair review" requirements — the plan must provide the basis for the denial and allow you to submit additional information, (4) State insurance laws do NOT apply to ERISA plans, (5) Document everything — ERISA lawsuits require a complete administrative record, (6) ERISA appeals are time-limited — do not assume state law deadlines apply.

**Q: What is the BCBS FEP appeal process?**

**Q: What is the difference between a Cigna administrative appeal and a clinical appeal?**

These are handled by Cigna's claims department, not clinical staff. They can often be submitted through the portal with corrected data. Clinical appeals involve: (1) Medical necessity denials, (2) Prior authorization denials, (3) Experimental/investigational denials, (4) Level of care disputes, (5) Benefit limit appeals. **Q: How do I appeal a denial from a Medicaid Managed Care Organization (MCO)?**

(2) State Fair Hearing: If the MCO appeal is denied, request a state fair hearing from the state Medicaid agency. The filing window is typically 90-120 days from the MCO's denial. The hearing is conducted by an administrative law judge. Key considerations: (1) The beneficiary has the right to continued benefits during the appeal (if requested within 10 days of the denial), (2) Each state has unique rules — check your state's Medicaid agency website, (3) MCOs for Medicaid follow managed care rules, not the state's fee-for-service rules, (4) For claim-level (retrospective) denials, the MCO internal appeal is the primary remedy — state fair hearings are typically for service-level (prospective) denials, (5) The appeal process for Medicaid MCOs must comply with 42 CFR Part 438.

**Q: What is the UHC peer-to-peer review process and how do I schedule one?**

**Q: What is the Workers' Compensation independent medical review (IMR) process in California?**
**A:** California's IMR (Independent Medical Review) process is used to resolve disputes about medical treatment after a UR denial. The process: (1) After a UR denial, the treating physician can request reconsideration from the UR agent within 30 days. (2) If the reconsideration is denied (or the physician did not request reconsideration), the physician or the patient can request IMR from the Division of Workers' Compensation (DWC) within 30 days of the UR decision. **Q: How do I appeal a denial from a non-standard payer (like VA, FEHB, or IHS)?**

**Q: What is the importance of the ERISA "full and fair review" requirement?**
**A:** ERISA Section 503 requires that all employee benefit plans provide a "full and fair review" of claims and appeals. This means: (1) The plan must provide specific reasons for the denial (not vague statements), (2) The claimant must have the opportunity to submit additional information, (3) The appeal must be decided by someone who was not involved in the initial decision, (4) The plan must follow its own procedures as described in the plan document, (5) The appeal decision must be provided in writing with specific reasons. If a self-funded plan violates the full and fair review requirement, the claimant can: (1) File a federal lawsuit under ERISA Section 502(a), (2) The court can order the plan to reprocess the claim with proper procedures, (3) In extreme cases, the court can award penalties and attorneys' fees. For providers: (1) Document every communication with the plan, (2) Ensure you have received a written denial with specific reasons, (3) If the plan does not follow its own procedures, document the specific violations, (4) Consider legal counsel for federal ERISA claims.

**Q: What is the California DMHC Independent Medical Review and how does it apply to HMO appeals?**

**Q: What is the process for appealing a claim from a non-participating provider (CO-174)?**

**Q: What is the Tricare appeals deadline and how strict is it?**
