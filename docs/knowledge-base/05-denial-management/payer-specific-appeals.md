# Payer-Specific Appeals: Comprehensive Reference for Denials Doctor

## Overview

Each health insurance payer has unique appeal processes, forms, timelines, and requirements. While the general appeal principles are similar across payers, the specific details matter enormously — filing a Medicare appeal correctly does not mean you know how to file a UHC appeal, and a BCBS of Texas appeal is different from a BCBS of California appeal.

This document provides detailed, payer-specific appeal guidance for all major payers, including exact submission procedures, forms, jurisdictional assignments, contact information, and strategies unique to each payer.

---

## 1. Medicare — Five-Level Federal Appeal Process

Medicare's appeal process is the most structured of all payers, with five defined levels, strict jurisdictional assignments, and specific forms for each stage. Understanding which contractor handles each level and jurisdiction is critical.

### Overview of the Five Levels

| Level | Name | Who Conducts | Filing Window | Decision Timeline | Form |
|-------|------|-------------|---------------|-------------------|------|
| 1 | Redetermination | Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) | 120 calendar days from remittance | 60 days (standard); 72 hours (expedited) | CMS-10003 or written letter |
| 2 | Reconsideration | Qualified Independent Contractor (QIC) | 180 calendar days from Level 1 decision | 30 days (standard); 72 hours (expedited) | CMS-20033 |
| 3 | ALJ Hearing | OMHA (Office of Medicare Hearings and Appeals) | 60 calendar days from Level 2 decision | 90 days (target) | OMHA-100 |
| 4 | Medicare Appeals Council | Departmental Appeals Board (DAB) | 60 calendar days from ALJ decision | 90-180 days | Written request |
| 5 | Judicial Review | Federal District Court | 60 calendar days from Council decision | Varies | Federal civil complaint |

### Level 1 — Redetermination Filing Methods

**Online (Recommended):**
- Most MACs offer an online portal for appeal submission
- Portals: Noridian Medicare Portal, Novitas Online Appeals, Palmetto Provider Portal, NGS Connex, WPS Portal, CGS Portal, First Coast Portal
- Faster processing than paper; immediate confirmation of receipt

**Fax:**
- Use the MAC-specific fax number (listed on the remittance advice)
- Get a fax confirmation page; keep the confirmation with the appeal records

**Mail:**
- Send to the MAC's appeals address (listed on the RA)
- Use certified mail with return receipt; allow extra time for delivery

**Phone (for expedited appeals only):**
- Call the MAC's provider contact center
- Must be for urgent/expedited appeals only; standard appeals cannot be filed by phone

### CMS-10003 Form — Detailed Requirements

The CMS-10003 is the standard form for filing a Medicare Level 1 appeal. Required sections:

- **Section I — Beneficiary Information**: Full name, Medicare Beneficiary Identifier (MBI), date of birth
- **Section II — Provider/Supplier Information**: Name, NPI, TIN, contact information (phone, email, address)
- **Section III — Claim Information**: Dates of service, claim number (from the remittance advice), specific items/services being appealed, amount in controversy
- **Section IV — Explanation of Why Denial is Incorrect**: Detailed clinical or coding justification referencing specific medical records and guidelines
- **Section V — Signature**: Provider, beneficiary, or authorized representative (original hand-written signature for paper filings; electronic signature accepted on MAC portals)

**Evidence required with CMS-10003:**
- Medical records that directly address the denial reason
- Progress notes, operative reports, discharge summaries
- Clinical guidelines (LCD/NCD, MCG, InterQual)
- Physician letter of medical necessity (recommended for complex cases)
- Supporting literature if applicable

**Note on form alternatives:**
An alternative to the CMS-10003 is a written letter containing the same information. The form is recommended but not strictly required at Level 1.

### CMS-20033 Form — Detailed Requirements

The CMS-20033 is the standard form for filing a Medicare Level 2 appeal (Reconsideration by the QIC). Required elements:

- Copy of the Level 1 redetermination decision
- All evidence that was submitted at Level 1
- Any NEW evidence not previously submitted (strongly recommended)
- Specific explanation of why the Level 1 decision was incorrect
- Provider must demonstrate it is a "party to the appeal"
- Signature of provider or authorized representative

**Evidence strategy for Level 2:**
- QICs conduct a de novo review (fresh review, not deferring to the MAC)
- New evidence is accepted and should be used to address deficiencies identified in the Level 1 denial
- If the Level 1 decision stated specific documentation gaps, fill those gaps at Level 2
- Organize evidence clearly with a table of contents referencing both old and new evidence

### Level 3 — ALJ Hearing (OMHA)

**Who conducts it:**
The Office of Medicare Hearings and Appeals (OMHA), an independent agency within the Department of Health and Human Services, not part of CMS.

**Filing the OMHA-100 form:**
The OMHA-100 (Request for Medicare Hearing by an Administrative Law Judge) must be filed within 60 days of the Level 2 QIC decision.

**Amount in controversy requirement:**
- 2024: $180 minimum (indexed annually)
- 2025: $190 minimum
- Multiple claims with common issues can be aggregated to meet the threshold
- If the amount does not meet the threshold, Level 3 is not available

**Hearing types:**
1. **In-person hearing**: Provider, beneficiary, and representatives appear before the ALJ. Most favorable option for complex cases. Requires travel to the OMHA hearing location.
2. **Video hearing**: Remote appearance via video conference. Increasingly common post-pandemic.
3. **Telephone hearing**: Phone conference. Less effective for complex clinical cases.
4. **Paper review**: No hearing — the ALJ decides based on the written record. Least favorable option.

**Success rate:** 60-70% (highest of all Medicare appeal levels)

### Level 4 — Medicare Appeals Council

**Who conducts it:**
The Medicare Appeals Council, part of the Departmental Appeals Board (DAB) within HHS.

**Filing requirements:**
- Written request within 60 days of the ALJ decision
- No minimum amount in controversy
- Scope of review: ALJ decision for errors of fact or law

**Grounds for appeal:**
1. The ALJ decision contained an error of law
2. The ALJ decision contained a clearly erroneous finding of fact
3. The ALJ abused their discretion
4. The ALJ's decision was not supported by substantial evidence
5. New and material evidence is available that was not available at the time of the ALJ hearing

**Success rate:** Less than 5% — very few appeals are granted review.

### Level 5 — Federal District Court

**Who conducts it:**
U.S. District Court for the district where the provider or beneficiary is located.

**Requirements:**
- Filing within 60 days of the Medicare Appeals Council decision
- Minimum amount in controversy: $1,830 (2024, indexed annually)
- Federal civil complaint must be filed
- Filing fee ($350-$402)
- Attorney representation strongly recommended

### Provider Enrollment Appeals (Separate Process from Claims Appeals)

Provider enrollment appeals (challenges to Medicare enrollment denial, revocation, or suspension) do NOT follow the standard 5-level claims appeal process:

| Issue | Appeal Path |
|-------|-------------|
| Enrollment application denial | Reconsideration by MAC, then ALJ at DAB Civil Remedies Division |
| Billing privileges revocation | Reconsideration by MAC, then ALJ at DAB Civil Remedies Division |
| Revalidation denial | Reconsideration by MAC, then ALJ at DAB |
| Deactivation (non-responding) | Request reinstatement with MAC (no formal appeal) |
| Screening-level changes | Reconsideration by MAC |

**Key distinction:** Claims appeals challenge payment decisions; enrollment appeals challenge participation/privilege decisions. They go to different ALJs (OMHA vs. DAB Civil Remedies Division). Do NOT use the claims appeal process for enrollment issues. Enrollment appeals do not have an amount-in-controversy requirement.

### Medicare Part C (Medicare Advantage) Appeals

Medicare Advantage plans (e.g., Aetna Medicare, UHC Medicare Advantage, Anthem Medicare) follow a process similar to Original Medicare but with some differences:

| Level | Name | Filing Window | Decision Timeline |
|-------|------|---------------|-------------------|
| Level 1 | Plan Redetermination | 60 days from denial notice | 30 days (standard); 72 hours (expedited) |
| Level 2 | IRE Reconsideration | 60 days from Level 1 decision | 30 days |
| Level 3 | ALJ Hearing (OMHA) | 60 days from Level 2 decision | 90 days (target) |
| Level 4 | Medicare Appeals Council | 60 days from ALJ decision | 90-180 days |
| Level 5 | Federal District Court | 60 days from Council decision | Varies |

**Key differences from Original Medicare:**
- Level 1 is 60 days to file (not 120)
- Level 1 decision is 30 days (not 60)
- Level 2 uses an IRE (Independent Review Entity — Maximus or C2C), not a QIC
- Plans have their own specific forms (not CMS-10003)
- Plans use their own medical policies (not LCDs/NCDs exclusively)
- For Part D drug denials, the process is similar but uses Medicare Part D coverage rules

### Medicare Appeal Strategy Tips

1. **Level 1 is the easiest reversal point**: The MAC will assign a different reviewer than the one who processed the claim. Success rate is 50-60%.
2. **Submit new evidence at Level 2**: The QIC wants to see that you addressed the Level 1 deficiencies. If you submit the same evidence that was rejected at Level 1, the QIC will likely uphold the denial.
3. **Request an in-person or video hearing at Level 3**: Paper reviews at Level 3 have the lowest success rate. In-person or video hearings allow the treating physician to present the case directly to the ALJ.
4. **The $180 minimum for Level 3 is per appeal**: You can aggregate multiple claims with the same issue to meet the minimum controversy amount.
5. **ALJ hearings have the highest success rate**: 60-70% of provider appeals succeed at the ALJ level. Do not give up after Level 2.
6. **Exhaust all levels before going to court**: You cannot skip levels. Each level must be exhausted before proceeding to the next.
7. **Always cite the specific LCD or NCD**: Medicare appeals are won on documentation — if the medical record supports medical necessity, the MAC will overturn the denial.
8. **For RAC denials**: The appeal process is the same, but the deadline runs from the RAC demand letter date, not the original remittance date.

## 2. UnitedHealthcare (UHC) Appeals

UHC has distinct appeal processes for commercial plans vs. Medicare Advantage plans. Providers must know which line of business applies.

### UHC Commercial Appeal Structure

| Level | Name | Who Conducts | Filing Window | Decision Timeline |
|-------|------|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Standard Review / First Level Appeal | UHC internal review (nurse or non-clinical reviewer) | 180 days from remittance | 30-45 days (standard); 72 hours (expedited) |
| 2 | Clinical Peer Review / Second Level Appeal | UHC physician reviewer in same or similar specialty | 60 days from Level 1 denial | 30-45 days (standard); 72 hours (expedited) |
| 3 | Independent External Review (IRO) | Independent Review Organization (e.g., MAXIMUS, MCMC) | 60 days from Level 2 denial | 30-60 days (binding on UHC) |

### UHC Submission Methods

**Online (Recommended):**
- UHC Provider Portal (www.uhcprovider.com)
- Navigate to Claims > Appeals
- Upload supporting documentation directly
- Immediate confirmation and tracking number
- Track appeal status in real-time

**Fax:**
- Use the UHC appeals fax number for your state/line of business (varies)
- Fax numbers available on the UHC provider portal
- Get fax confirmation

**Mail:**
- UHC appeals address varies by state
- Obtain the correct address from the UHC provider portal
- Use certified mail

**Phone (for expedited only):**
- UHC Provider Services: 877-842-3210 (varies by state)
- For urgent appeals: request expedited review
- Standard appeals: online or fax is preferred

### UHC Appeal Forms

UHC does NOT require a specific form for all appeals. A written letter on provider letterhead is acceptable. However, UHC provides optional forms:

1. **Standard Appeal Form**: Available on the UHC provider portal. Includes patient info, claim number, dates of service, and appeal reason.
2. **Clinical Appeal Form**: For medical necessity denials. Includes space for clinical justification and documentation.
3. **Claim Payment Dispute Form**: For contractual disputes (fee schedule, contract rate issues).

### UHC Gold Card Program

- Providers with Gold Card status may be exempt from certain prior authorization requirements
- Gold Card status is earned by maintaining a 90%+ authorization approval rate for 24 months
- Gold Card providers can appeal if their status is revoked or not renewed
- Gold Card exemption applies to designated services only (not all services)
- Not all states or product lines participate in the Gold Card program
- If a claim for a Gold Card provider is denied for no prior auth, appeal citing Gold Card status

### UHC Clinical Payment and Coding Policies (CPCPs)

UHC maintains detailed Clinical Payment and Coding Policies that govern how claims are adjudicated. These policies:
- Define which CPT/HCPCS codes are payable together and which are bundled
- Specify modifier usage requirements
- Define medical necessity criteria for specific services
- Are updated quarterly
- Can be found on the UHC provider portal under "Policies and Guidelines"
- Critical to cite in coding-related appeals

### UHC Medicare Advantage Appeal Process

UHC Medicare Advantage plans (UHC AARP, UHC MedicareComplete, etc.) follow Medicare Part C appeal rules as described in the Medicare section above.

**Key differences from UHC Commercial:**
- Shorter filing windows (60 days vs. 180 days)
- Medicare-specific forms may be required
- CMS regulations supersede UHC policies
- Expedited appeals use Medicare criteria (72-hour decision for ongoing treatment)
- Appeals follow Medicare coverage rules, not UHC commercial policies

### UHC-Specific Appeal Features

**UHC Clinical Peer Review (Level 2):**
- Available for medical necessity denials
- Involves a UHC medical director or physician reviewer in the same specialty
- The treating physician should participate in the phone call
- More effective than a written appeal for complex clinical cases
- Schedule by calling UHC Provider Services

**UHC Expedited Appeals:**
- Available for ongoing treatment decisions
- Must demonstrate that the standard timeline would cause harm
- Decision within 72 hours

**UHC State-Specific Rules:**
- California: UHC must comply with the California Department of Managed Health Care (DMHC) appeal standards
- New York: UHC must comply with NY Department of Financial Services (DFS) prompt pay and appeal laws
- Texas: UHC must comply with Texas Department of Insurance requirements

### UHC Appeal Strategy Tips

1. **Use the portal**: UHC's portal provides the fastest processing and real-time status tracking.
2. **Include the authorization number**: If prior auth was obtained, always include it in the appeal.
3. **For medical necessity denials**: Request a clinical peer review before filing a Level 2 — this informal conversation can resolve the issue faster.
4. **UHC may require "differential diagnoses"**: For some medical necessity denials, UHC asks what other diagnoses were considered and ruled out.
5. **For UHC Community Plan (Medicaid)**: Each state has separate rules and contacts — check the state-specific UHC provider manual.
6. **UHC Optum denials**: Some UHC denials originate from Optum (their clinical review subsidiary). The appeal goes to UHC, not Optum.
7. **Check the plan type**: HMO, PPO, POS, and Medicare Advantage plans each have different UHC appeal contacts.
8. **Include provider NPI and TIN on every page** of mailed appeals.
9. **UHC requires original signatures** for mailed appeals — electronic signatures not accepted.

### Aetna Appeal Forms

Aetna provides several optional appeal forms:

1. **Aetna Claim Appeal Form**: General appeal form for claim payment issues (coding, billing, timely filing)
2. **Aetna Clinical Appeal Form**: For medical necessity denials (recommended for clinical appeals)
3. **Aetna Provider Dispute Form**: For contractual or fee schedule disputes

**Important:** Aetna does NOT require a specific appeal form. A written letter on provider letterhead with all required information is acceptable.

### Aetna Case Management

Aetna assigns case managers to complex or high-cost cases through its Aetna Case Management program. Case managers:
- Coordinate care and facilitate communication between providers and Aetna
- Can proactively identify potential denial issues
- May authorize services that would otherwise require an appeal
- Are available for ongoing treatment situations
- Can be contacted directly by the provider to discuss clinical issues before filing an appeal

**When to engage Aetna Case Management:**
- Complex medical conditions requiring multiple services
- Ongoing treatment plans with anticipated utilization
- Cases where prior authorization boundaries may be exceeded
- Discharge planning for high-cost inpatient stays
- Coordination of care across multiple specialists

### Aetna Clinical Policy Bulletins (CPBs)

Aetna's CPBs are the primary reference for medical necessity determinations. Key facts:
- Developed by the Aetna Institute for Medical Excellence
- Available at www.aetna.com/health-care-professionals/clinical-policy-bulletins
- Over 500 CPBs covering medical, surgical, behavioral health, and pharmacy services
- Updated regularly (check the version/review date)
- CPBs that are cited in a denial MUST be addressed in the appeal
- Some CPBs are based on CMS coverage determinations; others are Aetna-specific

**How to use CPBs in appeals:**
1. Find the specific CPB cited in the denial (or the CPB most relevant to the service)
2. Read the "Medical Necessity Criteria" section carefully
3. Map the patient's clinical findings to each criterion
4. Cite the CPB number, title, and version date in the appeal
5. If the CPB is outdated (more than 2 years old with no update), cite more recent peer-reviewed literature

### Aetna Participating Provider Contracts

Aetna participating provider contracts establish the timely filing and appeal deadlines. Key contractual provisions:
- Standard timely filing: 180 days from date of service (unless state law requires more)
- Appeal filing: 180 days from the remittance date
- Corrected claims: Within 180 days of original claim processing
- The contract specifies which Aetna policies apply to claims adjudication
- Out-of-network providers may have different appeal rights
- ERISA plans administered by Aetna may follow plan-specific appeal rules

### Aetna Appeal Strategy Tips

1. **Aetna CPBs are not always updated**: Check the review date on the CPB. If the CPB is more than 2 years old, cite more recent evidence.
2. **Aetna Case Management**: For complex cases (high-cost, chronic conditions), request a case manager who can coordinate across the appeal process.
3. **Aetna Institute for Medical Excellence**: This internal group develops CPBs. In rare cases, appeal to this institute for policy-level disputes.
4. **Expedited appeals**: Aetna offers expedited review for ongoing care. Decision in 72 hours.
5. **Aetna Better Health (Medicaid)**: Each state has separate appeal rules. Contact the specific Aetna Better Health plan.
6. **Retrospective vs. prospective**: Aetna treats retrospective (claim-level) appeals differently from prospective (pre-service) appeals. Claim-level appeals have a 180-day window.
7. **Medical record submission**: Aetna strongly prefers electronic medical record submission through the portal. Avoid faxing medical records if possible.
8. **For pre-service denials**, request a clinical peer review (physician-to-physician) before filing a formal appeal.

Cigna's medical necessity decisions are based on Cigna Medical Coverage Policies (MCPs):
- Available at www.cigna.com/health-care-providers/coverage-policies
- Cover medical necessity, experimental/investigational, and coverage criteria
- Updated regularly with version dates
- Cigna also incorporates CMS coverage determinations for Medicare-like plans

**How to cite CMCPs in appeals:**
1. Identify the policy that applies to the service (e.g., "Cigna Medical Coverage Policy #XXXX — [Title]")
2. Note the effective date and version
3. Cite the specific medical necessity criteria
4. Map patient findings to each criterion
5. If the policy is outdated, note the availability of newer clinical evidence

### Cigna Appeal Types

Cigna distinguishes between two appeal types:

| Appeal Type | Description | Best Approach |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Administrative Appeal** | Coding errors, billing errors, contract interpretation, timely filing disputes | Submit through provider portal with supporting documentation (coding references, contract language) |
| **Clinical Appeal** | Medical necessity, experimental/investigational, level of care | Submit with full clinical records, physician letter, clinical guidelines, peer-reviewed literature |

**Important:** Submitting an administrative appeal to the clinical department (or vice versa) will cause processing delays. Use the correct submission method for the correct appeal type.

### Cigna for Health Care Professionals Portal

The Cigna provider portal is the primary submission channel:
- Access: www.cigna.com/health-care-providers
- Features: Claim status, appeal submission, eligibility verification, authorizations
- Portal submission provides immediate confirmation
- Track appeal status in real time

### Cigna Appeal Strategy Tips

1. **Cigna distinguishes appeal types**: Administrative appeals (coding, billing) are handled by claims staff. Clinical appeals (medical necessity) are handled by clinical reviewers. Submit to the correct department.
2. **Administrative appeals**: Can be submitted through the portal with corrections attached (corrected claim, correct modifier, etc.).
3. **Clinical appeals**: Require: physician letter of medical necessity, complete medical records, applicable MCP citation, and supporting literature.
4. **Cigna + Oscar**: Cigna's joint venture with Oscar Health has its own appeal process — do not use standard Cigna processes.
5. **Cigna LocalPlus**: Network-specific appeals may have different contacts.

## 9. Veterans Affairs (VA) Appeals

VA appeals underwent significant reform under the Appeals Modernization Act (AMA) of 2019.

### Appeals Modernization Act (AMA) — Current Process

The AMA replaced the legacy process with three distinct review lanes:

**Lane 1: Higher-Level Review (HLR)**
- A senior VA reviewer reviews the existing record
- No new evidence is accepted
- Filing window: 1 year from the date of the decision being appealed
- Decision timeline: 125 days
- The reviewer can grant the appeal or return it for correction of a clear and unmistakable error
- No hearing is offered

**Lane 2: Supplemental Claim**
- New and relevant evidence is submitted
- Filing window: 1 year from the date of the decision being appealed
- Decision timeline: 125 days
- Evidence must be "new" (not previously considered) and "relevant" (tends to prove or disprove a fact)
- Best option when additional medical records or evidence is available

**Lane 3: Board Appeal (to the Board of Veterans' Appeals)**
- Direct appeal to the Board of Veterans' Appeals (BVA)
- Filing window: 1 year from the date of the decision being appealed
- Three sub-types:
  a. **Direct Review**: No new evidence, no hearing — decision based on existing record. Fastest option.
  b. **Evidence Submission**: 90 days to submit new evidence, no hearing.
  c. **Hearing**: In-person or video hearing with the option to submit evidence.

**VA Form 10182 — Appeal to Board of Veterans' Appeals:**
The VA Form 10182 is the standard form for filing a Board Appeal under the AMA. Required elements:
- Veteran's name and VA file number
- Date of the decision being appealed
- The specific issue(s) being appealed
- The chosen docket type (Direct Review, Evidence Submission, or Hearing)
- Signature of the veteran or authorized representative

The form can be filed online through the VA portal, by mail, or in person at a VA regional office. Veterans are strongly encouraged to use a Veterans Service Organization (VSO) for assistance.

### Legacy Process (Pre-AMA)

The legacy process is still in effect for appeals filed before the AMA implementation (February 2019):
- Notice of Disagreement (NOD) — filed within 1 year of the decision
- Statement of the Case (SOC) — VA explains the rationale for the denial
- Form 9 (Substantive Appeal) — filed within 60 days of the SOC
- Board of Veterans' Appeals (BVA) hearing
- Court of Appeals for Veterans Claims (CAVC)
- Federal Circuit Court of Appeals
- Supreme Court

### Provider Appeals vs. Veteran Appeals

VA provider appeals (for Community Care and fee-basis services) follow a different process:
- Fee-basis claims: Standard VA claims appeal process
- Community Care claims: Follow the Community Care network provider's appeal process
- Provider appeals against the VA for fee disputes use the Contract Disputes Act process
- Veteran medical necessity appeals use the AMA process

### VA Appeal Tips

- For the AMA process, carefully choose the review lane based on the type of evidence available
- Higher-Level Review is fastest but does not accept new evidence — use only if the existing record is strong
- Supplemental Claim is best when new medical evidence is available
- Board Appeal is best when a hearing is needed or the case is complex
- The 1-year filing window is generous but strictly enforced
- VA appeals do not require a filing fee
- Veterans can be represented by a Veterans Service Organization (VSO) at no cost

## 10. Medicaid Appeals

Medicaid appeals vary significantly by state, with distinct processes for fee-for-service (FFS) and managed care (MCO) appeals.

### State Agency Appeals (Fee-for-Service)

Each state Medicaid agency has its own appeal process:
- Providers appeal claim denials to the state Medicaid agency
- The appeals process is governed by state administrative law
- Deadlines typically range from 30-180 days from the denial notice
- State fair hearings are required by federal law (due process)
- No filing fee for the provider or beneficiary

### State Fair Hearings

Federal law requires every state to provide a "fair hearing" for:
- Claim denials
- Service reductions or terminations
- Eligibility determinations
- Beneficiary grievances

**Fair hearing characteristics:**
- Conducted by an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) or hearing officer
- Can be in-person, by phone, or by video
- Decision is issued within 90 days (standard) or 3 days (expedited)
- The decision is binding on the state agency
- Beneficiaries can request continued benefits during the appeal (if requested within 10 days of the adverse action)
- Provider appeals may follow a different path than beneficiary appeals

### MCO Internal Appeals vs. State Fair Hearing

Medicaid Managed Care Organizations (MCOs) have a two-step appeal process:

**Step 1 — MCO Internal Appeal:**
- File with the MCO (the health plan)
- Deadline: 60 days from the denial (or sooner, depending on the state)
- Decision: 30 days (standard); 72 hours (expedited)
- The MCO reviews the denial using its own medical policies

**Step 2 — State Fair Hearing:**
- If the MCO upholds the denial, file a state fair hearing with the state Medicaid agency
- Deadline: 120 days from the MCO's decision (varies by state)
- Decision: 90 days from the hearing request
- The state fair hearing decision overrides the MCO's decision
- Beneficiaries can also bypass the MCO appeal and go directly to a state fair hearing

### Federal Waiver Programs

States with federal waivers (Section 1115, Section 1915(b), Section 1915(c)) may have modified appeal processes:

| Waiver Type | Appeal Implications |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Section 1115 Demonstration Waivers | Alternative service delivery; appeals still require state fair hearings |
| Section 1915(b) Managed Care Waivers | MCO internal appeals required before state fair hearing |
| Section 1915(c) HCBS Waivers | Beneficiaries have fair hearing rights for services denied or reduced |
| Section 1915(i) State Plan HCBS | Standard state fair hearing process applies |

**Key point:** Federal waiver programs cannot eliminate the beneficiary's right to a state fair hearing, but they can modify the process (e.g., requiring MCO internal appeal first).

### MCO Appeal Variations by State (Examples)

**California (Medi-Cal Managed Care):**
- MCO appeal: 90 days from denial
- State fair hearing: 90 days from MCO decision
- Medi-Cal managed care plans include: Anthem Blue Cross, Health Net, Blue Shield of California

**New York (NY Medicaid Managed Care):**
- MCO appeal: 60 days from denial
- State fair hearing: 120 days from MCO notice
- Managed care plans include: Fidelis Care, Healthfirst, EmblemHealth, MetroPlus

**Texas (STAR and CHIP):**
- MCO appeal: 30 days from denial
- State fair hearing: 120 days from MCO decision
- STAR plans include: Superior, UnitedHealthcare, BCBS, Cook Children's

**Florida (Medicaid Managed Care):**
- MCO appeal: 60 days from denial
- State fair hearing: 120 days from MCO decision

### Medicaid Fair Hearing Tips

1. **Beneficiary rights**: The beneficiary (patient) must be informed of their fair hearing rights in writing.
2. **Aid paid pending**: Beneficiaries can request continued benefits during the appeal (within 10 days of the notice). For providers, this means continued payment for ongoing treatment.
3. **Retroactive denials**: Claim-level denials go through the MCO internal appeal process, not the state fair hearing process.
4. **State-specific rules**: Each state has unique fair hearing procedures. Check with the state Medicaid agency.
5. **Expedited hearings**: Available if delaying the hearing would harm the patient's health. Decision within 3 days.

## 13. Agent Q&A Pairs for Training

**Q: What are the five levels of the Medicare appeals process and who conducts each level?**
**A:** The five levels of the Medicare appeals process are: Level 1 — Redetermination, conducted by the Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) that processed the claim. Level 2 — Reconsideration, conducted by a Qualified Independent Contractor (QIC) such as C2C Solutions (Jurisdictions 1, 3, 5, 6 for Part A) or Maximus Federal Services (Jurisdictions 2, 4, 7, 8 for Part B). Level 3 — ALJ Hearing, conducted by the Office of Medicare Hearings and Appeals (OMHA). Level 4 — Medicare Appeals Council, conducted by the Departmental Appeals Board (DAB). Level 5 — Judicial Review, conducted by the U.S. Federal District Court. Provider enrollment appeals do NOT follow this 5-level process — they go through a separate process involving the MAC and the DAB Civil Remedies Division.

**Q: Which QIC handles Medicare Part A appeals and which handles Part B appeals?**
**A:** For Medicare Level 2 reconsiderations: C2C Solutions handles Part A appeals (inpatient hospital, SNF, home health, hospice) for Jurisdictions 1, 3, 5, and 6. Maximus Federal Services handles Part B appeals (physician services, outpatient, DME) for Jurisdictions 2, 4, 7, and 8. To determine which QIC applies to a specific claim: first identify whether the denial involves a Part A service or a Part B service. The remittance advice will indicate this. Then look at the Level 1 denial notice from the MAC — it typically includes the QIC name and address for filing a Level 2 appeal. The QICs conduct a de novo review (fresh review) and accept new evidence not submitted at Level 1.

**Q: What is the difference between a Medicare provider enrollment appeal and a claims appeal?**
**A:** Provider enrollment appeals challenge decisions about a provider's ability to participate in Medicare (e.g., enrollment application denial, billing privileges revocation, revalidation denial). Claims appeals challenge payment decisions on specific claims. The appeal paths differ: Enrollment appeals go through the MAC for reconsideration, then to the DAB Civil Remedies Division for an ALJ hearing (not OMHA). Claims appeals follow the standard 5-level process (MAC -> QIC -> OMHA ALJ -> Medicare Appeals Council -> Federal Court). The forms also differ: enrollment appeals use CMS-specific forms for each issue type; claims appeals use CMS-10003 (Level 1), CMS-20033 (Level 2), and OMHA-100 (Level 3). Enrollment appeals do not have an amount-in-controversy requirement. Do NOT use the claims appeal process for enrollment issues.

**Q: What is the CMS-10003 form and when is it used?**
**A:** The CMS-10003 (also called "Appeal of Medicare Claim Decision" or "Redetermination Request Form") is the standard form for filing a Medicare Level 1 appeal (Redetermination). It is used to request a review of a denied Part A or Part B claim by the MAC. The form requires: beneficiary information (name, MBI, DOB), provider information (name, NPI, TIN), claim information (dates of service, claim number, specific items/services being appealed), a detailed explanation of why the denial is incorrect, and the provider's signature. Supporting documentation must be attached. The CMS-10003 is available on the CMS website and each MAC's provider portal. An alternative to the CMS-10003 is a written letter containing the same information — the form is recommended but not strictly required at Level 1.

**Q: What is the CMS-20033 form and when is it used?**
**A:** The CMS-20033 (also called "Medicare Reconsideration Request Form") is the standard form for filing a Medicare Level 2 appeal (Reconsideration by the QIC). It is used when the MAC has issued a Level 1 Redetermination decision that denied the claim and the provider disagrees. The form requires: a copy of the Level 1 decision, all evidence submitted at Level 1, any new evidence, a specific explanation of why the Level 1 decision was incorrect, and the provider's signature. Note: CMS-20027 is an older form number that was used in some jurisdictions; CMS-20033 is the current standard form. Always check the specific QIC's website for the correct current form. The CMS-20033 must be submitted to the QIC (not the MAC) within 180 calendar days of the Level 1 decision.

**Q: What evidence must I include at each level of a Medicare appeal?**
**A:** Evidence requirements differ by level. Level 1 (Redetermination by MAC): Submit all available medical records (progress notes, operative reports, discharge summaries), clinical guidelines (LCD/NCD, MCG, InterQual), and any other supporting documentation that directly addresses the denial reason. Level 2 (QIC Reconsideration): Submit ALL evidence from Level 1 PLUS any NEW evidence that addresses deficiencies identified in the Level 1 decision. The QIC conducts a de novo review and will consider new evidence. Level 3 (ALJ Hearing at OMHA): The complete record from Levels 1 and 2, plus a pre-hearing brief organizing the case, exhibits with a table of contents, and expert testimony. New evidence is accepted at Level 3. Level 4 (Medicare Appeals Council): The complete record from Levels 1-3, plus legal arguments about errors of law or fact in the ALJ decision. Level 5 (Federal District Court): Complete administrative record plus federal civil pleadings.

**Q: How does the UHC Gold Card program affect the appeal process?**
**A:** The UHC Gold Card program rewards providers with a 90%+ prior authorization approval rate over 24 months by exempting them from certain prior authorization requirements. Impact on appeals: (1) Gold Card providers receive fewer prior authorization denials because fewer services require authorization, reducing the need for authorization-related appeals. (2) If a Gold Card provider is denied authorization for a service that is not on the Gold Card exemption list, the standard UHC appeal process applies. (3) If the Gold Card status itself is revoked or not renewed, the provider can appeal the revocation through a separate process. (4) Gold Card exemption does not eliminate the need for medical necessity documentation — claims can still be denied for medical necessity and appealed through the standard process. (5) Not all states participate in the Gold Card program, and the covered service list varies.

**Q: What is the difference between appealing a UHC Commercial denial vs. a UHC Medicare Advantage denial?**
**A:** UHC Commercial and UHC Medicare Advantage appeals differ in several critical ways: (1) Filing window: Commercial has 180 days from denial; Medicare Advantage has 60 days from denial. (2) Appeal structure: Commercial has 3 levels (internal review, clinical peer review, IRO); Medicare Advantage follows the Medicare Part C 5-level process (plan redetermination, IRE reconsideration, ALJ hearing, Appeals Council, Federal Court). (3) Governing rules: Commercial appeals are governed by UHC policies and the plan document; Medicare Advantage appeals are governed by CMS regulations and Medicare coverage rules. (4) Forms: Commercial uses UHC-specific forms; Medicare Advantage uses Medicare forms. (5) External review: Commercial Level 3 is an IRO under UHC's process; Medicare Advantage Level 2 is the IRE under CMS contract. Always verify which line of business applies before filing an appeal.

**Q: What are Aetna Clinical Policy Bulletins (CPBs) and how do I use them in an appeal?**
**A:** Aetna Clinical Policy Bulletins (CPBs) are the medical necessity guidelines developed by the Aetna Institute for Medical Excellence. They are the primary reference for Aetna's medical necessity determinations. To use CPBs in an appeal: (1) Find the specific CPB that applies to the service — look for the CPB number cited in the denial or search on www.aetna.com/cpb. (2) Read the "Medical Necessity Criteria" section carefully. (3) List each criterion the CPB requires for the service to be medically necessary. (4) Map the patient's specific clinical findings to each criterion, citing the exact page and line of the medical record. (5) In your appeal letter, cite the CPB by number, title, and version date. (6) If the CPB is outdated (more than 2 years old with no update), argue that newer clinical evidence supports the service. CPBs cover procedures, drugs, devices, and services.

**Q: What is the filing deadline for an Aetna appeal, and does Aetna require a specific form?**
**A:** Aetna's standard appeal filing deadline is 180 days from the date of the remittance/denial notice. For expedited appeals (clinical urgency), file as soon as possible — decision within 72 hours. Aetna does NOT require a specific appeal form for Level 1 appeals — a written letter on provider letterhead is acceptable. However, Aetna provides optional appeal forms on its provider portal. The written appeal must include: provider name, NPI, TIN, and contact information; patient name, DOB, and member ID; claim number and date of service; specific reason for the appeal; and supporting documentation. For Level 2 (clinical peer review), the same written format is acceptable. Aetna also offers a Provider Dispute Form for contractual issues that are not medical necessity appeals.

**Q: What is the Cigna appeal structure and how many levels does it have?**
**A:** Cigna has a 3-level appeal structure. Level 1 — Re-Review: Conducted by Cigna's internal review team (typically a nurse or claims reviewer). Filing window is 180 days from the denial. Decision within 30 days (standard) or 72 hours (expedited). Level 2 — Clinical Review: Conducted by a Cigna physician reviewer who is board-certified in the same or similar specialty. Filing window is 60 days from the Level 1 denial. Decision within 30 days. Level 3 — External Independent Review: Conducted by an IRO. Filing window is 60 days from the Level 2 denial. Decision within 30-60 days. Cigna distinguishes between "administrative appeals" (coding, billing, contractual) and "clinical appeals" (medical necessity). Administrative appeals can be submitted through the Cigna for Health Care Professionals portal. Clinical appeals require full clinical documentation and a physician letter.

**Q: How do I know which BCBS plan handles a claim and what appeal process applies?**
**A:** There are 34 independent BCBS companies, each with its own appeal process. To determine the correct plan: (1) Look at the remittance advice — the payer name and address indicates which BCBS plan processed the claim. (2) Check the member ID card — the BCBS logo and plan name identifies the plan. Each BCBS plan has a 3-digit prefix on the member ID that identifies the plan. (3) For BCBS Federal Employee Program (FEP), the member ID has an "R" prefix — FEP has a centralized national appeal process. (4) The appeal process varies significantly by plan: filing windows (180-365 days), required forms (some require plan-specific forms, others accept letters), decision timelines, and medical policies. Never assume the same BCBS process works for different states. Always call the specific BCBS plan's provider services line or check the plan's provider portal for exact requirements.

**Q: What is the BCBS Federal Employee Program (FEP) appeal process?**
**A:** BCBS FEP covers over 5 million federal employees, retirees, and their families. The FEP appeal process is centralized through the Blue Cross Blue Shield Association. Key features: (1) Filing window: 180 calendar days from the denial/remittance. (2) Level 1: Reconsideration by the local BCBS plan that processed the claim. (3) Level 2: Review by the BCBS Association FEP Operations Center. (4) Level 3: External independent review (if applicable under the plan). (5) Decision timeline: 30 days for standard appeals. (6) FEP uses the Blue Shield Manual for medical necessity determinations, not the local BCBS plan's medical policies. (7) FEP follows Medicare coverage rules for many services. (8) FEP-specific appeal forms are available through the local BCBS plan. (9) Appeal decisions are binding on the local BCBS plan. Filing address: FEP Medical Necessity Appeals, P.O. Box 1340, Harrisburg, PA 17105-1340.

**Q: What is the Humana appeal process and how do I file through the Humana portal?**
**A:** Humana has a 2-level appeal process for most plans: Level 1 — First Level Appeal (Humana internal nurse reviewer, 180-day filing window, 30-day decision), and Level 2 — Second Level Appeal (Humana medical director or physician reviewer, 60 days from Level 1 denial, 30-day decision). There is also a Level 3 external independent review available. To file through the Humana SmartSummary provider portal: (1) Log in at www.humana.com/provider. (2) Navigate to the "Appeals" section. (3) Select the claim being appealed. (4) Complete the online appeal form. (5) Upload supporting documentation (medical records, clinical documentation). (6) Submit and record the confirmation number. (7) Track appeal status in real time through the portal. For Humana Medicare Advantage, follow Medicare Part C rules. For Humana Medicaid (Humana Healthy Horizons), state-specific rules apply.

**Q: What is the Tricare 3-level appeal process?**
**A:** The Tricare appeal process is: Level 1 — Contractor Internal Review: The regional Tricare contractor conducts an internal review. Filing window is 90 calendar days from the denial. Decision within 30-45 days. The contractor varies by region: Humana Military for Tricare East, Health Net for Tricare West. Level 2 — Tricare Program Office Review: The Tricare Program Office in Falls Church, Virginia reviews the decision. Filing window is 60 calendar days from the Level 1 decision. Decision within 45-60 days. Level 3 — Federal Court Review: Providers can appeal to federal court under the Contract Disputes Act. Filing window is 60 calendar days from the Level 2 decision. Tricare does not have a standard external independent review process. For Tricare for Life, the appeal path depends on whether the service is Medicare-covered (follow Medicare 5-level process) or Tricare-only covered (follow Tricare 3-level process).

**Q: How does the Workers' Compensation UR appeal process work in California?**
**A:** In California Workers' Compensation, the UR appeal process: (1) The treating physician submits a Request for Authorization (RFA) for medical treatment. (2) The UR agent reviews and issues a decision within 5 business days (standard) or 72 hours (urgent). (3) If denied, the denial must cite the specific medical treatment guideline (ACOEM, ODG, or California MTUS) and the specific criterion not met. (4) The treating physician has 30 days to request reconsideration or file for Independent Medical Review (IMR). (5) IMR is conducted by an independent physician reviewer. The IMR decision is binding on all parties. (6) If IMR upholds the denial, the physician can appeal to the Workers' Compensation Appeals Board (WCAB). (7) The WCAB can only review procedural issues and MTUS application — it cannot overturn the IMR's medical necessity determination. Key: The treating physician must be involved in the UR appeal; billing staff cannot do it alone.

**Q: What is the difference between the AMA (Appeals Modernization Act) process and the legacy process for VA appeals?**
**A:** Under the AMA (effective February 2019), veterans choose from three review lanes: (1) Higher-Level Review — senior reviewer reviews the existing record with no new evidence; decision in 125 days. (2) Supplemental Claim — new and relevant evidence is submitted; decision in 125 days. (3) Board Appeal — direct appeal to the Board of Veterans' Appeals, with three sub-types (Direct Review, Evidence Submission, or Hearing). Under the legacy process (for appeals filed before AMA): (1) The veteran files a Notice of Disagreement (NOD) within 1 year. (2) VA issues a Statement of the Case (SOC). (3) The veteran files a Form 9 (Substantive Appeal). (4) The Board assigns a docket by receipt date. The AMA process is faster (125-day target vs. multi-year waits). The VA Form 10182 is used for Board Appeals under the AMA. The legacy process still applies to appeals filed before February 2019.

**Q: How do Medicaid MCO appeals differ from Medicaid state fair hearings?**
**A:** Medicaid MCO appeals and state fair hearings differ in several ways: (1) MCO appeals are internal to the health plan; state fair hearings are conducted by the state Medicaid agency. (2) MCO appeals must typically be filed within 60 days of the denial; state fair hearings within 120 days (varies by state). (3) MCO appeals use the MCO's medical policies; state fair hearings use the state's Medicaid coverage policies. (4) Filing an MCO appeal first is required in most states before accessing a state fair hearing. (5) Beneficiaries can request continued benefits during an MCO appeal or state fair hearing (if requested within 10 days of the adverse action). (6) State fair hearings are required by federal law for due process; MCO appeals are required by contract. (7) State fair hearings are overseen by an Administrative Law Judge; MCO appeals are reviewed by the plan's internal reviewers.

**Q: What state fair hearing rights do Medicaid beneficiaries have?**
**A:** Federal law requires all state Medicaid programs to provide a "fair hearing" for beneficiaries who disagree with an adverse action. Key rights: (1) Notice: The state must provide written notice at least 10 days before the effective date. (2) Continued benefits: If the beneficiary requests a hearing within 10 days, benefits continue pending the decision. (3) Representation: The beneficiary can be represented by an attorney or authorized representative. (4) Evidence: The beneficiary can present evidence and cross-examine witnesses. (5) Timely decision: The hearing decision must be issued within 90 days (standard) or 3 days (expedited). (6) No cost: The hearing is free to the beneficiary. (7) Binding decision: The hearing decision is binding on the state agency. (8) Appeal: The beneficiary can appeal the hearing decision to state court. These rights apply to both fee-for-service and managed care Medicaid beneficiaries.

**Q: What is the VA Form 10182 and how is it used in the AMA Board Appeal process?**
**A:** The VA Form 10182 (Appeal to the Board of Veterans' Appeals) is the standard form for requesting a Board Appeal under the AMA process. It must be filed within 1 year of the VA decision being appealed. The form requires: veteran's name and VA file number, the date of the decision being appealed, the specific issue(s) being appealed, and the veteran's choice of docket type: (1) Direct Review — no new evidence, no hearing, fastest option. (2) Evidence Submission — 90 days to submit new evidence, no hearing. (3) Hearing — in-person or video hearing with option to submit new evidence. The chosen docket type determines processing time and the opportunity to submit additional evidence. The form can be filed online through the VA portal, by mail, or in person at a VA regional office.

**Q: What is the difference between a Medicare Level 1 appeal filed by a provider vs. by a beneficiary?**
**A:** The Level 1 appeal process is essentially the same whether filed by a provider or a beneficiary, but there are important differences: (1) Provider appeals: The provider files as a "party" to the appeal when they have accepted assignment and are appealing a claim payment denial. The provider must have a valid Medicare enrollment (NPI/TIN). (2) Beneficiary appeals: The beneficiary files when they are appealing a service that was denied as not medically necessary and they received the service or will receive it. (3) A provider cannot appeal on behalf of a beneficiary who has not assigned their benefits. (4) For assignment appeals, the provider can only appeal claim payment decisions, not coverage determinations. (5) The beneficiary has appeal rights even if the provider chooses not to appeal. (6) A provider who does not accept assignment cannot appeal on the beneficiary's behalf. In practice, most Medicare appeals are filed by providers who have accepted assignment.

**Q: What is the Cigna peer-to-peer review process and when should I use it?**
**A:** Cigna offers peer-to-peer (P2P) reviews for medical necessity denials. The process: (1) Call Cigna Provider Services at 1-800-882-2726 and request a P2P review. (2) Cigna will arrange a call between the treating physician and a Cigna medical director in the same or similar specialty. (3) The call typically lasts 10-20 minutes. (4) The treating physician explains the clinical rationale for the service. (5) The Cigna medical director may overturn the denial based on the discussion. (6) If not, a written denial with appeal instructions is provided. When to use P2P: pre-service denials, complex clinical cases where documentation does not fully capture the clinical picture, and before filing a formal appeal to potentially avoid the appeal process.

**Q: How do I determine the correct MAC for a Medicare appeal?**
**A:** To determine the correct MAC for a Medicare appeal: (1) Look at the remittance advice — the MAC name and address are typically printed on the remittance. (2) Use the CMS MAC lookup tool on the CMS website by entering the provider's ZIP code. (3) Call Medicare Provider Services at 1-800-MEDICARE for assistance. (4) Common MAC assignments: Noridian (Western states), Palmetto GBA (Southeastern states), NGS (Northeastern and Midwestern states), WPS (IA, KS, MO, NE, WI), First Coast (FL, PR, VI), CGS (DE, DC, MD, NJ, NY, PA), Novitas (DC, DE, MD, NJ, PA, CO, NM, OK, TX, VA, WV). (5) For DME claims, the four DME MACs cover specific jurisdictions. Always verify the correct MAC before filing — a misplaced appeal will cause delays.

**Q: What is the maximum number of appeal levels available for a UHC commercial claim vs. a UHC Medicare Advantage claim?**
**A:** For UHC commercial claims, there are 3 appeal levels: Level 1 (Standard Review/Internal Review), Level 2 (Clinical Peer Review), and Level 3 (Independent External Review/IRO). For UHC Medicare Advantage claims, there are 5 appeal levels following the Medicare Part C structure: Level 1 (Plan Redetermination), Level 2 (IRE Reconsideration by Maximus or C2C), Level 3 (ALJ Hearing at OMHA), Level 4 (Medicare Appeals Council), and Level 5 (Federal District Court). The Medicare Advantage process allows more opportunities for reversal (including the highly favorable ALJ hearing at Level 3). However, the filing windows are shorter for Medicare Advantage (60 days at Level 1 vs. 180 days for commercial). The key takeaway: if you have a Medicare Advantage denial, do not stop at the UHC internal review — escalate to the IRE and ALJ levels where the success rate is higher.
