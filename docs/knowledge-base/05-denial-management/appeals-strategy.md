# Appeals Strategy: Comprehensive Reference for Denials Doctor

## Overview

The appeals process is the formal mechanism for challenging a denied claim. For Denials Doctor, understanding the full appeals landscape — from Level 1 redetermination through federal court — is essential for maximizing revenue recovery. This document covers appeal levels by payer, appeal letter structure, evidence requirements, strategies by denial type, success rates, and payer-specific guidance.

---

## 1. Appeal Levels by Payer

### Medicare Parts A and B: Five-Level Appeal Process

#### Level 1 — Redetermination

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Who conducts** | Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) |
| **Filing window** | 120 calendar days from date of remittance notice |
| **How to file** | Written request (CMS-10003 form or standard letter) |
| **Decision timeline** | 60 calendar days (standard); 72 hours (expedited/urgent) |
| **Filing method** | Online via MAC portal, fax, mail, or phone for expedited |
| **Success rate** | 50-60% |
| **Cost to file** | No filing fee |

**Required elements for a valid Level 1 appeal:**
1. Beneficiary's name and Medicare number (HICN or MBI)
2. Specific service, item, or date of service being appealed
3. Date of the remittance notice being appealed
4. Specific reasons why the denial is incorrect
5. Signature of the provider, beneficiary, or authorized representative
6. Supporting documentation (medical records, clinical notes, etc.)

**How to file CMS-10003:**
- Download CMS-10003 from the CMS website or the MAC's website
- Complete all sections: beneficiary info, provider info, claim details, appeal reasons
- Attach supporting documentation
- Submit to the MAC that issued the denial
- Retain a copy and proof of submission (fax confirmation, certified mail receipt, online confirmation)

#### Level 2 — Reconsideration by Qualified Independent Contractor (QIC)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Who conducts** | QIC (e.g., C2C Solutions, Maximus, Burns & Associates) |
| **Filing window** | 180 calendar days from Level 1 denial notice |
| **How to file** | Written request (CMS-20033 form or standard letter to QIC) |
| **Decision timeline** | 30 calendar days (standard); 72 hours (expedited) |
| **Filing method** | Mail, fax, or online via QIC portal |
| **Success rate** | 15-25% |

**QIC jurisdictions and contact information:**

| QIC | Region | Jurisdictions |
|-----|--------|---------------|
| C2C Solutions, Inc. | Part A QIC | National — Part A inpatient hospital, SNF, HH, hospice |
| Maximus Federal Services | Part B QIC | National — Part B physician, outpatient, DME |
| Burns & Associates | Part A/B QIC | Administrative review only |

**Level 2 filing requirements:**
- Copy of the Level 1 redetermination decision
- All evidence submitted at Level 1
- Any NEW evidence not previously submitted
- Specific explanation of why the Level 1 decision was incorrect
- CMS-20033 form or equivalent written request
- Provider must demonstrate it is a "party to the appeal"

#### Level 3 — Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) Hearing

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Who conducts** | OMHA (Office of Medicare Hearings and Appeals) |
| **Filing window** | 60 calendar days from Level 2 QIC decision |
| **Minimum amount in controversy** | $180 (2024, indexed annually) |
| **Decision timeline** | 90 calendar days (target; actual may be longer due to backlog) |
| **Filing method** | OMHA online portal or mail (OMHA-100 form) |
| **Success rate** | 60-70% (highest of all levels) |
| **Hearing type** | In-person, video, teleconference, or paper review (provider's choice) |

**ALJ hearing types:**
1. **In-person hearing**: Provider, beneficiary, and representatives appear before the ALJ. Most favorable but requires travel.
2. **Video hearing**: Remote appearance via video conference. Increasingly common post-pandemic.
3. **Telephone hearing**: Phone conference with the ALJ.
4. **Paper review**: No hearing — the ALJ makes a decision based on the written record. Least favorable option.

**Preparation for ALJ hearing:**
- Review the entire case file (claim, medical records, Level 1 and Level 2 decisions)
- Prepare a pre-hearing brief summarizing the clinical justification
- Identify expert witnesses (treating physician, clinical specialists)
- Organize exhibits with a table of contents
- Highlight key clinical evidence that supports medical necessity
- Address the specific criteria that the MAC and QIC found lacking

#### Level 4 — Medicare Appeals Council

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Who conducts** | Departmental Appeals Board (DAB), Medicare Appeals Council |
| **Filing window** | 60 calendar days from ALJ decision |
| **Minimum amount in controversy** | None |
| **Decision timeline** | 90-180 calendar days |
| **Scope of review** | Review of ALJ decision for errors of fact or law |
| **Standard of review** | De novo review (new review of the case, not just ALJ's decision) |

**Grounds for appeal to Medicare Appeals Council:**
1. The ALJ decision contained an error of law
2. The ALJ decision contained a clearly erroneous finding of fact
3. The ALJ abused their discretion
4. The ALJ's decision was not supported by substantial evidence
5. New and material evidence is available that was not available at the time of the ALJ hearing

#### Level 5 — Federal District Court

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Who conducts** | U.S. District Court for the district where the provider/beneficiary is located |
| **Filing window** | 60 calendar days from Medicare Appeals Council decision |
| **Minimum amount in controversy** | $1,830 (2024, indexed annually) |
| **Filing requirements** | Federal civil complaint, filing fee ($350-$402) |
| **Legal representation** | Attorney representation strongly recommended |

### Commercial Payer Appeal Levels

#### Standard Three-Level Process

Most commercial payers use a three-level appeal process:

**Level 1 — Internal Appeal / First Level Appeal**

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Who conducts** | Payer's internal review team (non-clinical staff or nurse reviewer) |
| **Filing window** | 60-180 days from remittance (varies by payer and state) |
| **Decision timeline** | 30-45 calendar days (standard); 72 hours (urgent) |
| **Filing method** | Payer portal, fax, mail, phone |
| **Required** | Written explanation with supporting documentation |

**Level 2 — Clinical Peer Review / Second Level Appeal**

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Who conducts** | Physician reviewer in the same or similar specialty |
| **Filing window** | 60-180 days from Level 1 denial |
| **Decision timeline** | 30-60 calendar days (standard); 72 hours (urgent) |
| **Filing method** | Written appeal with clinical documentation |
| **Review type** | Clinical peer review; reviewer was not involved in Level 1 decision |

**Level 3 — External Independent Review / Third Level Appeal**

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Who conducts** | Independent review organization (IRO) — e.g., MAXIMUS, MCMC, IMS |
| **Filing window** | 60-180 days from Level 2 denial (varies by payer and state) |
| **Decision timeline** | 30-60 calendar days |
| **Cost** | Free to provider/patient; payer covers the IRO cost |
| **Binding** | Yes — the IRO decision is binding on the payer |

**Important note on external review:**
- Under the Affordable Care Act (ACA), most non-grandfathered health plans must offer external review for adverse benefit determinations
- Self-funded (ERISA) plans are NOT required by federal law to provide external review, but many voluntarily do
- State laws require external review for fully-insured plans in most states
- The external reviewer must be independent and without conflict of interest
- The health plan must cover the cost of the external review
- The external review decision is binding on the health plan

### Medicare Advantage (Part C) Appeals

Medicare Advantage plans have their own appeal process:

| Level | Name | Details |
|-------|------|---------|
| Level 1 | Plan Redetermination | 60 days from denial notice; 30-day decision standard, 72-hour expedited |
| Level 2 | IRE Reconsideration | Independent Review Entity (e.g., Maximus, C2C); 60 days from Level 1; 30-day decision |
| Level 3 | ALJ Hearing | Same as Medicare Parts A/B; $180 minimum (2024) |
| Level 4 | Medicare Appeals Council | 60 days from ALJ |
| Level 5 | Federal District Court | $1,830 minimum (2024) |

Note: Medicare Advantage appeals are faster than traditional Medicare at Levels 1 and 2, but follow the same process at Levels 3-5.

### Medicaid Appeals

Medicaid appeals vary significantly by state:

| State | Level 1 | Level 2 | Level 3 |
|-------|---------|---------|---------|
| California (Medi-Cal) | State hearing with Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) — 90 days from denial, no filing fee | Superior Court appeal | N/A |
| New York | Plan-level appeal — 60 days, 30-day decision | State fair hearing — 120 days from plan decision, 90-day decision | Article 78 court proceeding |
| Texas | Plan reconsideration — 30 days, 30-day decision | State fair hearing — 120 days from plan decision, 90-day decision | District Court appeal |
| Florida | Plan appeal — 60 days, 30-day decision | State administrative hearing — DOAH | District Court of Appeals |
| Illinois | Plan-level appeal — 180 days, 30-day decision | State administrative hearing | Circuit Court |

General Medicaid appeal rules:
- All states must provide a state fair hearing (due process requirement)
- Managed care Medicaid appeals follow the plan's internal appeal process first, then state hearing
- Medicaid appeals cannot charge the beneficiary a fee
- Beneficiaries retain the right to continued benefits during the appeal (if they request it within 10 days of the denial)
- Retroactive denials (claim-level) may have different appeal paths than prospective denials (service-level)

### Workers' Compensation Appeals

Workers' Comp appeals are entirely state-specific:

| State | UR Appeal Entity | Appeal Window | Final Appeal |
|-------|-----------------|---------------|--------------|
| California | DWC (Division of Workers' Compensation) — Independent Medical Review | 30 days from UR decision | WCAB (Workers' Compensation Appeals Board) |
| New York | WCB (Workers' Compensation Board) | 30 days from denial | WCB Full Board Review |
| Texas | DWC (Division of Workers' Compensation) | 20 days from denial | DWC Appeals Panel |
| Florida | JCC (Judge of Compensation Claims) | 30 days from denial | First DCA |
| Illinois | IWCC (Illinois Workers' Compensation Commission) | 30 days from denial | IWCC review |

Workers' Comp Medical Fee Schedule denials (payment amount disputes) follow separate processes from UR denials.

## 2. Appeal Letter Structure

### Standard Appeal Letter Format

A well-structured appeal letter is critical for success. Use this template structure:

```
[Provider Letterhead — include NPI, TIN, and contact information]

[DATE]

[Payer Name and Address]
[Appeals Department]
[Street Address]
[City, State, ZIP]

RE: First Level Appeal of Denied Claim
ATTN: Appeals Department

PATIENT: [Full Name]
DOB: [MM/DD/YYYY]
MEMBER ID: [Member Number]
CLAIM NUMBER: [Payer Claim Control Number]
DATE OF SERVICE: [MM/DD/YYYY - MM/DD/YYYY]
SERVICE DENIED: [CPT Code(s) and Description(s)]
DENIAL CODE: [CARC(s) and RARC(s)]
DENIAL REASON: [State the payer's exact denial reason from the remittance advice]

Dear Appeals Reviewer:

We are writing to appeal the denial of [service/procedure] for [patient name], as described in the remittance advice dated [remittance date]. The claim was denied with code [CARC] citing [denial reason from payer].

[Paragraph 1: Background and Clinical Summary]
[Briefly describe the patient's clinical presentation, relevant medical history, and the reason the service was ordered/performed. Keep this to 2-3 sentences with the most critical clinical facts.]

[Paragraph 2: Why the Denial is Incorrect]
[Specifically address each reason the payer gave for the denial. Map clinical documentation to the payer's medical necessity criteria. For each criterion the payer claims was not met, explain exactly how it was met with references to the attached documentation.]

[Paragraph 3: Supporting Evidence]
[Summarize the attached documentation and explain how each document supports the medical necessity or correct coding of the service.]

[Paragraph 4: Clinical References (if applicable)]
[Cite relevant LCD/NCD criteria, MCG/InterQual guidelines, peer-reviewed literature, or specialty society guidelines that support the service.]

[Closing]
Based on the clinical evidence and supporting documentation provided, we respectfully request that you reverse the denial and process this claim for payment in accordance with the patient's benefit plan.

If you require any additional information or have questions about this appeal, please contact [name] at [phone] or [email].

Sincerely,

[Provider Name, Credentials]
[Provider NPI]
[Provider Signature]

Enclosures: [List all enclosures]
```

## 3. Evidence Requirements for Appeals

### Types of Acceptable Evidence

**Primary Sources (Most Persuasive):**

| Evidence Type | Best For | What to Include |
|---------------|----------|-----------------|
| Office/progress notes | All denials | Date, time, chief complaint, HPI, exam findings, assessment, plan, signature |
| Operative reports | Surgical denials | Pre-op diagnosis, post-op diagnosis, procedure description, findings, estimated blood loss, specimens, signatures |
| Discharge summaries | Inpatient denials | Admission date, discharge date, admitting diagnosis, discharge diagnosis, hospital course, discharge instructions, discharge condition |
| Lab results | Medical necessity | Dates, values, reference ranges, clinical interpretation |
| Imaging reports | Medical necessity | Indication, findings, impression, comparison to prior studies |
| Pathology reports | Diagnosis support | Specimen source, gross description, microscopic description, diagnosis, comments |
| Consultation reports | Specialty services | Reason for consult, findings, recommendations |
| Nursing notes | Level of care | Vital signs, assessments, interventions, response to treatment |
| Physician orders | Authorizations | Signed, dated orders for the specific service |

**Secondary Sources (Supporting Evidence):**

| Evidence Type | Best For | What to Include |
|---------------|----------|-----------------|
| CMS LCD/NCD | Medicare denials | The specific LCD/NCD with the criteria highlighted and marked where patient meets criteria |
| MCG guidelines | Medical necessity | The specific guideline citation with criteria marked as met |
| InterQual criteria | Medical necessity | Criteria checklist with clinical findings mapped |
| Specialty society guidelines | Medical necessity, experimental | Published guidelines from AAFP, ACS, ACR, ASCO, AAN, etc. |
| Peer-reviewed literature | Experimental/investigational | PubMed-indexed studies, meta-analyses, systematic reviews |
| UpToDate | Medical necessity | Clinical topic review with treatment recommendations |
| CPT Assistant | Coding denials | Official AMA coding guidance for the specific code |
| AHA Coding Clinic | Coding denials | Official coding guidance for ICD-10-CM and ICD-10-PCS |
| FDA approval documentation | Drug/device denials | FDA label, approval letter, indication statement |

### How to Organize and Present Evidence

1. **Create a table of contents** listing each attached document with a brief description
2. **Tab and label** each document for easy reference (Tab A, Tab B, Tab C, etc.)
3. **Highlight key sections** that directly address the denial reason — use a highlighter or marginal notes
4. **Create a cross-reference table** mapping the payer's denial criteria to specific documentation:

```
PAYER'S DENIAL REASON: Service not medically necessary — patient did not meet 
the following criteria:

Payer Criterion                  How Met                        Evidence
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Patient must have failed      Patient tried NSAIDs for       Progress note     
   conservative therapy for      6 weeks without relief          4/15/2025 (Tab A)
   at least 4 weeks

2. Patient must have             MRI shows Grade III            MRI report
   imaging evidence of           meniscal tear                   3/20/2025 (Tab B)
   structural pathology

3. Patient must have             ROM limited to 90 degrees       Physical therapy
   functional limitation         flexion; VAS pain score 7/10    eval 4/10/2025 (Tab C)
```

5. **Include a brief summary** at the beginning explaining what each document proves
6. **Label each page** with the patient name, claim number, and date of service
7. **Staple or bind** documents securely — do not use paper clips for mailed appeals

### Evidence Checklist by Denial Type

**Medical Necessity Denial:**
- [ ] Physician progress note documenting clinical presentation and exam findings
- [ ] Relevant diagnostic test results (lab, imaging, pathology)
- [ ] Documentation of failed conservative therapy (if required)
- [ ] Physician's letter of medical necessity
- [ ] LCD or NCD showing criteria are met (for Medicare)
- [ ] Clinical guidelines (MCG, InterQual) showing criteria are met
- [ ] Prior authorization (if applicable)
- [ ] Referral from primary care or referring physician

**Coding Denial:**
- [ ] Operative or procedure note documenting the exact procedure performed
- [ ] CPT Assistant or AHA Coding Clinic reference supporting the code used
- [ ] Medical necessity documentation supporting the diagnosis code
- [ ] Modifier justification (if modifier was used)
- [ ] Place of service documentation (if relevant)
- [ ] Provider credentialing/npi/taxonomy documentation (if relevant)

**Prior Authorization Denial:**
- [ ] Copy of the authorization (approval letter, number, dates)
- [ ] Proof of submission (fax confirmation, portal screenshot)
- [ ] If emergency: clinical documentation showing emergency nature of the service
- [ ] If retroactive: clinical justification for why authorization was not obtained in advance
- [ ] Physician statement explaining urgency

**Timely Filing Denial:**
- [ ] Clearinghouse submission log showing the submission date
- [ ] 997 Functional Acknowledgment
- [ ] 277CA Claim Status showing received date
- [ ] Fax confirmation (if submitted by fax)
- [ ] Certified mail receipt (if submitted by mail)
- [ ] Payer portal screenshot
- [ ] If late: good cause exception request with supporting documentation

**Benefit Limit Denial:**
- [ ] Benefit summary showing remaining benefits
- [ ] Clinical justification for exceeding the limit
- [ ] Treatment plan with goals and expected outcomes
- [ ] Documentation of functional improvement to date
- [ ] If mental health: MHPAEA parity analysis

## 4. Appeal Strategies by Denial Type

### Medical Necessity Appeal Strategy

**Step 1: Understand the denial reason**
- Identify the specific medical necessity criteria the payer claims were not met
- Read the RARCs accompanying the denial (N386, N190, N191, N192, N325, N525)
- If the denial is from Medicare, check which LCD or NCD applies
- If from a commercial payer, get a copy of their medical policy

**Step 2: Gather clinical documentation**
- Collect all records from the date of service and relevant prior records
- Focus on documentation that directly addresses each unmet criterion
- If records are insufficient, obtain a retrospective physician letter of medical necessity

**Step 3: Create a criteria compliance table**
- List each criterion from the payer's medical policy
- Map specific clinical findings from the medical record to each criterion
- Identify and address any gaps in documentation

**Step 4: Draft the appeal**
- Start with the most clinically compelling facts
- Use the payer's own criteria language to frame your argument
- Be specific — do not make general statements about patient condition
- Reference specific page numbers in the attached records

**Step 5: Consider a peer-to-peer review**
- If available, request a peer-to-peer review with a physician in the same specialty
- Prepare the physician by reviewing the denial reason and the patient's clinical course
- The conversation is most effective when the treating physician makes the case directly

**Key to success:** The payer is looking for documentation that the service was:
1. **Medically appropriate** for the patient's symptoms and diagnosis
2. **Proven effective** for the condition (evidence-based)
3. **Not primarily for convenience** of the patient or provider
4. **The most appropriate level or setting** of care

### Coding Appeal Strategy

**Step 1: Verify the code is correct**
- Confirm the CPT/HCPCS code accurately represents the procedure performed
- Confirm the ICD-10-CM code is at the highest level of specificity
- Verify the modifier is correct and appropriate
- Check NCCI edits and MUE values

**Step 2: Identify the specific error**
- Was the code wrong? (wrong CPT, wrong diagnosis, wrong modifier)
- Did the payer misinterpret the code?
- Is this a payer-specific code restriction?
- Were NCCI edits applied incorrectly?

**Step 3: Gather supporting references**
- CPT Assistant for the specific code and guidance
- AHA Coding Clinic for ICD-10 guidance
- CMS NCCI Policy Manual for bundling issues
- Specialty-specific coding resources

**Step 4: Draft the appeal**
- Explain the coding logic clearly and step by step
- Reference specific coding guidelines
- Include the relevant documentation (operative note, procedure note)
- If modifier was used, explain the clinical rationale for the modifier

**Step 5: Correct and resubmit (if alternative)**
- If the code was truly wrong, consider a corrected claim (frequency code 7) instead of an appeal
- Only appeal if you believe your coding was correct and the payer's decision was erroneous

### Prior Authorization Appeal Strategy

**Scenario A: Authorization was obtained but not recognized**
1. Search thoroughly for the authorization record (portal, fax, phone call recording)
2. Gather all proof: authorization number, effective dates, approved CPT codes
3. Submit a formal appeal with the authorization documentation
4. Request the claims department attach the authorization to the claim
5. Escalate to a supervisor if the first-level representative cannot resolve

**Scenario B: Authorization was not obtained**
1. Determine if an exception applies:
   - Emergency services (EMTALA, prudent layperson standard)
   - Retroactive authorization (some payers allow this within 30 days)
   - Continuity of care provisions (patient in active treatment)
   - State law requiring coverage
2. Submit a retroactive authorization request if the payer allows it
3. If retroactive auth is denied, appeal based on medical necessity — the argument is that the service was medically necessary regardless of the authorization
4. Some states have "no prior auth for emergency services" laws — cite these

**Scenario C: Authorization was partial**
1. Determine the additional services needed beyond what was authorized
2. Document the clinical changes that made the additional services necessary
3. Request reconsideration of the authorization based on new clinical information
4. Appeal based on medical necessity of the additional services

### Timely Filing Appeal Strategy

**Scenario A: Claim was filed on time**
1. Gather proof of timely filing:
   - Clearinghouse logs showing submission date/time
   - 997 acknowledgment (if available)
   - 277CA showing the claim was received by the payer
   - Fax confirmation with date and time
   - Certified mail receipt
2. Submit the proof with a written statement explaining the timeline
3. Request the payer reprocess the claim with timely filing waived

**Scenario B: Claim was filed late**
1. Determine if a good cause exception applies (Medicare only)
2. Provide supporting documentation for the good cause:
   - Documentation of the catastrophic event
   - Medical records showing provider incapacitation
   - Proof of system failure
   - Correspondence with the payer showing conflicting instructions
3. Write a detailed explanation of why the filing was late
4. Submit the good cause request with the appeal

**Key point:** Timely filing is a strict liability rule — most payers do not waive it. Your best chance is proof of timely submission. If you cannot prove timely filing and no good cause exception applies, the appeal is unlikely to succeed.

### Benefit Limit Appeal Strategy

1. **Verify the benefit limit**: Confirm the exact limit, what has been used, and what remains
2. **Check for errors**: Payer systems often miscalculate remaining benefits or apply the wrong benefit category
3. **Request an exception**: Some payers allow additional visits with clinical justification:
   - Demonstrate functional improvement from prior visits
   - Show that treatment goals are not yet met
   - Provide a treatment plan with projected outcomes
   - Explain why additional visits are necessary to avoid regression
4. **Appeal based on medical necessity**: Argue that medical necessity should override the benefit limit
5. **Consider mental health parity**: If the limit applies to mental health services, compare it to medical/surgical limits — under MHPAEA, mental health limits cannot be more restrictive
6. **Patient responsibility**: If all appeal options fail, the patient may be financially responsible

### Bundling/Global Period Appeal Strategy

1. **Verify the bundling edit**: Check NCCI edits, global surgery period rules, or payer-specific bundling policies
2. **Document distinct services**: If appealing based on separate procedures, document:
   - Different anatomical sites
   - Separate incisions or approaches
   - Different diagnoses
   - Separate indications for each procedure
3. **Use the appropriate modifier** (if applicable):
   - 59 — Distinct procedural service
   - XE — Separate encounter
   - XS — Separate structure
   - XP — Separate practitioner
   - XU — Unusual non-overlapping service
4. **Appeal with clinical documentation**: Show why the services were distinct and separately identifiable
5. **If the bundling was correct**: Accept the payment and appeal only if the bundled service has independent medical necessity

## 6. Appeal Success Rates

### Medicare Appeal Success Rates by Level

| Level | Success Rate | Notes |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| Level 1 (Redetermination) | 50-60% | Most favorable initial level; new review by a different MAC reviewer |
| Level 2 (QIC Reconsideration) | 15-25% | QICs are more rigorous; most denials are upheld |
| Level 3 (ALJ Hearing) | 60-70% | Independent ALJs favorable to providers |
| Level 4 (Appeals Council) | <5% | Very few appeals granted |
| Level 5 (Federal Court) | <10% | High burden of proof |

### Commercial Payer Appeal Success Rates

| Level | Success Rate | Notes |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| Level 1 (Internal) | 40-55% | Similar to Medicare Level 1 |
| Level 2 (Peer Review) | 20-35% | Physician-to-physician review can be effective |
| Level 3 (External) | 40-50%| Independent review is favorable when a clinical basis exists |

### Factors That Increase Appeal Success

1. **Complete and organized documentation**: Appeals with organized, tabbed exhibits and a clear explanation have higher success rates
2. **Directly addressing the denial reason**: Do not provide generic clinical documentation — address each specific criterion the payer cited
3. **Physician involvement**: Appeals that include a physician's letter of medical necessity are more likely to succeed than those written only by billing staff
4. **Citing specific guidelines**: Referencing MCG, InterQual, LCD/NCD, or specialty society guidelines by name and section number
5. **Peer-to-peer review**: A pre-denial or pre-appeal phone call between the treating physician and a payer medical director
6. **Timeliness**: Filing within the first half of the appeal window (not waiting until the deadline)
7. **Multiple levels**: Most providers only appeal at Level 1. Filing Level 2 and Level 3 appeals increases cumulative success

### Cost-Benefit Analysis for Appeals

| Denial Amount | Appeal Effort | Recommended Action |
|---------------|--------------|-------------------|
| $0 - $50 | Low priority | Only appeal if pattern or precedent is important |
| $50 - $200 | Moderate | Appeal at Level 1 only |
| $200 - $1,000 | Significant | Appeal through Level 2 |
| $1,000 - $5,000 | High | Appeal through all available levels |
| > $5,000 | Critical | Appeal all levels; consider legal counsel for Level 3 |
| Pattern of small denials | Track for batch appeal | Aggregate multiple small denials for a batch appeal |

## Agent Q&A Pairs for Training

**Q: What is the filing deadline for a Medicare Level 1 appeal (Redetermination)?**
**A:** The filing deadline for a Medicare Level 1 appeal (Redetermination) is 120 calendar days from the date of the remittance notice (the date on the Medicare Summary Notice or remittance advice). This is a strict deadline — if the appeal is received after 120 days, it will be dismissed as untimely unless good cause for late filing is established. The 120-day window starts from the date of the notice, not the date of service. For example, if the remittance is dated March 1, 2025, the appeal deadline is June 29, 2025 (120 calendar days). Always file well before the deadline — do not wait until the last day.

**Q: What is the Medicare 5-level appeal structure in order?**
**A:** The Medicare 5-level appeal structure is: Level 1 — Redetermination by the Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC), filed within 120 days from the remittance, decided in 60 days. Level 2 — Reconsideration by a Qualified Independent Contractor (QIC), filed within 180 days from the Level 1 decision, decided in 30-60 days. Level 3 — Hearing by an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) at OMHA, filed within 60 days from the Level 2 decision, with a minimum $180 amount in controversy. Level 4 — Review by the Medicare Appeals Council (part of the Departmental Appeals Board), filed within 60 days from the ALJ decision, no minimum controversy. Level 5 — Judicial review in Federal District Court, filed within 60 days from the Appeals Council decision, with a minimum $1,830 amount in controversy.

**Q: What is the success rate for each Medicare appeal level?**
**A:** Level 1 (Redetermination by MAC): 50-60% success rate. This is the best opportunity for reversal as the MAC takes a fresh look at the claim. Level 2 (QIC Reconsideration): 15-25% success rate. QICs are more stringent and uphold most MAC decisions. Level 3 (ALJ Hearing): 60-70% success rate. ALJs are independent and more favorable to providers. Level 4 (Medicare Appeals Council): Less than 5% success rate. Very few cases are granted review. Level 5 (Federal District Court): Less than 10% success rate. The standard is very high for overturning Medicare decisions. The pattern is notable: Level 1 and Level 3 are the best opportunities for success. Level 2 is the most difficult level. If a claim can survive Level 2, Level 3 is very favorable.

**Q: How do I file a Medicare Level 1 appeal?**
**A:** To file a Medicare Level 1 appeal (Redetermination): (1) Obtain the CMS-10003 form (Appeal of Medicare Claim Decision) from the CMS website or your MAC's website, OR submit a written request with the same information. (2) Complete all required fields: beneficiary name, Medicare number (MBI), claim number, date of service, specific denial reason being appealed, and a detailed explanation of why the denial is incorrect. (3) Attach supporting documentation (medical records, clinical notes, supporting literature). (4) Submit to the MAC that issued the denial — find the address on the remittance advice. (5) Methods: online via the MAC's portal (fastest), fax (get confirmation), certified mail (proof of delivery). (6) Retain copies of everything submitted and the proof of submission. The decision must be issued within 60 calendar days.

**Q: What is a QIC and what role does it play in Medicare appeals?**
**A:** A QIC (Qualified Independent Contractor) handles Medicare Level 2 appeals (Reconsideration). QICs are independent contractors hired by CMS to provide an independent review of MAC decisions. There are two national QICs: C2C Solutions (handles Part A reconsiderations — inpatient, SNF, home health, hospice) and Maximus Federal Services (handles Part B reconsiderations — physician services, outpatient, DME). The QIC's role is to review the Level 1 decision, any new evidence submitted, and decide whether to uphold or reverse. QIC decisions are binding unless further appealed to an ALJ. QICs must issue a decision within 30 calendar days (standard) or 72 hours (expedited). Filing a Level 2 appeal requires the CMS-20033 form and must be submitted within 180 days of the Level 1 decision.

**Q: What is the minimum amount in controversy for a Medicare ALJ hearing?**
**A:** For 2024, the minimum amount in controversy for a Medicare ALJ hearing (Level 3) is $180. This amount is indexed annually and typically increases slightly each year. The amount is calculated as: (1) For Part A claims: the amount of Medicare payment that is at issue (the denied payment amount), (2) For Part B claims: the amount of the denied Part B payment, (3) The total must meet the minimum threshold, and (4) Multiple claims can be aggregated to meet the minimum if they involve the same or similar issues. For Level 5 (Federal District Court), the minimum amount in controversy is $1,830 for 2024. If the amount in controversy does not meet the threshold, that appeal level is not available.

**Q: What should I include in a medical necessity appeal letter?**
**A:** A medical necessity appeal letter should include: (1) Patient and claim identification: patient name, DOB, member ID, claim number, date of service, CPT code(s), denial code and reason. (2) Clinical summary: Brief patient history, presenting symptoms, clinical findings, diagnosis. (3) Specific response to each denial criterion: List each criterion the payer said was not met, and explain with specific clinical evidence how the patient met each criterion. (4) Supporting documentation references: Cite specific records (progress note dated X, lab results dated Y, imaging report dated Z) and highlight the relevant sections. (5) Clinical guidelines cited: Reference MCG, InterQual, LCD/NCD, specialty society guidelines by name and section. (6) Treating physician's statement of medical necessity. (7) Closing: Explicit request for reversal, contact information. The key is to address each specific reason the payer gave for the denial — do not write a general letter about the patient's condition.

**Q: Should I submit new evidence at a Medicare Level 2 (QIC) appeal?**
**A:** Yes, you should submit NEW evidence at the Medicare Level 2 QIC appeal if it is available. The QIC's review is independent and they will consider new evidence that was not submitted at Level 1. In fact, submitting new evidence that addresses the deficiencies found at Level 1 is one of the most effective strategies. If the Level 1 decision identified missing documentation or insufficient clinical justification, address those gaps with the new evidence at Level 2. However, if you have no new evidence to submit and your Level 1 submission was comprehensive, you can rely on the existing record. Note that the QIC will not seek additional evidence on your behalf — you must submit everything you want them to consider.

**Q: What is the difference between a standard appeal and an expedited appeal?**
**A:** A standard appeal follows the normal processing timeline (60 days for Medicare Level 1, 30-45 days for most commercial). An expedited appeal (also called urgent or fast-track) is available when: (1) The appeal involves ongoing treatment that could be delayed, (2) The standard timeline could seriously jeopardize the patient's life or health, (3) The patient is in severe pain that cannot be managed without the appealed service. Expedited appeals must be decided within 72 hours for both Medicare and most commercial payers. The request for expedited review can be made verbally (by phone) for Medicare. The provider (or patient) must explain why the standard timeline would cause harm. If the payer denies the expedited request, the appeal automatically reverts to standard processing. Use expedited appeals sparingly — they should be reserved for truly urgent clinical situations.

**Q: What is an external independent review and when is it available?**
**A:** An external independent review (EIR) is a review of a denied claim by an organization that is independent of both the provider and the payer. Under the Affordable Care Act, most non-grandfathered health plans must offer external review for adverse benefit determinations. Key features: (1) The external reviewer is a certified IRO (Independent Review Organization) such as MAXIMUS, MCMC, IMS, or similar entities. (2) The IRO reviews whether the plan correctly applied its medical necessity criteria. (3) The decision is binding on the plan (but not necessarily on the provider/patient). (4) The provider/patient does not pay for the external review. (5) The IRO has appropriate clinical experts (physicians in the relevant specialty). (6) External review is typically available after the plan's internal appeals are exhausted (Level 2 for most plans). Caveat: Self-funded ERISA plans are not required by federal law to offer external review, but many voluntarily do. Check the plan document for external review availability.

**Q: My claim was denied by a RAC (Recovery Audit Contractor). What appeal process do I follow?**
**A:** RAC denials follow the standard Medicare 5-level appeal process — there is no special RAC appeal process. However, there are important differences: (1) The RAC issues an overpayment demand letter, not a claim denial — you receive this separately from your usual remittance advice. (2) The appeal timeline starts from the RAC overpayment letter date, NOT the original claim remittance date. (3) You have 120 days from the RAC overpayment letter to file a Level 1 Redetermination with the MAC. (4) Unlike initial claim denials, RAC denials often involve review of medical records that must be submitted within 45 days of the RAC's records request. (5) If you do not respond to the RAC's medical records request, the claim is denied by default. (6) RAC denials frequently involve medical necessity for inpatient admissions — be prepared with comprehensive clinical documentation. The most common RAC denial issue is inpatient status vs. observation status.

**Q: How do I appeal a claim that was denied for both medical necessity AND prior authorization issues?**
**A:** When a claim is denied for multiple reasons, you must address ALL denial reasons in your appeal. The approach: (1) For the prior authorization issue: If authorization was obtained, provide the authorization number, effective dates, and approved CPT codes. If not obtained, explain the circumstances (emergency, retroactive request, etc.). (2) For the medical necessity issue: Provide full clinical documentation, a physician's letter of medical necessity, and cite clinical guidelines that support the service. (3) The appeal letter should have separate sections addressing each denial reason with distinct evidence for each. (4) Some payers apply "layered denials" — they first deny for lack of auth, and then also deny for medical necessity. If the auth issue is resolved, the medical necessity denial still stands and must be addressed. (5) Include a cover sheet listing all denial reasons and indicating which section of the appeal addresses each reason.

**Q: What is a peer-to-peer review and how do I request one?**
**A:** A peer-to-peer (P2P) review is a conversation between the patient's treating physician and a medical director or physician reviewer at the insurance company. It allows the treating physician to explain the clinical rationale for a service directly to a clinical decision-maker. To request a P2P: (1) Determine if the payer offers P2P reviews — most major payers do, especially for pre-service denials. (2) Call the payer's provider services line and ask for a physician-to-physician review. (3) Have the patient's treating physician available for the call (not billing staff). (4) Prepare: review the denial reason, the patient's chart, relevant clinical guidelines, and have key clinical facts ready. (5) Be concise and focused — the call typically lasts 5-15 minutes. (6) Document the call with the date, reviewer name, what was discussed, and the outcome. P2P reviews are most effective when the physician can clearly articulate why the service was medically necessary, cite specific clinical findings, and reference evidence-based guidelines. For Medicare Advantage plans, P2P is often required before filing a formal appeal.

**Q: How do I appeal a Workers' Compensation denial?**
**A:** Workers' Compensation appeals are entirely state-specific. General steps: (1) Identify the type of denial — UR (Utilization Review) denial of treatment, or claim denial (the injury/condition is not compensable). (2) For UR denials: Most states require a written request for reconsideration to the UR agent within 20-60 days of the denial. (3) The treating physician should request the UR reconsideration — supporting documentation is critical. (4) If the UR reconsideration is denied, most states allow an appeal to the state Workers' Compensation board, commission, or appeals panel. (5) For claim denials: File an application for adjudication with the state Workers' Comp board. (6) Evidence should include: physician reports, diagnostic studies, a detailed narrative linking the injury to a specific work event, accident reports, and witness statements. (7) Deadlines are very strict — missing a WC deadline can permanently bar the claim. (8) Workers' Comp appeals can involve multiple issues: medical treatment, temporary disability, permanent disability, and vocational rehabilitation — each may have separate appeal paths and deadlines.

**Q: What is a corrected claim and when should I use it instead of an appeal?**
**A:** A corrected claim (submitted with frequency code 7) is used to fix minor data errors on a claim that has already been adjudicated but not yet denied, or to provide missing information. Use a corrected claim when: (1) The claim was paid but with an incorrect code that needs adjustment, (2) The claim was rejected (not denied) due to a data entry error (wrong DOB, wrong member ID, wrong NPI), (3) You need to add a missing modifier, (4) You need to correct a diagnosis code, (5) You need to add missing information (referring provider NPI, taxonomy code, etc.). Do NOT use a corrected claim when: (1) The claim was denied — always appeal a denial, (2) You want to challenge a medical necessity decision — this requires an appeal, (3) The claim was denied for timely filing — a corrected claim will not reset the filing window. A corrected claim with frequency code 7 is processed as a new claim with a new timely filing clock. An appeal challenges the initial adjudication decision.

**Q: How do I calculate the Medicare appeal filing deadline from the remittance date?**
**A:** The Medicare appeal filing deadline is 120 calendar days from the date on the remittance advice (RA) or Medicare Summary Notice (MSN). Calculation: Start counting from the day AFTER the RA date. The last day to file is 120 days later. Example: RA dated January 15, 2025. Day 1 = January 16, 2025. Day 120 = May 15, 2025. If Day 120 falls on a weekend or federal holiday, the deadline extends to the next business day. For Level 2 (QIC), the deadline is 180 calendar days from the Level 1 decision date. For Level 3 (ALJ), the deadline is 60 calendar days from the Level 2 decision date. It is critical to calendar all deadlines as soon as you receive the denial. Many appeals are denied because providers miscalculate the deadline. Always file well before the deadline — do not wait until the last day.

**Q: What is the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA) and how does it apply to appeal?**
**A:** MHPAEA (federal law, effective 2010) requires that health plan financial requirements and treatment limitations for mental health and substance use disorder (MH/SUD) benefits cannot be more restrictive than those for medical/surgical benefits. This means: (1) Deductibles, copays, and coinsurance for MH/SUD must be comparable to medical/surgical, (2) Visit limits (e.g., 20 therapy visits per year) cannot be more restrictive than limits on medical/surgical services, (3) Prior authorization requirements for MH/SUD must be comparable, (4) Non-quantitative treatment limitations (NQTLs) like medical necessity criteria, step therapy, and fail-first protocols must be applied no more restrictively. If you are appealing a benefit limit denial for mental health services, check if the limit is consistent with medical/surgical limits. If not, cite MHPAEA in your appeal. Examples of MHPAEA violations: mental health visits limited to 20 per year while medical visits have no annual limit; authorization required for every MH visit but not for comparable medical visits. The Departments of Labor, Health and Human Services, and Treasury enforce MHPAEA.

**Q: What is the difference between a Medicare Level 1 appeal filed by a provider vs. by a beneficiary?**
**A:** The Level 1 appeal process is essentially the same whether filed by a provider or a beneficiary, but there are important differences: (1) Provider appeals: The provider files as a "party" to the appeal when they have accepted assignment and are appealing a claim payment denial. The provider must have a valid Medicare enrollment (NPI/TIN). (2) Beneficiary appeals: The beneficiary files when they are appealing a service that was denied as not medically necessary and they received the service (liability) or will receive the service (pre-service). (3) A provider cannot appeal on behalf of a beneficiary who has not assigned their benefits. (4) For assignment appeals, the provider can only appeal claim payment decisions, not coverage determinations that affect the beneficiary (like preventive service denials). (5) The beneficiary has appeal rights even if the provider chooses not to appeal. (6) A provider who does not accept assignment cannot appeal on the beneficiary's behalf — the beneficiary must appeal directly. In practice, most Medicare appeals are filed by providers who have accepted assignment.

**Q: How do I appeal a claim that was denied under the Medicare 3-day payment window rule?**
**A:** The 3-day payment window rule requires that outpatient diagnostic and non-diagnostic services provided within 3 calendar days of an inpatient admission be bundled into the inpatient DRG payment. To appeal: (1) Determine if the services were truly related to the admission — if the services are unrelated (completely different diagnosis, different body system), they can be billed separately. (2) For unrelated services: Appeal with documentation showing the services were unrelated to the admission reason — include medical records from both the outpatient service and the inpatient admission to demonstrate the different clinical conditions. (3) If the services were related: The bundling is correct and cannot be appealed. (4) Alternative strategy: If the inpatient admission was later downgraded to observation (and the denial was based on inpatient medical necessity), the 3-day window no longer applies and the outpatient services should be paid separately. (5) The 3-day window runs from the date of admission counting backward 3 calendar days (including the admission date). Exception: The 3-day window does not apply to Part B services for dialysis patients enrolled in Medicare as their ESRD benefit.

**Q: What should I do if my appeal is denied at Level 1 and I do not have new evidence for Level 2?**
**A:** Even without new evidence, you should still file a Level 2 appeal if the denial amount justifies it. The QIC (Qualified Independent Contractor) conducts an independent review, which means they do not simply defer to the MAC's Level 1 decision. Even with the same evidence, the QIC could reach a different conclusion. Strategies: (1) Strengthen your argument — refine your explanation of how the evidence meets the medical necessity criteria, (2) Reorganize your evidence in a clearer format — a better-organized appeal can be more persuasive, (3) Get a letter from the treating physician specifically addressing the Level 1 denial reason (this is effectively "new" evidence if it was not submitted before), (4) If a peer-to-peer review is available, request one before filing the Level 2 appeal, (5) Cite any new clinical guidelines or literature that support the service, (6) If you truly have no new evidence and no new arguments, you may choose to skip Level 2 and proceed to Level 3 (ALJ) if the amount in controversy meets the threshold. However, you must complete Level 2 to be eligible for Level 3.

**Q: How do I determine if I should use a corrected claim or an appeal for a coding error?**
**A:** Use this decision tree: (1) Was the claim paid? Yes — use a corrected claim (frequency code 7) if the error was in the coding. No — go to step 2. (2) Was the claim denied for a reason other than coding (e.g., medical necessity, no auth)? Yes — file an appeal, not a corrected claim. No (denied solely for coding) — go to step 3. (3) Was the code truly incorrect (wrong CPT, wrong ICD-10, wrong modifier)? Yes — use a corrected claim with the correct code. No (the code was correct but the payer's edit was wrong) — file an appeal. (4) Was the denial due to an NCCI edit? Yes — appeal with modifier justification or corrected modifier. (5) Did the payer incorrectly apply an edit? Yes — file an appeal. (6) Is it within the payer's corrected claim window? Some payers accept corrected claims only within a specific timeframe (typically 60-120 days from original submission). Key rule: If the claim was denied for any clinical reason (medical necessity, authorization), always appeal. If the claim was denied for a purely administrative/coding error (invalid code, missing modifier, wrong DOB), a corrected claim may be appropriate.

**Q: What is an overpayment and how is it different from a denied claim?**
**A:** An overpayment is when the payer has already paid a claim and subsequently determines the payment was incorrect and demands repayment. This is different from a denied claim (where no payment was made). Overpayments can result from: (1) RAC audits finding insufficient medical necessity, (2) Correct coding reviews finding upcoding or unbundling, (3) Duplicate payment identification, (4) Retroactive eligibility loss (patient lost coverage retroactively), (5) COB changes after payment, (6) Provider enrollment issues. Overpayment appeals follow the same Medicare 5-level process if from Medicare. Commercial overpayment appeals follow the commercial payer's provider dispute process. Overpayment response timelines are shorter than claim denial timelines — typically 30-60 days to respond. If you do not respond to an overpayment demand, the payer will recoup the amount from future payments. If you think the overpayment is incorrect, file a written appeal immediately.

**Q: How do I appeal a denial for a procedure that was not pre-authorized because it was an emergency?**
**A:** Emergency services have special protections that can be cited in appeals: (1) EMTALA (Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act) — hospitals must provide stabilizing treatment for emergency conditions regardless of insurance or authorization status. (2) The "prudent layperson standard" — if the patient's symptoms would cause a reasonable person to believe emergency care was needed, the service should be covered even without prior authorization. (3) Most states have laws requiring health plans to cover emergency services without prior authorization. (4) Most payer policies include an exception for emergency services. To appeal: (1) Cite the applicable law (EMTALA and/or state law), (2) Cite the prudent layperson standard, (3) Provide clinical documentation showing the patient's presenting symptoms and the emergency nature of the condition, (4) Explain why the service could not be pre-authorized (the patient came through the ER, the condition was acute and required immediate intervention), (5) Include the triage note, ED record, and discharge summary showing the evaluation and treatment. This appeal should focus on the legal requirement to cover emergency services, not on the authorization deficiency.

**Q: What is the process for appealing a medical necessity denial at the ALJ hearing level?**
**A:** The ALJ hearing (Medicare Level 3) is a formal hearing process. To prepare: (1) File the request on OMHA-100 form within 60 days of the Level 2 QIC decision — ensure the minimum $180 amount in controversy is met. (2) Decide on hearing type: in-person (most favorable, but requires travel), video (effective alternative), telephone (limited for complex cases), or paper review (least favorable).
