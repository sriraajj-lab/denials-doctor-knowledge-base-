# Timely Filing Rules: Comprehensive Reference for Denials Doctor

## Overview

Timely filing rules establish the deadlines by which healthcare claims and appeals must be submitted to payers. Missing these deadlines is one of the most common — and most preventable — reasons for claim denials. Unlike medical necessity or coding denials, timely filing denials are purely administrative and often irreversible without extraordinary circumstances.

This document provides a complete reference on timely filing rules for all major payers, state-specific requirements, proof of timely submission, good cause exceptions, and prevention strategies.

---

## 1. Understanding Timely Filing

### What is Timely Filing?

Timely filing is the requirement that a healthcare claim be submitted to a payer within a specific period following the date of service (or date of discharge for inpatient services). If the claim is submitted after the deadline, the payer will deny the claim with CARC CO-29 ("The time limit for filing has expired") without considering the clinical validity of the claim.

### Why Timely Filing Rules Exist

- **Claims processing efficiency**: Payers need to close their books and process claims within specific fiscal periods
- **Reserve management**: Insurers set aside financial reserves based on expected claims; late claims disrupt this process
- **Record availability**: Claims are easier to verify when submitted closer to the service date
- **Prevention of fraud**: Late submissions can be harder to verify for medical necessity

### Key Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Date of Service (DOS)** | The date the service was rendered (or from-date if a range) |
| **Date of Discharge** | For inpatient claims, the date the patient was discharged |
| **Date of Receipt** | The date the payer received the claim (NOT the date submitted) |
| **Filing Window** | The maximum number of days from the DOS/discharge to submit the claim |
| **Timely Filing Deadline** | The last date on which the claim can be submitted and considered timely |
| **Date of Remittance** | The date on the payment/denial notice from the payer |
| **Appeal Filing Window** | The maximum number of days from the remittance date to file an appeal |
| **Good Cause Extension** | An exception to the timely filing deadline for extraordinary circumstances |

## 3. Filing Deadline Calculation Methods

### Standard Calculation from Date of Service

The simplest method: count forward from the date of service.

**Example: Medicare (12-month deadline)**
- Date of Service: March 15, 2025
- Filing Deadline: March 15, 2025 + 365 days = March 15, 2026
- Claim must be RECEIVED by the MAC by March 15, 2026

**Example: UHC (180-day deadline)**
- Date of Service: March 15, 2025
- Filing Deadline: March 15, 2025 + 180 days = September 11, 2025
- Claim must be RECEIVED by UHC by September 11, 2025

### Inpatient Claims (Date of Discharge)

Most payers use the date of discharge (not date of admission) for inpatient claims.

**Example: Medicare Inpatient**
- Admission: January 5, 2025
- Discharge: January 10, 2025
- Filing Deadline: January 10, 2025 + 365 days = January 10, 2026

**Example: UHC Inpatient**
- Admission: January 5, 2025
- Discharge: January 10, 2025
- Filing Deadline: January 10, 2025 + 180 days = July 9, 2025

### Periodic/Recurring Claims

For services billed on a recurring basis (e.g., home health, hospice, DME):

| Service Type | Billing Period | Filing Calculation |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Home Health | 30-day episodes | 12 months from last DOS in episode (Medicare) |
| Hospice | 30-day periods | 12 months from last DOS in period |
| DME (capped rental) | Monthly | 12 months from each individual DOS |
| Physical Therapy | Per visit or per period | 12 months from each DOS |

### Secondary Claims

Timely filing for secondary claims can be calculated differently:

1. **Standard method**: From the date of service (same as primary)
2. **COB method**: From the date of the primary payer's EOB/RA — some payers allow a secondary window starting from the primary payment/denial
3. **Best practice**: File secondary claims as soon as the primary payer's EOB/RA is received
4. **Warning**: Some payers use BOTH the DOS deadline AND the primary EOB receipt date — the earlier of the two deadlines applies

### Appeal Deadline Calculation

**Example: Medicare Appeal**
- Remittance Date: March 1, 2025
- Level 1 Appeal Deadline: March 1, 2025 + 120 days = June 29, 2025

**Example: UHC Appeal**
- Denial Notice Date: April 15, 2025
- Level 1 Appeal Deadline: April 15, 2025 + 180 days = October 12, 2025

### Weekend/Holiday Rule

If the filing deadline falls on a weekend or federal/state holiday, the deadline typically extends to the next business day. However, this rule is NOT universally applied. Some payers may strictly enforce the calendar date regardless. Always file before the deadline rather than relying on the next-business-day rule.

### Important Note on State Laws vs. ERISA

State timely filing laws apply ONLY to fully-insured health plans. Self-funded (ERISA) plans are governed by federal law and the plan document terms. State timely filing laws do NOT apply to:

- Self-funded employer health plans (most large employers)
- Multi-employer welfare arrangements (MEWAs)
- Church plans
- Government plans (federal, state, local)
- Medicare Advantage plans (CMS regulation)
- Medicaid (state-specific regulation)

To determine if a plan is fully-insured or self-funded, look at:
1. The plan document (Summary Plan Description — SPD)
2. The member ID card (self-funded plans often have different card designs)
3. The claims address (self-funded plans often use a TPA's address)
4. Contact member services

## 5. Proof of Timely Filing

### Types of Acceptable Proof

When a claim is denied for timely filing (CO-29), the provider must prove the claim was submitted within the deadline. The following types of evidence are accepted:

| Evidence Type | Strength | Description |
|---------------|----------|-------------|
| **277CA Claim Status Response** | Strong | Electronic acknowledgment showing the payer's received date |
| **997 Functional Acknowledgment** | Moderate | Confirms file-level receipt (not claim-level) |
| **Clearinghouse Submission Log** | Strong | Date-stamped log from the clearinghouse showing when submitted |
| **Payer Portal Submission Confirmation** | Strong | Screenshot or PDF from the payer's provider portal |
| **Fax Confirmation Page** | Moderate | Date-stamped fax confirmation from the payer's fax number |
| **Certified Mail Receipt** | Strong | USPS return receipt showing delivery date |
| **TA1 Interchange Acknowledgment** | Moderate | Confirms EDI interchange was accepted |
| **Billing System Submission Report** | Moderate | Date-stamped report from practice management system |
| **Payer Phone Log** | Weak | Notes from a phone call; no independent verification |
| **Payer Correspondence** | Moderate | Any written acknowledgment from the payer about the claim |

### How to Obtain Proof of Timely Filing

**From a clearinghouse:**
- Most clearinghouses (Change Healthcare/Optum, Availity, SSI, Waystar, ZirMed) maintain detailed submission logs
- Logs include submission date, time, batch number, and claim IDs
- Logs are typically available for 12-24 months
- Some clearinghouses provide a "certified submission report" upon request

**From a payer portal:**
- Most payer portals show the "date received" or "date of service" in the claim status
- Take a screenshot immediately after submission
- Some portals allow you to download a claim status report

**From an EDI system:**
- The 997 and 277CA are generated automatically by the payer's system
- Configure your practice management system to store these acknowledgments
- If you did not store them, request them from the clearinghouse or payer

### How to Submit Proof of Timely Filing in an Appeal

1. **Write a cover letter** explaining what happened:
   - Date the claim was submitted
   - Method of submission
   - Any acknowledgment or confirmation received
   - Why the payer may have missed the timely filing

2. **Attach the proof**: Include the submission log, fax confirmation, or portal screenshot

3. **Be specific**: Do not say "the claim was submitted on time" — provide the exact date, time, and method of submission

4. **Request reprocessing**: Ask the payer to reprocess the claim as timely filed

5. **Include tracking information**: For mailed appeals, use certified mail with return receipt

## 6. Good Cause Exceptions

### Medicare Good Cause Extensions

CMS permits good cause exceptions to the timely filing deadline in specific circumstances. The provider must submit a written request explaining the circumstances and providing supporting documentation.

**When good cause is recognized:**

| Circumstance | Required Documentation |
|-------------|----------------------|
| **Catastrophic illness/death of provider or immediate family** | Death certificate, medical records, obituary |
| **Natural disaster (hurricane, earthquake, flood, fire, wildfire)** | FEMA declaration, news reports, insurance claims |
| **Destruction of records (fire, flood, other casualty)** | Insurance claim, fire department report |
| **Provider incapacitation (physical or mental)** | Medical records, physician statement |
| **Substantial clerical error by the provider** | Documentation of the specific error and correction |
| **CMS/MAC instruction error or misinformation** | Copies of the incorrect instructions, correspondence with MAC |
| **System failure (PMS, clearinghouse, or MAC system failure)** | System logs, IT documentation, clearinghouse correspondence |
| **Delay due to government shutdown or public health emergency** | Official government announcements |

**How to request a CMS good cause extension:**

1. Prepare a written statement explaining the specific circumstances that caused the late filing
2. Attach supporting documentation (as listed above)
3. Submit to the MAC along with the claim or appeal
4. The MAC will review and either grant or deny the good cause request
5. If denied, the MAC will explain the reason
6. The good cause denial can be appealed through the standard Medicare appeal process

**Sample Good Cause Request Letter:**

```
[DATE]

[MAC Name and Address]
Attn: Appeals Department
[Address]

RE: Good Cause Exception Request — Timely Filing
Claim Number: [XXX]
Patient Name: [XXX]
Date of Service: [XXX]

To Whom It May Concern:

I am requesting a good cause extension for the late filing of the above-referenced 
claim. The claim was originally due on [deadline date] but was filed on [filing date].

The reason for the late filing is as follows:
[Detailed explanation of the circumstances causing the delay.]

Supporting documentation is attached:
1. [Document 1 describing the event]
2. [Document 2 providing additional evidence]

Based on the circumstances described above, I respectfully request a good cause 
extension under 42 CFR § 424.44 and CMS Manual System guidance.

Sincerely,

[Provider Name, Credentials]
[Contact Information]
```

### Commercial Payer Good Cause

Commercial payers generally do NOT have a formal good cause exception process. However:

1. **Some payers may grant exceptions** on a case-by-case basis for extraordinary circumstances
2. **State insurance departments** may intervene if the payer's timely filing policy is unreasonable
3. **The provider contract** may outline exceptions — review the contract carefully
4. **Network termination threats** may be effective for large providers with significant market leverage
5. **Legal action** is rarely cost-effective for timely filing disputes

## 7. Timely Filing Prevention Strategies

### Automated Tracking System Requirements

An effective timely filing tracking system should:

1. **Capture payer-specific deadlines**: Store each payer's filing window (in days) and appeal window
2. **Calculate deadlines automatically**: From date of service, date of discharge, or remittance date
3. **Send automated alerts**: At 60, 30, 14, and 7 days before each deadline
4. **Track filing status**: Submitted, accepted, pended, denied, paid
5. **Track acknowledgments**: 997 received, 277CA received, payer portal confirmation
6. **Track secondary claims**: Set deadlines from primary payment/denial date
7. **Track appeals**: Set deadlines from remittance date for each claim

### Best Practices for Timely Filing

**Daily:**
- Submit claims daily (same day as service or within 24-48 hours)
- Check clearinghouse for claim acceptance/transmission status
- Review and resolve rejected claims within 24 hours

**Weekly:**
- Run a claims aging report by payer
- Identify claims approaching 60 days from DOS
- Identify claims with "accepted but not paid" status over 30 days
- Review clearinghouse reports for stuck or pended claims

**Monthly:**
- Run a timely filing risk report showing all claims within 30 days of deadline
- Review claims over 90 days from DOS for high-risk payers
- Reconcile claim submission logs with practice management system reports
- Follow up on all claims approaching the deadline

**Quarterly:**
- Review all payer timely filing policies for changes
- Update the payer deadline database
- Audit timely filing denial rates as a percentage of total denials
- Implement process improvements for payers with high timely filing denial rates

## 8. Timely Filing Denial Appeal Examples

### Example 1: Claim Submitted on Time But Denied

**Facts:**
- DOS: January 15, 2025
- UHC (180-day deadline): Due by July 14, 2025
- Claim submitted: January 20, 2025 (via clearinghouse)
- Claim denied: August 1, 2025 (CO-29 — timely filing)

**Action:**
1. Retrieve clearinghouse submission log showing submission on January 20, 2025
2. Retrieve 277CA showing payer received date of January 22, 2025
3. Write an appeal letter explaining that the claim was submitted well within the 180-day window
4. Attach proof of submission
5. Appeal as a CO-29 denial with proof of timely filing

### Example 2: Claim Filed Late — Good Cause Exception

**Facts:**
- DOS: March 1, 2025
- Medicare (12-month deadline): Due by March 1, 2026
- Claim submitted: April 15, 2026 (13.5 months after DOS)
- Reason for delay: Hurricane destroyed the provider's office in September 2025; all records were lost; practice management system was offline for 4 months

**Action:**
1. Request good cause exception under CMS rules
2. Submit FEMA declaration for the hurricane
3. Submit insurance claim for property damage
4. Submit proof of system restoration timeline
5. Reconstruct medical records from EHR backups
6. Submit the good cause request with the claim to the MAC

### Example 3: Secondary Claim Filed After Primary EOB

**Facts:**
- DOS: January 15, 2025 (Medicare primary, BCBS secondary)
- Medicare paid: March 1, 2025

## Agent Q&A Pairs for Training

**Q: What is the timely filing deadline for Medicare Part A and Part B claims?**
**A:** Medicare Part A and Part B claims must be filed within 12 calendar months (365 days) from the date of service. For inpatient claims, the clock runs from the date of discharge. This is a federal requirement established by 42 CFR § 424.44. Medicare has the most generous timely filing window of any major payer. The claim must be RECEIVED by the Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC), not just mailed or submitted to a clearinghouse. If the claim is submitted to the clearinghouse on day 364 but the MAC receives it on day 366, the claim may be denied as untimely. However, for electronic claims, the date the clearinghouse receives the claim is generally considered the date of receipt. Medicare Secondary Payer (MSP) claims have a separate timeline of 3 years from the date of service for conditional payment claims.

**Q: What does CARC CO-29 mean?**
**A:** CARC CO-29 means "The time limit for filing has expired." This is the standard code used by all payers to indicate a claim was submitted after the timely filing deadline. The adjustment group is CO (Contractual Obligation), meaning the adjustment is considered a contractual matter. The dollar amount in the CAS segment will be the full billed amount (or the amount that should have been paid). CO-29 denials are strict — the payer does not consider the clinical validity of the claim. However, CO-29 denials CAN be appealed if: (1) the provider can prove the claim was actually submitted on time, (2) a good cause exception applies (Medicare only), or (3) the payer's calculation of the deadline was incorrect. A CO-29 denial does not necessarily mean the end of the road — gather your proof and appeal.

**Q: How do I calculate the filing deadline for a claim with dates of service from January 5-10, 2025, for UnitedHealthcare?**
**A:** UHC has a 180-day filing window from date of service. For a single DOS, the calculation is straightforward: DOS + 180 days = deadline. For a range of dates (January 5-10, 2025), most payers use the LAST date of service (January 10, 2025) as the starting point. So: January 10, 2025 + 180 days = July 9, 2025. Alternatively, some payers use the FROM date (January 5). Always check the specific payer's policy. For inpatient claims, use the date of discharge. For recurring claims (home health, DME), use each individual date of service. Calendar the deadline in your system and set alerts at 60, 30, 14, and 7 days before the deadline. File well before the deadline — do not wait until the last day.

**Q: What proof of timely filing should I keep?**
**A:** The best proof of timely filing is: (1) 277CA (Health Care Claim Acknowledgment) — shows the specific claim was received by the payer and the date of receipt. This is the strongest claim-level evidence. (2) 997 Functional Acknowledgment — confirms the electronic file was received, but only at the file level, not claim level. (3) Clearinghouse submission log — date-stamped report from the clearinghouse showing when the claim was submitted and forwarded to the payer. (4) Payer portal submission confirmation — screenshot or PDF from the payer's online portal showing the submission date. (5) Fax confirmation page — with date, time, and recipient fax number (only for claims submitted by fax). (6) Certified mail receipt — USPS return receipt with date stamp (only for paper claims). (7) Billing system submission logs — reports from your practice management system showing when claims were created and transmitted. Keep all proof for at least 24 months (longer for Medicare) in case of a late-appearing denial.

**Q: Can I appeal a CO-29 timely filing denial if I have proof the claim was submitted on time?**
**A:** Yes, absolutely. If you have proof that the claim was submitted within the timely filing window, file an appeal immediately. The strategy: (1) Gather your strongest proof of timely submission (277CA is best, clearinghouse log is good, fax confirmation is acceptable). (2) Write a clear appeal letter explaining that the claim was submitted on [date] which is within the [X]-day filing window. (3) Attach the proof of submission. (4) Request the payer reprocess the claim based on timely filing. (5) If the payer still denies, escalate to Level 2 with additional proof. (6) For Medicare, include a timeline of submission events. Many CO-29 denials are overturned when proof of timely submission is provided — the denial may have been issued automatically or due to a processing error in the payer's system. Do not accept a CO-29 denial without verifying the submission records.

**Q: What is a good cause exception for Medicare timely filing and how do I request one?**
**A:** A good cause exception allows Medicare to accept a claim filed after the 12-month deadline when circumstances beyond the provider's control caused the delay. CMS recognizes good cause for: (1) catastrophic illness or death of the provider or immediate family member, (2) natural disaster (hurricane, flood, fire, earthquake), (3) destruction of business records due to fire or flood, (4) provider incapacitation due to physical or mental condition, (5) substantial clerical error by the provider, (6) CMS or MAC instruction error or misdirection, (7) system failure (PMS, clearinghouse, or MAC system). To request: (1) Write a letter explaining the specific circumstances. (2) Attach supporting documentation (FEMA declaration, death certificate, medical records, insurance claim, etc.). (3) Submit to the MAC with the late claim. (4) The MAC reviews the request and decides. If granted, the claim is processed normally. If denied, the denial can be appealed through the standard Medicare appeals process.

**Q: Do state timely filing laws apply to self-funded ERISA plans?**
**A:** No. State timely filing laws do NOT apply to self-funded (ERISA) health plans. Self-funded plans are governed by the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA), which preempts state insurance laws. For self-funded plans, the timely filing deadline is determined by the plan document (Summary Plan Description). To determine if a plan is self-funded: (1) Check the plan document — if it says "self-insured" or "self-funded," state laws do not apply. (2) Check the member ID card — self-funded plans often reference a TPA (Third Party Administrator) rather than an insurance company. (3) Ask the plan administrator. (4) Look for a "Plan Sponsor" name rather than an insurance company name on correspondence. If the plan IS self-funded, the timely filing deadline in the plan document controls, regardless of state law.

**Q: What is the timely filing deadline for California (Medi-Cal) claims?**
**A:** Medi-Cal (California Medicaid) has different timelines depending on the provider type: (1) Institutional claims (hospitals, nursing facilities, clinics): 365 days (12 months) from the date of service or date of discharge. (2) Professional claims (physicians, specialists): 180 days (6 months) from the date of service. (3) Dental claims: Varies by service type. (4) Pharmacy claims: 90 days from the date of service. (5) Medicare crossover claims: 12 months from the Medicare EOB date. (6) Appeal filing: 90 days from the date of the denial notice for a state fair hearing. Medi-Cal uses the "timely filing date" in their Provider Manual — always check the most current manual. Late claims may be accepted with a written justification, but Medi-Cal is strict about the deadline for most claim types.

**Q: What is the 277CA and why is it important for proving timely filing?**
**A:** The 277CA (Health Care Claim Acknowledgment) is an electronic X12 transaction sent by the payer or clearinghouse that provides the status of each individual claim in a submission batch. It shows: (1) Individual claim-level status (Accepted, Rejected, or Pending), (2) The date the claim was received by the payer, (3) The payer's claim control number, (4) Specific error codes for rejected claims. The 277CA is critical for proving timely filing because it provides claim-level proof that the SPECIFIC claim was received on a SPECIFIC date by the payer. Unlike the 997 (which only confirms file-level receipt), the 277CA confirms individual claim receipt. If you receive a CO-29 denial and have a 277CA showing the claim was received by the payer on a date within the filing window, you have very strong evidence for the appeal. Always configure your practice management system to store 277CA data.

**Q: What is the difference between date of receipt and date of submission for timely filing?**
**A:** The distinction is critical: Date of Submission is when the provider or billing service transmits the claim to a clearinghouse, fax machine, or postal service. Date of Receipt is when the PAYER actually receives the claim. The timely filing clock is measured from the DATE OF RECEIPT by the payer, not the date of submission. For electronic claims: The date the clearinghouse transmits the claim to the payer is typically considered the receipt date. For paper claims: The date the payer receives and processes the claim (which may be different from the date it was mailed). For faxed claims: The date on the fax confirmation (when the payer's fax machine received it). The gap between submission and receipt can be 1-10 days or more, depending on the submission method and processing time. Best practice: Submit claims as early as possible, at least 30 days before the deadline, to account for transmission and processing delays.

**Q: How do I handle a timely filing denial for a secondary claim?**
**A:** Secondary claims have unique timely filing challenges. Options: (1) If the secondary payer's deadline is based on the date of service and it has been exceeded, check if the deadline can be calculated from the primary payer's EOB date instead — some payers allow this. (2) File the secondary claim immediately upon receiving the primary payer's EOB/RA — do not delay. (3) If the secondary claim was denied for timely filing and you have proof it was submitted within the secondary payer's deadline, appeal with the proof. (4) If the secondary claim is truly late, contact the secondary payer about their process for late secondary claims — some have different rules for secondary submissions. (5) For Medicare as secondary (MSP), conditional payment claims have a 3-year filing window — use this if applicable. (6) Check state law — some states require secondary claims to be accepted within a certain period of the primary payment. (7) For secondary claims filed late, you may need to write off the balance or bill the patient.

**Q: My claim was denied 14 months after submission. The payer says it is past the timely filing deadline. What do I do?**
**A:** This situation is unusual — the payer should have processed the claim before 14 months if it was submitted on time. First, verify the payer received the claim by checking your submission logs and acknowledgments. If the claim was submitted within the timely filing window but the payer did not process it for 14 months, the timely filing denial should NOT stand because the submission was timely. Steps: (1) Gather proof of timely submission (277CA, clearinghouse log, etc.). (2) Write an appeal stating the claim was SUBMITTED on [date] within the [X]-day filing window — the payer's failure to process it promptly does not change the submission date. (3) Request the payer waive the timely filing and process the claim. (4) If the payer still denies, escalate to Level 2. (5) If the claim was submitted to a clearinghouse and the clearinghouse did not forward it to the payer for 14 months, the clearinghouse may be responsible — contact your clearinghouse representative. (6) In extreme cases, involve the state insurance department.

**Q: What is the timely filing deadline for Workers' Compensation claims?**
**A:** Workers' Compensation timely filing rules are entirely state-specific. There is no national standard. General ranges: (1) Medical bill filing: typically 30 days to 12 months from date of service, depending on the state. (2) Claim filing (first report of injury): typically 30-90 days from the date of injury. (3) UR (Utilization Review) appeals: typically 20-60 days from the UR denial. (4) Request for authorization (RFA): state-specific timelines. Examples: California has 12 months from DOS for medical bills. New York has 18 months for medical bills. Texas has 180 days for medical bills. Florida requires electronic submission within 30 days. Check each state's Workers' Compensation division website for specific rules. Workers' Comp deadlines are strict — missing them can permanently bar payment. Always verify the specific state's requirements, as the rules differ significantly from commercial insurance.

**Q: How do state timely filing laws affect my claims?**
**A:** State timely filing laws establish the MINIMUM filing period that health insurers must offer. If an insurer's contract says 90 days but state law mandates 180 days, the state law controls (for fully-insured plans). Key points: (1) If the state law is MORE GENEROUS than the payer's stated deadline, cite the state law in your appeal. (2) If the state law matches or is less generous than the payer's stated deadline, the payer's deadline applies. (3) State laws only apply to FULLY-INSURED plans — they do not apply to self-funded ERISA plans. (4) State laws may also mandate how the deadline is calculated (e.g., from date of service vs. date of discharge). (5) If the payer's deadline is shorter than the state minimum, file an appeal citing the state law and request the claim be processed within the state-mandated period. (6) Some states have specific enforcement mechanisms — you may file a complaint with the state insurance department.

**Q: What is the timely filing process for corrected claims (frequency code 7)?**
**A:** Corrected claims (frequency code 7 on the 837 transaction, or a revised CMS-1500/UB-04 with "CORRECTED" marked) must generally be filed within the SAME timely filing window as the original claim. However: (1) For Medicare: Corrected claims must be filed within 12 months of the date of service (the original timely filing window). (2) For commercial payers: Most follow the same rule — the corrected claim must be within the original timely filing window. (3) Some commercial payers have a SEPARATE corrected claim window that starts from the original claim processing date (e.g., "file corrected claims within 180 days of the original adjudication"). (4) If the original denial was not timely filing, filing a corrected claim may be an alternative to an appeal — but ONLY for coding/billing errors, not for clinical denials. (5) Always check the specific payer's corrected claim policy. (6) Important: A corrected claim is NOT the same as an appeal — it resubmits the claim with corrections, it does not challenge the original decision. Use the right approach for the right situation.

**Q: What should I do if my clearinghouse reports the claim as "accepted" but the payer says they never received it?**
**A:** This is a disputed receipt scenario. Steps: (1) First, check the 277CA from the payer (not the clearinghouse) — if the 277CA shows the claim was received, you have proof the payer received it. (2) If you do not have a 277CA, request a claim status inquiry (276 transaction) or check the payer portal. (3) If the payer truly has no record of the claim, ask your clearinghouse for the transmission log showing the claim was forwarded to the payer's electronic mailbox. (4) Ask the clearinghouse to "retransmit" the claim — they can often do this and the payer's system may find it on the second transmission. (5) If the clearinghouse can confirm timely transmission but the payer still denies receipt, file an appeal with the clearinghouse's transmission log as proof of timely submission. (6) In some cases, the claim may be stuck in the payer's electronic intake queue and simply needs to be located. Call the payer's EDI department (not claims department) for assistance. (7) As a last resort, resubmit the claim if still within the timely filing window.

**Q: How do I track timely filing deadlines across multiple payers?**
**A:** Create a payer-specific deadline tracking system: (1) Maintain a database (spreadsheet or within your practice management system) with each payer's: filing window in days, appeal window in days, whether the deadline is from DOS or discharge date, and any state-specific requirements. (2) Set automated alerts in your billing system: at 60 days before deadline (yellow flag), at 30 days (orange flag), at 14 days (red flag), at 7 days (critical). (3) Run weekly aging reports sorted by payer and DOS — identify claims approaching each payer's deadline. (4) For secondary claims: set a separate tracking system from the primary payer's EOB date. (5) For appeals: set tracking from the remittance/denial date. (6) At least monthly, review all payer policies for updates — filing windows can change. (7) Use a clearinghouse that provides automated timely filing alerts. (8) For high-volume payers (Medicare, UHC, BCBS), set up specific workflows with dedicated staff responsible for timely filing monitoring.

**Q: Can I bill the patient if a claim is denied for timely filing?**
**A:** Generally, yes — if a claim is denied because the provider did not submit it on time, the patient is not responsible for the provider's administrative error. However: (1) If the patient was responsible for submitting the claim (out-of-network provider, patient handled their own insurance), you can bill the patient for timely filing denials. (2) If the provider was responsible for submitting the claim, most payer contracts and state laws prohibit balance billing the patient for timely filing denials. (3) For Medicare: If no valid ABN was obtained, you CANNOT bill the beneficiary for a timely filing denial — the provider must write off the charges. (4) For commercial payers: Check your provider contract — most contracts prohibit balance billing for any denial, including timely filing. (5) Some states have specific laws protecting patients from balance billing for administrative denials. (6) The safest approach: Write off timely filing denials as a cost of doing business — the amount is typically not worth the patient relationship damage or regulatory risk.

**Q: What are the appeal deadlines for Medicare?**
**A:** Medicare appeal deadlines: Level 1 (Redetermination by MAC): 120 calendar days from the date of the remittance notice. Level 2 (Reconsideration by QIC): 180 calendar days from the date of the Level 1 decision. Level 3 (ALJ Hearing at OMHA): 60 calendar days from the date of the Level 2 decision. Level 4 (Medicare Appeals Council): 60 calendar days from the date of the Level 3 (ALJ) decision. Level 5 (Federal District Court): 60 calendar days from the date of the Level 4 (Appeals Council) decision. All deadlines are calendar days, not business days. If the deadline falls on a weekend or holiday, it typically extends to the next business day. File appeals well before the deadline — do not wait until the last day. For commercial payers, appeal deadlines are typically 180 days from the denial date at all levels, but always verify with the specific payer.

**Q: What is the timely filing policy for BCBS Federal Employee Program (FEP)?**
**A:** BCBS FEP (Federal Employee Program) has specific timely filing rules: (1) Standard claims: 365 days (12 months) from date of service. (2) Inpatient claims: 365 days from date of discharge. (3) Out-of-area claims: 365 days from DOS. (4) Corrected claims: Within 12 months of original claim processing. (5) Appeal deadline: 180 days from the date of the denial/remittance. (6) FEP is a national program but is administered locally by BCBS plans — the timely filing policy is national. FEP follows the BCBS Association's standard timely filing rules. FEP does not typically grant good cause exceptions for late filing. The 365-day window for FEP is one of the most generous among commercial payers. FEP claims must include the correct FEP member ID and group number — incorrect ID may result in a processing delay that pushes the claim toward the deadline.

**Q: What is a "technical rejection" vs. a "timely filing denial"?**
**A:** A technical rejection (not a denial) occurs when a claim is submitted with data errors that prevent the payer's system from processing it — the claim is returned (rejected) without being entered into the adjudication system. The claim has NOT been adjudicated and the timely filing clock continues to run. A timely filing denial (CO-29) occurs when the claim was technically processed (entered into the system) but the adjudication determined the claim was submitted too late. The key difference: A technical rejection can be corrected and resubmitted within the remaining timely filing window. A timely filing denial means the claim was processed but determined to be late. When you receive a technical rejection: (1) Fix the data error immediately, (2) Resubmit the claim within 24-48 hours, (3) Track the original timely filing deadline — the clock did not reset with the rejection, (4) If the resubmission is rejected again, you are still within the original timely filing window. Do NOT treat rejections as denials — they are fixable data entry issues.

**Q: What is the timely filing deadline for Cigna claims?**
**A:** Cigna commercial claims must be initially submitted within 180 calendar days from the date of service. For inpatient claims, the 180-day window runs from the date of discharge. Cigna corrected/replacement claims must be submitted within 12 months from the date of service (note: this is more generous than the initial 180-day window for replacements). Cigna's appeal deadline is also 180 days from the denial notice. Cigna participates in CAQH CORE rules. Special considerations: Cigna + Oscar joint venture plans may have different rules; Cigna LocalPlus network may have separate requirements; Cigna self-funded (ASO) plans follow the plan document, not Cigna's standard policy.

**Q: What is the timely filing deadline for Aetna claims?**
**A:** Aetna commercial claims must be initially submitted within 180 calendar days from the date of service. For inpatient claims, the window runs from the date of discharge. Aetna corrected claims must be submitted within 180 days from the date of service. Aetna's appeal deadline is 180 days from the date of the denial. Aetna participates in CAQH CORE rules, which means they must accept electronic claims for at least 12 months if the claim was initially submitted within the timely filing window (this applies to resubmissions/corrections). For Aetna Better Health (Medicaid), deadlines vary by state (typically 90-365 days). For Aetna Medicare Advantage, follow Medicare Part C rules. Aetna also offers a provider dispute process for contractual issues separate from the clinical appeal process.

**Q: What is the timely filing deadline for Humana claims?**
**A:** Humana commercial claims must be initially submitted within 180 calendar days from the date of service. Corrected claims must also be within 180 days from the date of service. Humana's appeal deadline is 180 days from the remittance date. For Humana Medicare Advantage, follow Medicare Part C rules (typically 365 days from DOS). Humana Medicaid deadlines vary by state. Humana participates in CAQH CORE rules for electronic claims. Use Humana SmartSummary (portal) to track claim submission status and timely filing. Special note: Humana Military (Tricare East) is a separate line of business with separate timely filing rules following Tricare's 1-year window.

**Q: What is the timely filing deadline for BCBS of Texas claims?**
**A:** BCBS of Texas commercial claims must be initially submitted within 180 calendar days from the date of service. This is the same as most other BCBS plans, but shorter than some (365 days). The appeal window for BCBS of Texas is 180 days from the date of denial. Corrected claims must be submitted within the original 180-day filing window. BCBS of Texas participates in the Texas Department of Insurance prompt pay laws, which require payment within 45 days for clean electronic claims — if the claim was submitted on time but not paid within 45 days, you may be entitled to interest. BCBS of Texas also offers external review through the Texas Department of Insurance after internal appeals are exhausted. Important: BCBS of Texas and BCBS of Texas Medicare Advantage (BCBSTX Medicare) have different rules — MA follows Medicare Part C timelines.

**Q: What is the impact of a patient losing eligibility on timely filing?**
**A:** Retroactive eligibility loss (when a patient loses coverage retroactively, meaning they were never actually covered on the date of service) is different from a timely filing issue. When a patient loses eligibility retroactively: (1) The payer will recoup any payment already made on the claim. (2) The provider can then bill the patient for the full charges, since the patient was not covered at the time of service. (3) This is not a timely filing issue — it is an eligibility issue. However, sometimes a claim is denied both for timely filing AND for eligibility. In those cases: (1) Resolve the eligibility issue first (verify the patient's coverage on DOS). (2) If the patient WAS eligible, submit proof of eligibility. (3) If the patient was NOT eligible, the provider should bill the patient — the timely filing issue is moot because the claim was never covered. (4) If the patient was eligible but the claim is denied as untimely, appeal with proof of timely submission. The two issues should be handled separately. Always verify eligibility before the date of service to avoid this complication.

**Q: What happens when a payer mergers or acquisitions affect timely filing rules?**
**A:** When payers merge or acquire other payers (e.g., Aetna/CVS, Cigna/Express Scripts, Anthem/Elevance rebranding), timely filing rules may change during the transition: (1) During the transition, claims may be processed by either the old or new system, causing confusion about deadlines. (2) Payers typically issue transition guidance — stay subscribed to payer newsletters for updates. (3) If a claim is denied for timely filing during a transition period, appeal with documentation of when the claim was submitted — the payer's system migration may have caused a processing delay. (4) Some states require payers to honor pre-merger timely filing windows during transition periods. (5) If a payer's timely filing policy changes due to a merger, the change must typically be communicated to providers in writing 30-60 days in advance. (6) For claims submitted just before a merger, it is safest to assume the more generous of the two payers' deadlines applies. (7) Document everything during transition periods — system issues are common and denials are frequently overturned.

**Q: How do I verify the timely filing deadline for a specific patient and plan?**
**A:** To verify the specific timely filing deadline for a patient: (1) Check the member ID card — some cards list the timely filing deadline on the back. (2) Call the payer's provider services line and ask: "What is the timely filing deadline for this specific plan for date of service [DOS]? Is it based on the date of service or date of discharge?" (3) Check the payer's provider manual — most payers publish timely filing policies online. (4) If the plan is self-funded (ERISA), request a copy of the Summary Plan Description (SPD) from the employer or plan administrator — the SPD will state the timely filing rules. (5) Check state law for fully-insured plans — state law establishes the minimum filing window. (6) For secondary claims: ask if the deadline is from DOS or from the primary payer's EOB date. (7) Document the date you verified the deadline, who you spoke with, and what they said. The most common error is assuming all plans from a single payer have the same deadline — they do not. Different lines of business (HMO vs PPO vs Medicare) often have different deadlines, and self-funded plans can set their own deadlines entirely.

**Q: What is the timely filing window for VA Community Care claims?**
**A:** VA Community Care (formerly "fee basis" or "Choice") claims have specific timely filing rules: (1) Professional claims (physician services): 180 days from date of service. (2) Institutional claims (hospital, outpatient facilities): 365 days from date of service. (3) Corrected claims: must be submitted within the original timely filing window. (4) Appeals: 60 days from the date of the denial or payment determination. (5) The VA uses the VA Fee Schedule, not Medicare rates (though they are often similar). All VA Community Care services require prior authorization — if the claim is denied for timely filing but the authorization was obtained, the timely filing issue is separate from the authorization. The VA processes claims through its regional claims processing contractors (e.g., Optum, PGBA). Late claims may be considered if the delay was caused by the VA or its contractor. VA Community Care timely filing is strictly enforced — missing the deadline usually results in a complete write-off.

**Q: How do I handle a timely filing situation where the patient had multiple periods of coverage?**
**A:** When a patient has multiple periods of eligibility within a single time frame (e.g., coverage gap, re-enrollment, plan change mid-year), the timely filing rules can be complex: (1) Claims for dates during a coverage gap: the claim can only be paid if the patient had retroactive reinstatement. If no retro reinstatement, the claim is patient responsibility. (2) Claims for dates during the first coverage period: file within the standard timely filing window from DOS. (3) Claims for dates during the second coverage period: file within the standard window from those DOS. (4) If the patient changed plans within the same payer (e.g., switched from PPO to HMO mid-year): each plan may have different timely filing deadlines. File separately under the correct plan. (5) If the patient had a short coverage gap (e.g., 2 weeks) and was retroactively reinstated: the timely filing clock runs from the original DOS — retro reinstatement does not reset the clock. (6) Track each coverage period separately in your billing system. (7) Always verify eligibility before filing appeals to confirm the patient was covered on the specific DOS under the correct plan.

**Q: What is the timely filing deadline for out-of-state Medicaid claims?**
**A:** Out-of-state (non-resident) Medicaid claims have different timely filing rules: (1) California (Medi-Cal): out-of-state providers have 90 days from DOS (shorter than 365 days for in-state). (2) New York: out-of-state providers follow the same 365-day rule but must use the correct billing process. (3) Texas: out-of-state providers have 95 days (Texas-specific). (4) Florida: out-of-state providers have 180 days. (5) Most other states: out-of-state providers typically have shorter windows (90-180 days) than in-state (365 days). (6) Out-of-state providers must be enrolled in the state's Medicaid program or be a Medicare-participating provider to bill. (7) Some states require out-of-state providers to submit claims through a specific clearinghouse or portal. (8) Out-of-state providers should always verify the specific state's rules before providing services — the timely filing window may be significantly shorter than expected. The general rule: out-of-state Medicaid deadlines are shorter and stricter than in-state deadlines.

**Q: What is the timely filing deadline for auto insurance / PIP (Personal Injury Protection) claims?**
**A:** PIP (Personal Injury Protection) claims from auto insurance have state-specific timely filing rules: (1) Florida PIP: claims must be submitted within 30 days of the date of service (electronic) or 5 business days (paper). This is the shortest of any major payer. (2) New York No-Fault: claims must be submitted within 45 days of the date of service (or 30 days for the initial claim). (3) Michigan PIP: claims must be submitted within 1 year of the date of service. (4) Texas PIP: claims must be submitted within 6 months of the date of service. (5) California PIP: claims follow the 180-day standard. (6) Most other states: 30-180 days from DOS, varying widely. PIP deadlines are extremely short compared to health insurance deadlines. Medical providers billing auto insurance must have dedicated workflows to meet these tight deadlines. Missing a PIP deadline often results in a permanent denial with no appeal rights — the no-fault carrier is not required to pay late-submitted medical bills. Always verify the specific state's PIP statute for exact deadlines.

**Q: What is the timely filing appeal process for a claim that was denied for timely filing but the payer made a processing error?**
**A:** If a claim was denied for timely filing due to a payer processing error (e.g., payer's system rejected the claim and it was not actually processed, or payer's system recorded the wrong receipt date): (1) Gather evidence of the processing error: 277CA showing "Accepted" status, clearinghouse logs with correct transmission date, or payer portal screenshots showing the claim was in process. (2) Write an appeal stating: "This claim was submitted on [date] within the [X]-day timely filing window. The CO-29 denial appears to be a processing error — the attached 277CA shows the claim was accepted on [date]. Please reprocess."
