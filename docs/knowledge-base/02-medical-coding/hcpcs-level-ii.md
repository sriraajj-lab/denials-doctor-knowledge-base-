# HCPCS Level II: Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System

## Overview

HCPCS (Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System) Level II codes are alphanumeric codes used primarily to identify products, supplies, and services not included in the CPT code set. They are maintained by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) and are required for Medicare, Medicaid, and many private payer claims.

- **Structure**: Single alphabetic letter followed by 4 numeric digits (A0000-V9999)
- **Purpose**: Identifies items and services not covered by CPT (drugs, DME, supplies, ambulance, etc.)
- **Updates**: CMS publishes quarterly updates
- **Jurisdiction**: CMS maintains HCPCS Level II; the AMA maintains CPT (referred to as HCPCS Level I)

## HCPCS Modifiers

### KX Modifier (DME Medical Necessity)

The KX modifier indicates that the supplier has documentation on file that meets Medicare's medical necessity criteria for the item billed. It is primarily used with DMEPOS (Durable Medical Equipment, Prosthetics, Orthotics, and Supplies).

**Use**: Append KX to the HCPCS code when you have documented:
- Face-to-face examination
- Written order from treating physician
- Medical necessity criteria met (e.g., for CPAP, AHI > 15 documented on sleep study)

### GA/GY/GZ Modifiers (ABN-Related)

| Modifier | Meaning | Use |
|----------|---------|-----|
| GA | ABN on file | Waiver of liability issued to beneficiary for expected denial |
| GY | Excluded by law | Item or service statutorily excluded from Medicare coverage |
| GZ | Expected denial | Item/service expected to be denied as not reasonable/necessary, no ABN on file |

**Example**: If a beneficiary is receiving a service that Medicare likely will not cover (e.g., cosmetic surgery), use GY. If the service may be denied as not medically necessary but the patient signed an ABN, use GA.

### QW Modifier (CLIA-Waived Test)

The QW modifier is appended to laboratory HCPCS codes (80000 series) to indicate the test is performed under a CLIA Certificate of Waiver. If a lab performs CLIA-waived tests (e.g., urine dipstick, glucose fingerstick, rapid strep), the QW modifier must be appended to the test code.

**Common QW codes**:
- 81002QW (Urinalysis, non-automated, without microscopy)
- 81025QW (Urine pregnancy test)
- 82270QW (Fecal occult blood test)
- 82962QW (Glucose, blood by device)
- 83036QW (Hemoglobin A1c)
- 85013QW (Blood count, spun microhematocrit)
- 85018QW (Hemoglobin by copper sulfate)
- 85651QW (Sedimentation rate)
- 87880QW (Streptococcus A test)
- 87804QW (Influenza test)

## Q&A Pairs

**Q:** What is the difference between a CPT code and a HCPCS Level II code?

**A:** CPT codes (HCPCS Level I) are maintained by the AMA and describe physician services, surgical procedures, and diagnostic tests. HCPCS Level II codes are maintained by CMS and describe products, supplies, drugs, DME, ambulance services, and temporary procedures not covered by CPT. In practice, physicians primarily use CPT for professional services and HCPCS Level II for injectable drugs and supplies. Hospitals use both extensively.

---

**Q:** A physician administers a vaccine (e.g., influenza). What HCPCS codes are needed?

**A:** Two codes are needed:
1. The **vaccine product code** (e.g., 90688 for influenza vaccine, 0.5 mL, preservative-free) -- CPT
2. The **administration code** (e.g., 90471 for immunization administration, one vaccine)

The vaccine product CPT codes (90476-90749) describe the vaccine itself; the administration codes (90460-90461, 90471-90474) describe the service of giving the vaccine. If the vaccine is purchased under a specific GAVI or VFC program, use the appropriate HCPCS code (e.g., G0008 for influenza vaccine administration).

---

**Q:** When should you use the KX modifier?

**A:** The KX modifier is used with DMEPOS claims to certify that the supplier has documented medical necessity on file. For example, with a CPAP device (E0601), the KX modifier indicates the supplier has:
- A written order from the treating physician
- A face-to-face clinical evaluation
- Documentation of a positive sleep study (AHI > 15)
- Signed and dated documentation

The KX modifier does NOT mean Medicare has pre-approved the item; it means the supplier attests they have met the requirements.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between the GA, GY, and GZ modifiers?

**A:** 
- **GA**: "Waiver of liability on file." The patient signed an Advanced Beneficiary Notice (ABN) accepting responsibility if Medicare denies the service as not medically necessary.
- **GY**: "Statutorily excluded." The item or service is excluded from Medicare coverage by law (e.g., cosmetic surgery, hearing aids, routine foot care). No ABN is needed because coverage is never available.
- **GZ**: "Expected denial." The service is expected to be denied as not reasonable and necessary, but NO ABN was obtained from the patient. The provider cannot bill the patient for a GZ-denied service.

---

**Q:** A patient needs a walker for mobility. What is the correct HCPCS code and what documentation is required?

**A:** The standard walker code is E0100 (Walker, standard, without wheels). If a wheeled walker is needed, use E0135 (Walker, folding, with wheels). Document:
1. Medical necessity (patient is at risk of falling)
2. Face-to-face encounter with the physician
3. Written order specifically for the walker
4. Duration of need (usually 5+ years for non-changed condition)

---

**Q:** How do you bill for an injectable drug administered in the office (e.g., Depo-Medrol 40mg)?

**A:** Use the appropriate J-code:
- **Drug code**: J1030 (Methylprednisolone acetate, 40mg)
- **Administration code**: 96372 (Therapeutic, prophylactic, or diagnostic injection, SC or IM)

Bill on separate lines. If the drug is from a multi-dose vial that is single-use in an individual patient, bill for the amount administered, not the amount wasted (unless the payer allows wastage billing with modifier JW).

---

**Q:** What is the difference between an E-code and a K-code for wheelchairs?

**A:** E-codes (E0100-E0199, E1050-E1298) are the standard Medicare DME codes for wheelchairs and mobility equipment. K-codes (K0001-K0108) are temporary codes specifically for wheelchair bases and frames. K0001-K0009 are the standard codes for manual wheelchair bases. K-codes were created to provide more specific coding for wheelchair features that E-codes did not cover. Eventually, K-codes are either incorporated into the E-code range or retired.

---

**Q:** A physician provides a Medicare Annual Wellness Visit (AWV). What G-codes should be used?

**A:** 
- **Initial AWV**: G0438 (Annual wellness visit, includes personalized prevention plan of service, initial visit)
- **Subsequent AWV**: G0439 (Annual wellness visit, subsequent visit)

The AWV is different from the Initial Preventive Physical Exam (IPPE or "Welcome to Medicare" visit), which is coded G0402. The AWV does NOT include a comprehensive physical exam but does include health risk assessment, screening schedule review, and advanced care planning discussion.

---

**Q:** A hospital administers IV Rocephin (ceftriaxone) 1 gram in the outpatient setting. What codes are used?

**A:** 
- J0696 (Ceftriaxone sodium, per 250mg) -- bill 4 units for 1 gram
- 96374 (Therapeutic, prophylactic, or diagnostic injection, IV push, single or initial substance/drug)
If hydration is also given, use 96360 (IV infusion, hydration, initial 31 minutes to 1 hour) plus 96361 for each additional hour.

---

**Q:** What is the C-code range used for?

**A:** C-codes (C0000-C9999) are temporary HCPCS codes used specifically for the Medicare Outpatient Prospective Payment System (OPPS). They are used by hospital outpatient departments to report items and services that are not separately payable under CPT. C-codes are NOT used by physician offices or professional fee billing. Examples: C8900 (CTA of coronary arteries), C9746 (Transcatheter intra-arterial infusion for cancer therapy).

---

**Q:** A patient needs a CPAP device for sleep apnea. What HCPCS code(s) and modifier(s) are needed?

**A:** 
- **Device**: E0601 (Continuous airway pressure [CPAP] device)
- **Modifier**: None for the device itself, but the claim must be accompanied by documentation of medical necessity
- **Supplies**: A7030 (Full face mask), A7034 (Nasal interface/mask), A7035 (Headgear), A7037 (Tubing)

After the first 3 months of rental, Medicare requires documentation of adherence (use of at least 4 hours/night for 70% of nights in a 30-day consecutive period).

---

**Q:** Can modifier 25 be used with HCPCS Level II codes?

**A:** Modifier 25 is a CPT modifier and is used with E/M codes (99202-99499). However, it can be applied to HCPCS G-codes that represent E/M services (e.g., G0438, G0439). For example, if a patient receives an AWV (G0439) and also has an acute problem addressed during the same visit, you would append modifier 25 to the G0439 (G0439-25). Check payer-specific policies.

---

**Q:** When billing for a wheelchair, what is the difference between a standard wheelchair (K0001) and a lightweight wheelchair (K0003)?

**A:** 
- **K0001**: Standard wheelchair, 36+ lbs, basic frame, fixed armrests, swing-away footrests, sling seat/back. For patients who are independent and have good upper body strength.
- **K0003**: Lightweight wheelchair, 30-36 lbs, lighter frame for easier self-propulsion. For patients who need to self-propel and have adequate strength but would benefit from reduced weight.

Documentation must show why a lighter wheelchair is medically necessary (e.g., patient cannot propel a standard weight chair due to weakness or cardiopulmonary limitation).

---

**Q:** A physician prescribes oxygen therapy for a patient with COPD. What codes are needed?

**A:** 
- **Oxygen concentrator**: E1390 (Oxygen concentrator, single delivery port) or E1392 (Portable oxygen concentrator)
- **Oxygen contents**: E0441 (Stationary oxygen contents, gaseous, 1 month) or E0442 (Stationary oxygen contents, liquid, 1 month)

**Q:** What are modifier Q5 and Q6 used for in HCPCS billing?

**A:** Both modifiers are used for **locum tenens** (substitute provider) arrangements:
- **Q5**: Service furnished by a substitute physician under a reciprocal billing arrangement (e.g., physicians taking turns covering each other's practices)
- **Q6**: Service furnished by a locum tenens physician (a paid substitute, not part of a reciprocal arrangement)

The regular physician bills for the service using their own NPI and appends the Q5 or Q6 modifier. The substitute physician does not bill for these services.

**Q:** What is the correct way to bill for a hearing aid under Medicare?

**A:** Hearing aids are **statutorily excluded** from Medicare coverage. Use the GY modifier (G-Y = statutorily excluded) to indicate the item is excluded by law. Even with GY, Medicare will not pay. However, the claim should be submitted to Medicare for tracking purposes and to allow Medigap/Medicare Advantage plans to process potential secondary benefits. For non-Medicare patients, S-codes or V-codes can be used.

**Q:** A patient needs custom-fabricated ankle-foot orthosis (AFO). What L-code should be used?

**A:** For a custom-fabricated AFO, the appropriate code depends on the specific design:
- **L1970**: Ankle-foot orthosis, plastic, custom-fabricated
- **L1990**: Ankle-foot orthosis, double upright, custom-fabricated
- **L1900**: AFO, supramalleolar, custom-fabricated

For prefabricated (off-the-shelf) AFOs, use codes like L1910 (AFO, prefabricated, plastic) or L1920 (AFO, prefabricated, with joints). The distinction between custom-fabricated and prefabricated is critical for coverage and payment.

**Q:** What is the HCPCS code for a hospital bed for home use?

**A:** 
- **E0250**: Hospital bed, fixed height, with any type side rails and mattress
- **E0251**: Hospital bed, variable height, with any type side rails and mattress
- **E0255**: Hospital bed, variable height, with any type side rails and mattress, semi-electric
- **E0260**: Hospital bed, variable height, with any type side rails and mattress, total electric
- **E0301**: Hospital bed, heavy-duty, extra-wide, with any type side rails and mattress

Medical necessity documentation must specify the patient's condition (e.g., inability to get in/out of a standard bed due to weakness, paralysis, or post-surgical requirements).

**Q:** A patient receives Zoledronic Acid (Reclast) 5mg for osteoporosis. What codes should be used?

**A:** 
- **Drug code**: J3487 (Zoledronic acid, 1mg) -- bill 5 units
- **Administration code**: 96365 (IV infusion, initial) or 96374 (IV push)

Zoledronic acid is typically administered as a 5mg infusion over at least 15 minutes. The HCPCS code is per mg. Verify the specific HCPCS code (J3486 vs. J3487) based on the drug formulation (Reclast vs. Zometa vs. generic).

**Q:** What is QW modifier used for and when is it necessary?

**A:** The QW modifier indicates a CLIA-waived test. It is appended to laboratory CPT/HCPCS codes when the test is performed using a CLIA-waived method (e.g., waived urinalysis, waived glucose, waived strep A test). The modifier is necessary for Medicare reimbursement of these tests performed in a physician office or CLIA-waived laboratory. It tells CMS that the test was performed in a waived setting.

**Q:** A physician provides transitional care management (TCM) services. What codes are used?

**A:** TCM is billed using CPT codes:
- **99495**: TCM, moderate medical decision complexity (face-to-face visit within 14 days of discharge)
- **99496**: TCM, high medical decision complexity (face-to-face visit within 7 days of discharge)

These are not HCPCS Level II codes. However, Medicare may require specific G-codes for reporting TCM in certain circumstances. The TCM service includes interactive contact (call, visit) within 2 business days of discharge and non-face-to-face services for the first 30 days post-discharge.

**Q:** Can multiple J-codes be billed for the same encounter?

**A:** Yes, multiple J-codes can be billed for the same encounter when multiple drugs are administered. Each drug requires its own line item. However, follow these rules:
- Do NOT bill multiple J-codes for the same drug on the same date from the same vial
- Use modifier JW for wasted drug (discarded from a single-dose vial)
- Sequence J-codes with the highest cost first
- Do not use modifier 59 with J-codes for different drugs

**Q:** What is G2211 and when should it be used?

**A:** G2211 is a Medicare add-on code introduced in 2024 for "Visit complexity inherent to evaluation and management associated with medical care services that serve as the continuing focal point for all needed health care services and/or with ongoing care of a patient with a single, serious, or complex chronic condition." It is billed in ADDITION to the primary E/M code (99202-99215). It represents the additional work of being the patient's primary care provider or ongoing specialist managing a serious chronic condition.

Use G2211 with modifier 25 if the E/M also carries modifier 25. Do NOT use G2211 with:
- E/M codes for preventive services (99381-99397)
- Transitional Care Management (99495-99496)
- Chronic Care Management (99490, 99487)
- Prolonged services (99417, G2212)
