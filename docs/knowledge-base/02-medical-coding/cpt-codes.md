# CPT Codes: Current Procedural Terminology

## Overview

Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) is a medical code set maintained by the American Medical Association (AMA) that describes medical, surgical, and diagnostic services. CPT codes are used by physicians, hospitals, and other healthcare providers to report services and procedures to payers for reimbursement. CPT codes are the foundation of outpatient and professional fee billing in the United States.

## CPT Code Categories

### Category I: Procedural Codes (00000-99999)

Category I codes represent widely performed procedures and services that are approved by the FDA, supported by peer-reviewed literature, and have demonstrated clinical efficacy. These are the most commonly used codes.

| Range | Section | Description |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| 00100-01999 | Anesthesia | Anesthesia services for all body regions |
| 10021-69990 | Surgery | Surgical procedures by body system |
| 70010-79999 | Radiology | Diagnostic imaging, nuclear medicine, radiation oncology |
| 80047-89398 | Pathology/Laboratory | Lab tests, panels, molecular pathology |
| 90281-99607 | Medicine | Immunizations, psychiatry, cardiology, pulmonary, etc. |
| 99202-99499 | Evaluation and Management | Office visits, hospital visits, consultations, nursing facility |

### Category II: Performance Measurement Codes (0001F-9007F)

These are optional tracking codes used for performance measurement and quality reporting (e.g., PQRS, MIPS). They are not billed and do not affect reimbursement. Example: 4000F = Blood pressure measured.

### Category III: Emerging Technology Codes (0004T-0955T)

Temporary codes for new and emerging technologies not yet approved for Category I. Replaced every 5 years unless renewed or promoted to Category I. Example: 0503T = Fecal microbiota transplantation.

## Evaluation and Management (E/M) Codes (99202-99499)

E/M codes are the most frequently reported codes in medical billing. They describe patient encounters for evaluation and management of health conditions.

### 2024 E/M Changes

The AMA implemented significant changes to E/M coding guidelines effective January 1, 2023 (with continued adoption through 2024):

- **MDM or Time**: Level is determined by either Medical Decision Making (MDM) OR total time on the date of encounter
- **History and Exam no longer count**: The history and physical exam are no longer used to determine E/M level for office/outpatient visits
- **Time definition changed**: Time now means total provider time on the date of the encounter (including pre-chart review, documentation, ordering, coordinating care)

### Medical Decision Making (MDM) Levels

MDM has three elements, each assessed for complexity:

1. **Problems Addressed**: Number and severity of diagnoses/conditions managed
2. **Data Reviewed**: Medical records, tests, results reviewed/ordered
3. **Risk of Complications**: Morbidity/mortality risk from management

**Straightforward MDM**:
- 1 self-limited problem
- Minimal/no data
- Minimal risk

**Low MDM**:
- 2+ self-limited problems or 1 stable chronic
- Limited data (1 test order)
- Low risk

**Moderate MDM**:
- 1+ chronic with exacerbation or 1 acute with systemic symptoms
- Moderate data (2+ tests, independent historian)
- Moderate risk

**High MDM**:
- 1+ chronic with severe exacerbation or acute threat to life
- Extensive data (3+ tests, independent historian plus discussion)
- High risk

## Anesthesia Codes (00100-01999)

Anesthesia coding is unique because payment is calculated as:

**Payment = (Base Units + Time Units + Modifying Units) x Conversion Factor**

- **Base Units**: Assigned per CPT anesthesia code, representing procedure complexity
- **Time Units**: 1 unit per 15 minutes of anesthesia time (starts when anesthesia provider begins preparing patient, ends when patient is safely recovered)
- **Physical Status Modifiers**: P1 (normal healthy) through P6 (brain-dead organ donor) -- these are informational but may affect payment
- **Qualifying Circumstances**: Additional codes (99100-99140) for special situations
- **Conversion Factor**: Geographic-specific rate per unit, updated annually by CMS

**Example**: A cholecystectomy (00790) with base units of 6, lasting 90 minutes (6 time units), P2 physical status, in a locality with CF of $22.00:
Payment = (6 + 6) x $22.00 = $264.00

## CPT Modifiers

CPT modifiers are two-digit codes (or two characters) appended to CPT codes to provide additional information about the service provided.

| Modifier | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| 22 | Increased Procedural Service | Surgery requiring significantly more work than typical |
| 25 | Significant, Separately Identifiable E/M | E/M on same day as a procedure (e.g., office visit + mole removal) |
| 26 | Professional Component | Radiology interpretation only |
| 50 | Bilateral Procedure | Same procedure performed on both sides |
| 51 | Multiple Procedures | Second and subsequent procedures |
| 52 | Reduced Services | Service partially reduced or eliminated |
| 53 | Discontinued Procedure | Procedure stopped due to patient risk |
| 59 | Distinct Procedural Service | Different session, different site, different lesion |
| 62 | Co-Surgery | Two surgeons performing distinct parts of same procedure |
| 66 | Surgical Team | Complex procedure requiring multiple specialists |
| 76 | Repeat Procedure by Same Physician | Same procedure repeated post-op or during same session |
| 77 | Repeat Procedure by Different Physician | Same procedure by different provider |
| 78 | Return to OR for Related Procedure | Unplanned return to OR during global period |
| 79 | Unrelated Procedure During Global | New condition requiring surgery during global |
| 80 | Assistant Surgeon | Physician assisting primary surgeon |
| 81 | Minimum Assistant Surgeon | Minimal assistance required |
| 82 | Assistant Surgeon (Resident Unavailable) | Teaching hospital, qualified resident not available |
| 99 | Multiple Modifiers | Multiple modifiers on one code line |

### Modifier 25 -- Detailed Guidance

**Q:** When is modifier 25 appropriate?

**A:** Modifier 25 is appended to an E/M code (99202-99215) when a separately identifiable E/M service is performed on the same day as a minor surgical procedure (0/10-day global). The E/M must be significant and separate from the procedure. Documentation must clearly support the separate nature.

**Correct example**: Patient comes for follow-up of hypertension (99213-25) and also has a mole removed (17000). The visit addressed medication adjustment (separate work).

**Incorrect example**: Patient comes for wart removal only, physician documents "brief exam of wart area" and bills 99213-25. This is not separately identifiable.

### Modifier 59 -- Detailed Guidance

**Q:** What is the correct use of modifier 59?

**A:** Modifier 59 indicates a distinct procedural service. It is used when two procedures that would normally be bundled (per NCCI edits) are performed at:
- Different anatomic sites
- Different patient encounters
- Different sessions
- Different surgical sessions

CMS recommends using the appropriate **anatomical modifier** (LT, RT, etc.) instead of 59 when possible. The National Correct Coding Initiative (NCCI) has created specific modifiers that are often preferable to 59: XE, XS, XP, XU.

## Add-On Codes

Add-on codes are identified by a "+" symbol in the CPT manual and can **only** be billed with a primary procedure. They are never billed alone. Examples:

| Code | Description | Parent Code |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| +1006X | Each additional lesion | 10061 |
| +47550 | Cholangiogram with cholecystectomy | 47562-47564 |
| +92978 | IVUS add-on | 92975 |

**Billing rule**: Add-on codes are excluded from multiple procedure reductions and are paid at 100%.

## Multiple Surgery Rules

When multiple surgical procedures are performed during the same operative session, Medicare applies a reduction:

- **Highest valued procedure**: 100% of fee schedule
- **Second procedure**: 50% of fee schedule
- **Third and subsequent procedures**: 25% of fee schedule

This applies to the surgical codes themselves; add-on codes, bilateral procedures, and assistant surgeon fees are handled separately.

**Example**: Three procedures performed with fee schedule amounts:
1. 27130 (Hip replacement, $1,400) -- paid at 100% = $1,400
2. 27372 (Removal of hardware, $500) -- paid at 50% = $250
3. 29876 (Knee arthroscopy, $600) -- paid at 25% = $150

Total = $1,800

### Bilateral Surgery Rules

- If a code has a bilateral indicator of **1**: bill with modifier 50, pay = 150%
- If a code has a bilateral indicator of **2**: bill two units on one line, pay = 150%
- If a code has a bilateral indicator of **3**: always bilateral by definition, pay = 100%
- If a code has a bilateral indicator of **0**: do not use modifier 50, report on two lines with RT/LT

### Co-Surgery (Modifier 62)

Two surgeons performing distinct parts of a single procedure. Each surgeon reports the same code with modifier 62. Each is paid at **62.5%** of the fee schedule amount.

### Assistant Surgeon (Modifiers 80/81/82/AS)

- Assistant surgeon is paid at **20%** of the fee schedule for the primary procedure
- Must use modifier 80 (or AS for PA/NP)
- Some codes are designated as "assistant at surgery not allowed" -- check the Medicare Physician Fee Schedule

## Q&A Pairs

**Q:** A patient presents for an initial office visit for management of diabetes, hypertension, and heart failure. The physician spends 60 minutes face-to-face. What E/M code should be reported?

**A:** 99205. Under 2024 guidelines, the code can be selected based on either MDM or total time. With 60 minutes of total provider time on the date of encounter, this falls within the 60-74 minute range for 99205 (high level new patient). Alternatively, the multiple chronic illnesses with acute decompensation would qualify as high MDM.

---

**Q:** Can modifier 25 be appended to 99281 (ED visit, level 1)?

**A:** No. Modifier 25 requires a "significant, separately identifiable" E/M service. A level 1 ED visit (99281) represents a minimal service that does not meet the threshold for significance. If a procedure is performed, the 99281 is typically not separately billable. CMS guidelines state that 99281 is not separately payable with a procedure -- many payers will deny the combination.

---

**Q:** A surgeon performs a laparoscopic cholecystectomy (47562) and finds the anatomy unclear, so they convert to open cholecystectomy (47600). How should this be billed?

**A:** Report only the open cholecystectomy (47600) with modifier 22 (increased procedural service) since the conversion represents additional work. The laparoscopic attempt is included in the open procedure. Do not bill both codes. Add supporting documentation describing the increased difficulty.

---

**Q:** A patient has a screening colonoscopy (G0105) during which a polyp is found and removed. What codes are reported?

**A:** The appropriate code depends on the polyp removal method. If a snare polypectomy was performed, report 45385 with modifier PT (colorectal cancer screening converted to diagnostic) and append KX or modifier 33 (preventive service). Some payers require G0105 with modifier 33 and also the polypectomy code. For a hot biopsy forceps removal, use 45380. The patient may owe the deductible for the diagnostic portion.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between CPT modifier 59 and the -X{EPSU} modifiers?

**A:** The -X{EPSU} modifiers were created by CMS as more specific alternatives to modifier 59:
- **XE**: Separate encounter -- distinct encounter on different date
- **XS**: Separate structure -- distinct anatomic site/organ
- **XP**: Separate practitioner -- different provider
- **XU**: Unusual non-overlapping -- service not described by code combination
Use of these modifiers is preferred over 59 as they provide greater specificity. However, not all private payers accept them.

---

**Q:** A physician performs an E/M service (99214) and a trigger point injection (20553) during the same visit. How should this be billed?

**A:** Bill 99214-25 (significant, separately identifiable E/M) and 20553. The modifier 25 indicates the E/M service was separate from the procedure. The documentation must show that the E/M addressed distinct medical decision-making beyond the decision to perform the injection.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between Medicare's multiple procedure payment reduction (MPPR) and CPT multiple surgery rules?

**A:** 
- **CPT Multiple Surgery Rules**: Apply to surgical procedures by the same surgeon in the same operative session. 100% / 50% / 25% reduction applies to the professional component.
- **MPPR**: Applies to multiple therapy services (PT/OT/SLP) and diagnostic imaging services billed by the same provider on the same day. Typically the second and subsequent procedures are reduced by 20%.

---

**Q:** When is it appropriate to use modifier 22 (Increased Procedural Service)?

**A:** Modifier 22 is used when the work required is substantially greater than typically required for the procedure. Examples include:
- Severely obese patient requiring extra surgical time
- Extensive scar tissue/adhesions requiring additional dissection
- Anatomic variation making the procedure more difficult
- Bleeding disorder requiring extra hemostatic measures

Documentation must specifically describe the increased complexity, the extra time involved, and why it was medically necessary.

---

**Q:** A patient receives a vaccine (90686, influenza) and the physician spends 5 minutes counseling the patient. Can an E/M code be billed?

**A:** For Medicare: No, if the sole reason for the visit was the vaccine administration. For some commercial payers: If the counseling was significant and separately identifiable (e.g., reviewing abnormal lab results), 99211 could be appropriate. Generally, minimal E/M with vaccine administration is bundled and not separately reimbursable. However, for new patients or if the visit is for a specific problem, a higher level E/M may be appropriate.

---

**Q:** What are the documentation requirements for critical care codes 99291 and 99292?

**A:** Critical care time must be:
- Documented in the medical record as total time spent
- Clearly identifiable as critical care (not routine post-op or floor management)
- At least 30 minutes for 99291 (first unit)
- Each 99292 unit requires at least 15 minutes beyond the first 30
- Time should be summed and documented, not necessarily contiguous
- Critical care is life-threatening organ system failure requiring high-complexity MDM

---

**Q:** If a physician performs a procedure in the office that has a 90-day global period and the patient returns 2 weeks later for an unrelated new problem, how should the E/M be billed?

**A:** Append modifier 24 (Unrelated E/M by Same Physician During Postoperative Period) to the E/M code. The documentation must clearly establish the service is unrelated to the original surgery. Payers will deny the E/M without modifier 24 during the global period.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between a "packaged" service and a "separately payable" service?

**A:** A packaged service is included in the payment for a primary procedure and not separately reimbursable. Examples: most surgical supplies, local anesthesia, routine pre-operative evaluation. A separately payable service is distinct and billed independently. The NCCI edits database determines which code pairs are packaged vs. separately payable. Modifiers can unbundle services when specific criteria are met.

---

**Q:** A bilateral procedure is performed. When do you use modifier 50 vs. billing two lines with RT and LT?

**A:** Use modifier 50 when the CPT code's bilateral indicator is **1 or 2**. This results in one line with modifier 50, paid at 150% of the fee schedule. Bill two lines with RT and LT when the bilateral indicator is **0 or 9** -- this means the code is not defined as bilateral, and each side is billed separately (no bilateral adjustment applies).

---

**Q:** Can a surgical code be billed with modifier 51?

**A:** Yes, but most payers do not require modifier 51 on the claim. Medicare's multiple procedure reduction is applied automatically by their claims processing system based on the order of codes. Many commercial payers still request modifier 51 on the second and subsequent procedures. However, modifier 51 is never used with add-on codes, E/M codes, or preventive services.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between a Category III code and a Category I code?

**A:** Category III codes (XXXXX) are temporary codes for emerging technologies, services, and procedures. They:
- Are alphanumeric (4 digits + T, e.g., 0503T)
- Do not have a designated relative value (RVU)
- Are typically not separately payable by Medicare
- Are replaced every 5 years
- Are used for data collection to determine clinical efficacy
Category I codes are permanent, have assigned RVUs, and are payable by most insurers.

---

**Q:** A physician performs a comprehensive history and physical, moderate MDM, and spends 50 minutes total time on a new patient office visit. What code is reported under 2024 guidelines?

**A:** 99205. Under 2024 guidelines, total time can be used. 50 minutes falls in the 60-74 minute range for 99205. However, if using MDM, moderate MDM would map to 99204 (45-59 minutes). The provider would choose the code based on whichever method (time or MDM) gives the most appropriate level. The time must be total provider time, not just face-to-face time.

---

**Q:** What is the correct way to bill for a telephone evaluation (99441-99443) vs. a telehealth visit?

**A:** 
- **Telephone codes (99441-99443)**: Audio-only, non-face-to-face, for established patients only. Do not use if originating from a related E/M within the previous 7 days or leading to an E/M within the next 24 hours.
- **Telehealth**: Uses standard E/M codes (99202-99215) with modifier 95 (synchronous telemedicine) or place of service 02. Subject to originating site requirements.

---

**Q:** A physician performs Mohs micrographic surgery. How is this coded?

**A:** Mohs surgery uses codes 17311-17315. The first stage is 17311 (head/neck/genitals) or 17313 (other areas). Each additional stage uses +17312 or +17314. Each Mohs stage includes both surgical removal AND pathologic examination. Do not separately bill pathology codes.

---

**Q:** What documentation is needed to support a prolonged services code (99417)?

**A:** Bill 99417 when the total time of an office/outpatient E/M exceeds the maximum time for the primary code by at least 15 minutes. Documentation must:
- Specify the total time spent
- Note that the extra time was for medical care (not administrative)
- 99417 can only be used with 99205 or 99215
- Cannot be used with critical care or other time-based codes

---

**Q:** What are the NCCI Edits and how do they impact CPT coding?

**A:** The National Correct Coding Initiative (NCCI) edits are CMS-developed code pairs that define which procedure codes should not be billed together (column 1/column 2 edits). When two codes are in a Column 1/Column 2 edit:
- Column 1 = comprehensive code
- Column 2 = component code
You should report only the comprehensive code unless a modifier (typically 59 or -XU) is appropriate to indicate distinct services.

---

**Q:** Can an E/M service and a therapeutic procedure (e.g., 97110 therapeutic exercise) be billed together on the same day?

**A:** Yes, but modifier 25 must be appended to the E/M code, and the documentation must show that the E/M was a separately identifiable service addressing a distinct medical problem, not just the supervision of the therapy. The E/M must be significant and separately identifiable from the therapy service.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between code 99281 and 99282 in the ED?

**A:** 
- **99281**: Level 1 ED -- problem-focused history, problem-focused exam, straightforward MDM. Typically used for minor complaints that may not require physician presence (e.g., prescription refill, suture removal). Minimum of 0 minutes (usually brief).
- **99282**: Level 2 ED -- expanded problem-focused history and exam, low MDM. Used for conditions like minor lacerations, simple infections, uncomplicated URI. Typically 0-5 minutes. Both are low-level ED codes but 99282 requires documentation supporting low MDM.

---

**Q:** A surgeon performs a procedure in the office setting that has a 0-day global period. The patient also receives an E/M service. Should modifier 25 be used?

**A:** Yes. Even with 0-day global periods (e.g., skin biopsies, simple suturing), the E/M service requires modifier 25 to be separately billable. The documentation must show the E/M addressed a separate condition or was significant enough to justify separate payment. Payers routinely deny 9921x-25 with minor procedures if the documentation only supports "the patient came in for the procedure."

---

**Q:** What is the concept of "bundling" in CPT coding?

**A:** Bundling refers to the inclusion of multiple service components into a single CPT code. For example, a surgical code (e.g., 47562 laparoscopic cholecystectomy) includes:
- Local anesthesia
- Opening and closing the surgical site
- The primary surgical procedure
- Routine post-operative care (during the global period)
- Supplies and equipment ordinarily used

Services that are separately billable include: significant E/M services (modifier 25), procedures performed for a different diagnosis, and complications requiring return to OR.

---

**Q:** How do you code for a patient who receives E/M services and an immunization (90471) during the same visit?

**A:** The vaccine administration code (90471) is separately billable with the E/M if:
- The E/M is for a significant problem (not just vaccine counseling)
- Modifier 25 is appended to the E/M
- The vaccine product codes (e.g., 90688) are billed separately

For preventive visits (99381-99397), the well-visit typically covers the vaccine counseling and administration does not require modifier 25.

---

**Q:** What is a "Medically Unlikely Edit" (MUE)?

**A:** MUEs are CMS edits that limit the maximum number of units of service for a single CPT code that can be billed per patient per date of service. For example, if code 12345 has an MUE of 2, you cannot bill more than 2 units on a single date, even across multiple claim lines. MUEs prevent unbundling and excessive billing.

---

**Q:** A patient undergoes surgery on day 1. On day 3, the surgeon returns to the OR for a complication. How is this coded?

**A:** The return to the OR during the global period is reported with the appropriate surgical code for the reoperation, appended with modifier 78 (Return to the Operating Room for a Related Procedure During the Postoperative Period). The modifier indicates the original surgery's global period includes the pre- and post-op care, but the return to OR is separately payable. Medicare pays 80% of the fee schedule for 090-day global procedures billed with modifier 78.

---

**Q:** How are MIPS quality data codes (G-codes) different from Category II CPT codes?

**A:** Category II CPT codes (0001F-9007F) are AMA-developed performance measurement codes used to track processes, outcomes, and patient experience. G-codes are CMS-developed temporary codes used for the same purpose in Medicare programs (MIPS, PQRS). Both are informational only and do not generate payment. The difference is who develops them (AMA vs. CMS) and which payer recognizes them.
