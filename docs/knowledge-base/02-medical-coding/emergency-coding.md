# Emergency Department Coding

## Overview

Emergency Department (ED) coding covers evaluation and management (E/M) services provided in the emergency department setting, as well as critical care, observation services, trauma team activation, and emergency surgical procedures. ED coding is performed by emergency physicians, hospitalists, and trauma surgeons.

---

## ED E/M Codes (99281-99285)

ED E/M codes are used for services provided in the emergency department setting. Under the 2024 guidelines, ED E/M levels are determined by either **Medical Decision Making (MDM)** or **time**.

| Code | MDM Level | Time (minutes) | Description |
|------|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| 99281 | Straightforward | 0-4 | Minimal problem, no MDM required |
| 99282 | Low | 5-14 | Minor problem, self-limited |
| 99283 | Low-Moderate | 15-24 | Acute illness/injury with systemic symptoms |
| 99284 | Moderate | 25-34 | Moderate severity, urgent problem |
| 99285 | High | 35-49 + | Critical/high severity, life-threatening |

### 2024 Changes for ED E/M Codes

The 2024 E/M changes apply to ED codes:
- **MDM or time**: Level determined by either MDM or total provider time (NOT facetime)
- **History and exam**: Not used to determine level, but must still be documented
- **Time definition**: Total provider time on the date of encounter, including:
  - Preparing to see the patient (chart review)
  - History and exam
  - Medical decision-making
  - Ordering medications, tests, and procedures
  - Documentation
  - Communicating with patient/family/other providers
  - Coordinating care
  - Arranging follow-up or admission

### Code-Specific Guidance

#### 99281 -- Level 1 ED Visit
- **Typical**: Suture removal, prescription refill, work note
- **MDM**: Straightforward -- minimal problems (1 self-limited)
- **Data**: Minimal
- **Risk**: Minimal
- **Documentation**: Brief note, problem-focused

#### 99282 -- Level 2 ED Visit
- **Typical**: Minor laceration, simple UTI, viral URI
- **MDM**: Low MDM (2+ self-limited or 1 stable chronic)
- **Data**: Limited (1 lab/test order)
- **Risk**: Low risk

#### 99283 -- Level 3 ED Visit
- **Typical**: Sprained ankle, simple fracture, headache, abdominal pain
- **MDM**: Low MDM (1+ chronic with exacerbation, or 1 acute with systemic symptoms)
- **Data**: Moderate (2+ tests ordered)
- **Risk**: Moderate

#### 99284 -- Level 4 ED Visit
- **Typical**: Chest pain (rule out ACS), renal colic, pneumonia, COPD exacerbation
- **MDM**: Moderate MDM
- **Data**: Moderate to extensive (multiple tests, independent historian)
- **Risk**: Moderate

#### 99285 -- Level 5 ED Visit
- **Typical**: Cardiac arrest, stroke, respiratory distress, trauma, sepsis
- **MDM**: High MDM (life-threatening, acute threat to life or limb)
- **Data**: Extensive (multiple tests, direct discussion with other MDs)
- **Risk**: High (life-threatening)

### ED E/M Documentation Requirements

1. **Chief complaint**: Reason for ED visit
2. **History**: Pertinent HPI for the presenting problem
3. **Physical exam**: Documented, but no longer used to determine level
4. **Medical decision-making**: Document the medical necessity for the level
5. **Time**: If using time to determine level, document total time spent
6. **Disposition**: Admit, discharge, transfer, or observation

## Critical Care Codes (99291 and 99292)

Critical care is the direct delivery of medical care for a critically ill or critically injured patient. It involves high-complexity decision-making to assess, manipulate, and support vital organ system failure, and/or to prevent further life-threatening deterioration of the patient's condition.

### Code Definitions

| Code | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 99291 | Critical care, evaluation and management of the critically ill/critically injured patient; first 30-74 minutes |
| 99292 | Critical care, each additional 30 minutes (listed separately in addition to code for primary service) |

### What is Critical Care?

Critical care requires:
1. **Critically ill/injured patient**: One or more vital organ systems are acutely failing (e.g., respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation, septic shock requiring vasopressors)
2. **High-complexity medical decision-making**: Frequent reassessment, manipulation of therapies based on real-time data
3. **Time**: The physician spends the majority of the time (more than 50%) in direct patient care activities

### Time Requirements for Critical Care

| Total Critical Care Time | Codes Billed |
|-------------------------|--------------|
| Less than 30 minutes | Use E/M code (99281-99285 or observation) |
| 30-74 minutes | 99291 x 1 |
| 75-104 minutes | 99291 x 1 + 99292 x 1 |
| 105-134 minutes | 99291 x 1 + 99292 x 2 |
| 135-164 minutes | 99291 x 1 + 99292 x 3 |

**Critical care time calculation**:
- Time can be continuous or aggregated
- Time must be documented in the medical record
- Only time spent devoted to the critically ill patient counts
- Time includes: at bedside, reviewing labs, discussing with consultants, documenting in the chart, coordinating care

### Services Bundled into Critical Care

The following are NOT separately billable when performed during critical care time:
- Chest X-ray interpretation (71045-71048)
- Blood draw (36415)
- ECG interpretation (93005-93010)
- Pulse oximetry (94760-94762)
- Ventilator management (94002-94004)
- Vascular access (36591-36592)

### Services Separately Billable with Critical Care

- Procedures requiring more than minimal time/effort (e.g., central line placement 36556, intubation 31500)
- CPR (92950)
- IABP insertion (33967)
- Dialysis

### Critical Care in the ED vs. Floor

Critical care can be provided anywhere in the hospital (ED, ICU, step-down unit, even the floor). The location does not matter. What matters is:
- The patient's condition (critically ill)
- The service provided (high-complexity MDM, time-based)
- Documentation supporting critical care

## Observation Services

Observation is a well-defined set of specific, clinically appropriate services provided to patients who need to be monitored for a short period (typically less than 24 hours) to determine whether they require admission to the hospital.

### Observation Codes for Emergency Physicians

| Code | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 99218 | Initial observation care, low severity |
| 99219 | Initial observation care, moderate severity |
| 99220 | Initial observation care, high severity |
| 99224 | Subsequent observation care, low severity |
| 99225 | Subsequent observation care, moderate severity |
| 99226 | Subsequent observation care, high severity |
| 99234 | Observation/inpatient same-date admit/discharge, low |
| 99235 | Observation/inpatient same-date admit/discharge, moderate |
| 99236 | Observation/inpatient same-date admit/discharge, high |

### Observation vs. Inpatient Admission

| Factor | Observation | Inpatient |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Expected length | < 2 midnights | > 2 midnights |
| Admission order | Observation order | Inpatient admit order |
| Patient status | Outpatient | Inpatient |
| Reimbursement | APC (OPPS) or per diem | DRG-based |
| Physician billing | Observation codes | Hospital admission codes |

**Two-Midnight Rule**: Medicare presumes that a hospital stay crossing 2 midnights after the physician order qualifies as inpatient. Stays of less than 2 midnights are generally observation, unless medical necessity supports a shorter inpatient stay.

## Emergency Surgery Modifiers

### Modifier 54 -- Surgical Care Only

When the emergency surgeon provides only the surgical procedure (not pre- or post-op care), use modifier 54.

**Example**: A trauma surgeon performs an emergency splenectomy but the patient is transferred to the ICU team for post-op management. The surgeon bills the surgical code with modifier 54.

### Modifier 55 -- Postoperative Management Only

If a surgeon assumes post-operative care of an emergency surgery patient but did not perform the surgery, use modifier 55.

**Example**: A patient is transferred to a trauma center after emergency surgery at an outside hospital. The receiving surgeon manages post-op care. Bill the surgical code with modifier 55.

### Modifier 56 -- Preoperative Management Only

Use when a physician provides pre-operative care for an emergency surgery but does not perform the surgery.

### Modifier 57 -- Decision for Surgery

When an ED physician or surgeon provides an E/M service that results in the decision to perform emergency surgery, use modifier 57 on the E/M code.

## Trauma Team Activation

### Code G0390 -- Trauma Response Team Activation

**Description**: Trauma response team activation associated with hospital-based trauma services.

**Use**: Code G0390 is used by the hospital (facility side), NOT by the physician. It covers the activation and readiness of the trauma team.

**Documentation**: Requires:
- Trauma team activation criteria met (e.g., mechanism of injury, vital signs, injuries)
- Documentation that the trauma team was activated
- Documentation of the trauma surgeon's presence at the bedside

### Trauma Surgery Bundling

Trauma surgery typically includes:
- Assessment and resuscitation
- Diagnostic procedures (FAST ultrasound, DPL)
- Surgical procedures
- Post-operative ICU management (within global period)

**Separately billable**:
- Interventional radiology procedures
- Procedures outside the global period
- Critical care (if documented separately and trauma surgeon is the primary physician managing critical care)

### ED Procedure Documentation Requirements

For procedures in the ED, document:
1. **Pre-procedure**: Indication, consent, anesthesia
2. **Procedure**: Technique, findings, specimens
3. **Post-procedure**: Disposition, complications, follow-up
4. **Separate identification**: For E/M billing with modifier 25

## ED Billing Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Simple Laceration Repair

Patient presents with a 3 cm laceration on the forearm. The physician evaluates the patient, documents a brief exam, and performs simple closure.

**Coding**: 
- 12002 (Simple repair, 2.6-7.5 cm, scalp/neck/trunk/extremities)
- 99282 (ED visit, Level 2) only if separately identifiable E/M -- modifier 25 required

**Note**: In most EDs, the evaluation is inherent to the laceration repair. The MDM for the laceration is straightforward. A separate E/M with modifier 25 is typically not appropriate unless there is a separate complaint.

### Scenario 2: Chest Pain Workup

Patient presents with chest pain, SOB, diaphoresis. Workup includes ECG, cardiac enzymes, CXR. Rule out ACS.

**Coding**:
- 99284 (ED visit, Level 4 -- moderate MDM, chest pain workup)
- 93005 (ECG, tracing)
- 71045 (Chest X-ray, single view) -- if interpreted by ED physician, add modifier 26

### Scenario 3: Trauma Activation

Patient arrives after MVA with multiple injuries. Trauma team activated. FAST ultrasound, chest X-ray, pelvic X-ray, CT scan.

**Coding**:
- 99285 (ED visit, Level 5 -- high MDM)
- 93307 (Echocardiography, transthoracic, complete) -- if trauma surgeon performs FAST
- 71045 (Chest X-ray)
- 72170 (Pelvis X-ray)
- G0390 (Trauma team activation) -- facility code only

### Scenario 4: Critical Care, Respiratory Failure

Patient presents in respiratory distress, requires intubation and mechanical ventilation.

**Coding**:
- 99291 (Critical care, first 30-74 minutes)
- 31500 (Intubation, endotracheal, emergency procedure)

**Note**: Ventilator management (94002-94004) is bundled into critical care. Do not bill separately.

### Scenario 5: Observation to Inpatient

Patient presents with pneumonia. Placed in observation (99219, initial observation). Later, the patient decompensates and is admitted to inpatient status.

**Coding**:
- 99221 (Initial hospital care) when conversion to inpatient occurs
- Do NOT bill both observation and inpatient codes on same day

## Denial Reasons in ED Coding

| Denial Reason | Cause | Prevention |
|---------------|-------|------------|
| Level not supported | MDM documentation does not support level billed | Clearly document problems, data, and risk |
| Modifier 25 missing | E/M billed same day as procedure | Append modifier 25 when E/M is separate |
| Medical necessity | Diagnosis does not support ED visit level | Ensure diagnosis justifies the level |
| Critical care time insufficient | <30 minutes documented | Use E/M code for <30 minutes of critical care |
| Duplicate E/M | ED physician and admitting physician both bill E/M | ED physician uses 9928x, admitting uses 99221-99223 |
| Observation vs. inpatient | Wrong status order | Clarify observation vs. inpatient at registration |
| Bundled services | Procedural component billed with critical care | Do not separately bill bundled services |
| Incorrect modifier | Wrong modifier for procedure | Use correct modifier (59, 25, 57, etc.) |

## Q&A Pairs

**Q:** What is the minimum time requirement for billing critical care (99291)?

**A:** The minimum critical care time for 99291 is 30 minutes. If the physician spends less than 30 minutes providing critical care services, use the appropriate E/M code (99281-99285 or 99221-99223) instead. The 30 minutes must be exclusively devoted to the critically ill patient. Time spent on other patients does not count.

---

**Q:** Can an emergency physician bill both an ED E/M code (99285) and critical care (99291) on the same day?

**A:** No. Critical care (99291) is an E/M service itself. You cannot bill a separate ED E/M code (99281-99285) on the same day as critical care for the same patient. However, you can bill a procedure (e.g., intubation 31500, central line 36556) separately from critical care, as long as the time spent on the procedure is not included in the critical care time.

---

**Q:** A patient presents with a simple finger laceration. The physician evaluates the patient and repairs the laceration. Is the E/M separately billable?

**A:** Typically, no. The evaluation is inherent to the decision to repair the laceration. The E/M is not a significantly, separately identifiable service from the procedure. If the physician also addressed an unrelated complaint (e.g., the patient also has a separate complaint of hypertension), a separate E/M with modifier 25 may be appropriate. Otherwise, bill only the procedure (e.g., 12001 for simple finger laceration repair).

---

**Q:** What is the difference between ED E/M code 99283 and 99284?

**A:** 
- **99283 (Level 3)**: Low-to-moderate MDM. Examples: acute sprain, uncomplicated laceration, mild abdominal pain, simple fracture. Typically requires 1-2 diagnostic tests.
- **99284 (Level 4)**: Moderate MDM. Examples: chest pain (rule out ACS), renal colic, COPD exacerbation, pneumonia. Typically requires 3+ tests or extensive data review. The key distinction is the MDM level -- moderate vs. low-to-moderate.

---

**Q:** When is an ED visit for "pulse ox check" or "recheck on a known condition" billed as 99281 (Level 1)?

**A:** 99281 is appropriate when the patient's presenting problem is minimal (e.g., suture check, BP check, medication refill), the physician provides a problem-focused history and exam, and the MDM is straightforward. The service requires a physician's presence but does not involve significant medical decision-making. Most payers scrutinize 99281 claims, so ensure documentation supports the minimal nature of the visit.

---

**Q:** A patient is in the ED for 4 hours for workup of chest pain. The physician documents "total time 45 minutes." What code is appropriate?

**A:** If using MDM: If the MDM is moderate (chest pain workup, multiple tests ordered), use 99284 (moderate MDM). If using time: 99284 requires 25-34 minutes, and the physician documents 45 minutes of total provider time. Both support 99284. However, the time must be total provider time, not the length of the ED stay. The patient being in the ED for 4 hours does not equal physician time.

---

**Q:** Can a physician bill for critical care in the ED if the patient is also admitted to the ICU?

**A:** Yes, as long as the ED physician provides critical care (managing life-threatening organ failure) for at least 30 minutes before transferring care to the ICU team. The ED physician's critical care time must be documented and occurs before the admission order. Once the patient is admitted by the inpatient team, the ED physician's critical care ends.

---

**Q:** What is the correct way to bill for observation services that span midnight?

**A:** When a patient is placed in observation and the care spans midnight:
- Day 1: Bill the initial observation code (99218-99220) for the first day
- Day 2: Bill the subsequent observation code (99224-99226) for the second day
- Discharge day: If discharged on day 3, use 99217 (Observation care discharge day management)

If the patient is admitted within the same calendar day as discharge, use 99234-99236 (same-date admit/discharge).

---

**Q:** A trauma patient requires open reduction of a femur fracture in the OR. The same surgeon also provides critical care before the OR. Can both be billed?

**A:** Yes, if the services are distinct and documented separately. The critical care (99291) is billed for the time spent managing the critically injured patient before taking them to the OR. The surgery (e.g., 27506 for femur ORIF with intramedullary nail) is billed separately. The critical care time must not include time spent preparing for the surgery. Use modifier 25 on the E/M code if same-day, or bill the critical care and the surgery as separate services on the same date with modifier 25 on the E/M code.

---

**Q:** What documentation is needed to support a Level 5 ED visit (99285)?

**A:** For 99285, the documentation must support HIGH medical decision-making:
1. **Problems**: At least 1 chronic illness with severe exacerbation, or an acute threat to life or limb function
2. **Data**: Extensive (multiple tests, independent historian, direct discussion with other clinicians)
3. **Risk**: High risk -- drug therapy requiring intensive monitoring, decision for major surgery, threat to life

Examples: Cardiac arrest, major trauma, stroke with acute neurological deficit, respiratory failure requiring intubation, septic shock. The note should clearly convey the severity and complexity.

---

**Q:** What is role of modifier 57 in the ED for acute surgery?

**A:** When an ED physician or surgeon provides an E/M service that results in the decision to perform acute/emergency surgery, modifier 57 is appended to the E/M code. This is used for major surgeries (090-day global period) where the decision must be made rapidly. For example:
- Patient with acute appendicitis -- E/M results in decision for lap appendectomy
- Patient with hip fracture -- E/M results in decision for ORIF

The E/M (e.g., 99284-57 or 99285-57) is billed with modifier 57, separate from the surgical code.

---

**Q:** Can an emergency physician bill for both ED E/M and observation services on the same day?

**A:** Yes, if the patient is placed in observation by the ED physician AND the ED physician continues to manage the patient's observation care. Bill:
- 99281-99285 (ED visit) for the initial evaluation and treatment in the ED
- 99218-99220 (Initial observation care) for the observation period

However, CPT guidelines state that the ED visit and observation care should not be billed together when the ED physician merely initiates observation and then transfers care to the hospitalist or observation service. Bill the appropriate evaluation code based on the physician's role.

---

**Q:** What is the appropriate code for a patient who is "admitted to observation" but discharged after 6 hours?

**A:** If the patient is admitted and discharged on the same calendar day, use 99234-99236 (Observation/inpatient same-date admit/discharge). The documentation should capture the initial assessment and plan, monitoring during observation, and the discharge assessment. If the patient is discharged the next calendar day, bill the initial observation (99218-99220) for day 1 and 99217 (Observation discharge) for day 2.

---

**Q:** What common procedures in the ED have a 0-day global period and can be billed with ED E/M?

**A:** Common 0-day global ED procedures billable with E/M (with modifier 25):
- Laceration repair (12001-13160)
- I&D of abscess (10060-10061)
- Joint injection/aspiration (20600-20610)
- Wound debridement (11042-11047)
- Foreign body removal (10120-10121)
- Nail avulsion (11730-11732)
- Burn treatment (16000-16030)
- Fracture/dislocation reduction (closed treatment)

For 0-day global procedures, the E/M requires modifier 25 and must be significant and separately identifiable.

---

**Q:** Can a physician bill critical care for a patient who is not in the ICU?

**A:** Yes. Critical care can be provided anywhere in the hospital -- ED, ICU, step-down unit, or even a regular floor. The location does not matter. What matters is:
1. The patient meets the definition of critically ill (acute organ failure, life-threatening)
2. The physician provides high-complexity decision-making
3. Time is documented showing at least 30 minutes devoted to the patient

A patient on a regular floor who develops septic shock requiring vasopressors can be billed critical care.

---

**Q:** A patient is transferred from the ED to another hospital for a higher level of care. What code should be used?

**A:** The ED physician should bill the appropriate ED E/M code (99281-99285) based on the MDM provided. The fact of transfer does not change the code. However, document:
- Why the transfer was necessary (higher level of care needed)
- Arrangements made with the receiving facility
- Mode of transport
- Patient condition at time of transfer
- Any care provided during the transfer (if the ED physician accompanies the patient)

---

**Q:** What is the correct coding for an ED physician who manages a patient in observation and then admits the patient to inpatient?

**A:** If the ED physician places the patient in observation and later admits to inpatient:
- Bill only the inpatient admission code (99221-99223)
- Do NOT bill both observation and inpatient codes on the same day
- The observation time is included in the inpatient admission

Exception: If the observation was provided by a different physician (e.g., ED physician initiates observation, hospitalist admits), the ED physician bills 99281-99285 and the hospitalist bills 99221-99223. Both can be billed on the same day.

---

**Q:** What is the role of modifier 59 in ED procedure coding?

**A:** Modifier 59 is used in the ED when two normally bundled procedures are performed at different sites or in different sessions. Examples:
- I&D of two separate abscesses on different body parts: 10060-59 for the second
- Repair of lacerations at different sites: 12001 for one site, 12001-59 for the second
- Lumbar puncture (62270) following a CT head (70450) -- if the LP is prompted by CT findings, use modifier 59

Use the most specific anatomic modifier when possible (XS, E1-E4, FA-F9, etc.).

---

**Q:** A patient presents after a fall and has both a scalp laceration and a fractured wrist. The physician repairs the laceration and performs closed reduction of the wrist fracture. How should this be coded?

**A:** 
- 12001 (Simple repair of scalp laceration, 2.5 cm or less)
- 25600 (Closed treatment of distal radial fracture, without manipulation) or 25605 (with manipulation)
- 99284-25 (ED visit, Level 4, with modifier 25, if the E/M is significant and separate)

Bill the highest-valued procedure first. The second procedure is subject to multiple procedure reduction (50%). The E/M with modifier 25 requires documentation of the separate evaluation.

---

**Q:** When is it appropriate to bill observation care from the ED for an emergency physician?

**A:** Billing observation care is appropriate when:
1. The emergency physician places the patient in observation status
2. The emergency physician remains the managing physician for the observation period
3. The patient does not get admitted to an inpatient team

If the ED physician initiates observation but a hospitalist takes over, the ED physician bills only the ED E/M code and the hospitalist bills the observation codes. The ED physician should NOT bill both ED and observation codes in this scenario.

---

**Q:** Can a patient be billed for critical care and then subsequent floor management on the same day?

**A:** Yes, if the patient transitions from critical care to non-critical care on the same calendar day. The physician would bill:
- 99291 (Critical care) for the time spent managing the critically ill patient (at least 30 minutes)
- 99231-99233 (Subsequent hospital care) for the non-critical portion of the day

The critical care time and the subsequent care must be documented separately and must occur at different times of the day. The subsequent care cannot be billed if it is included in the critical care time.
