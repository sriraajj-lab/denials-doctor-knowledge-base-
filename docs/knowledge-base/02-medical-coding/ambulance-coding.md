# Ambulance Coding: Ground and Air Medical Transport

## Overview

Ambulance coding covers emergency and non-emergency medical transportation by ground (ambulance), rotary wing (helicopter), and fixed wing (airplane). These services are coded using HCPCS Level II A-codes and require specific origin/destination modifiers to indicate the pickup and drop-off locations.

---

## Ambulance HCPCS Codes (A0425-A0437)

### Ground Ambulance Codes

| Code | Description | Service Level |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| A0425 | Ground mileage, per statute mile | Billed per mile (loaded miles only) |
| A0426 | Ambulance service, Advanced Life Support (ALS), non-emergency, transport, 1 unit (per 15 minutes) | ALS non-emergency |
| A0427 | Ambulance service, Advanced Life Support (ALS), emergency, transport, 1 unit (per 15 minutes) | ALS emergency |
| A0428 | Ambulance service, Basic Life Support (BLS), non-emergency, transport, 1 unit (per 15 minutes) | BLS non-emergency |
| A0429 | Ambulance service, Basic Life Support (BLS), emergency, transport, 1 unit (per 15 minutes) | BLS emergency |
| A0430 | Ambulance service, Specialty Care Transport (SCT), transport, 1 unit (per 15 minutes) | SCT (critical care transport) |
| A0431 | Paramedic ALS intercept (PI), per transport | ALS intercept (rural areas) |
| A0432 | Paramedic ALS intercept (PI), 1 unit (per 15 minutes) | PI, time-based alternative |
| A0433 | ALS2 (two or more ALS providers) | ALS2 transport |
| A0434 | Specialty care transport (SCT), 1 unit (per 15 minutes) | SCT, time-based |
| A0435 | Fixed wing air ambulance, per statute mile | Airplane transport |
| A0436 | Rotary wing air ambulance, per statute mile | Helicopter transport |
| A0888 | Non-covered ambulance mileage | Billed when mileage is not covered |
| A0998 | Ambulance response and treatment, no transport | Response only |

### Service Level Definitions

**Basic Life Support (BLS)**: Transport by ground ambulance vehicle with BLS capabilities. At a minimum, one emergency medical technician (EMT) in attendance. Used for stable patients who require monitoring but not advanced interventions.

**Advanced Life Support (ALS)**: Transport by ground ambulance with ALS capabilities. At least one paramedic or other ALS provider in attendance. Used for patients requiring IV access, cardiac monitoring, drug administration, or other ALS procedures.

**ALS2**: ALS transport with TWO or more ALS providers (paramedics) in the ambulance. Used for critical patients requiring multiple ALS providers.

**Specialty Care Transport (SCT)**: Interfacility transport of critically ill or injured patients requiring the constant attendance of a physician or specially trained registered nurse. Examples: ICU-to-ICU transfer, neonatal transport.

**Paramedic Intercept (PI)**: Service provided by an ALS provider who intercepts a BLS ambulance en route to the hospital to provide ALS-level care. Typically used in rural areas.

### Air Ambulance Codes

| Code | Description |
|------|-------------|
| A0430 | Fixed wing air ambulance, per statute mile |
| A0431 | Fixed wing air ambulance, per loaded statute mile |
| A0435 | Fixed wing air mileage (per statute mile) |
| A0436 | Rotary wing air mileage (per statute mile) |
| A0438 | Fixed wing air ambulance, 1 unit (per 30 minutes) |
| A0439 | Rotary wing air ambulance, 1 unit (per 30 minutes) |

**Fixed Wing (Airplane)**: Used for long-distance transports (usually over 100 miles) where ground transport would be impractical.

**Rotary Wing (Helicopter)**: Used for shorter distances where speed is critical, terrain is inaccessible, or ground transport is not feasible (e.g., trauma scene, rural hospital to trauma center).

Air ambulance is reimbursed only when:
- The patient's condition requires rapid transport to a higher level of care
- Ground transport is not clinically appropriate (time, distance, terrain)
- The transport is medically necessary

## Origin/Destination Modifiers

Ambulance services use a two-character modifier to indicate the origin (first character) and destination (second character). These modifiers describe where the patient was picked up and dropped off.

### Origin/Destination Codes

| Code | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| D | Physician's office, outpatient clinic, or dialysis center |
| E | Residential, domiciliary, nursing home, or custodial care facility |
| G | Hospital-based ESRD facility |
| H | Hospital (inpatient) |
| I | Site of transfer between modes of transport (e.g., ground to air) |
| J | Freestanding ESRD facility |
| N | Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF) |
| P | Non-hospital-based dialysis facility |
| R | Residence (patient's home) |
| S | Scene of accident or acute event |
| X | Intermediate stop at a physician's office en route to destination |

### Common Modifier Pairs

| Modifier | Origin | Destination | Typical Use |
|----------|--------|-------------|-------------|
| RH | Residence | Hospital | Most common emergency transport |
| RI | Residence | Site of mode transfer | Patient taken to airport for air transport |
| SH | Scene of accident | Hospital | Accident scene to hospital |
| SI | Scene of accident | Mode transfer | Accident to air transport |
| HH | Hospital | Hospital | Interfacility transfer |
| HN | Hospital | SNF | Transfer to skilled nursing |
| HR | Hospital | Residence | Hospital discharge home |
| RG | Residence | Dialysis center | Routine dialysis transport |
| RD | Residence | Physician office | Doctor appointment |
| DH | Physician's office | Hospital | Office to hospital transfer |
| EH | Nursing home | Hospital | Nursing home to hospital |
| EN | Nursing home | SNF | Between facilities |
| NH | SNF | Hospital | SNF to hospital emergency |
| NR | SNF | Residence | SNF discharge home |
| SR | Scene | Residence | Refused transport (rare) |
| XH | Intermediate stop | Hospital | Picked up en route |

### Billing Rules for Modifiers

- **Always required**: Every ambulance claim must include an origin/destination modifier
- **Two characters**: Origin is the first character; destination is the second
- **Same character OK**: e.g., HH for hospital-to-hospital transfer
- **Medicare specific**: Use these codes for Medicare claims; private payers may have their own requirements

## Medicare Ambulance Fee Schedule

### Fee Schedule Structure

Medicare pays for ambulance services under a national fee schedule with geographic adjustments.

**Components**:
1. **Base rate**: A flat fee for the transport service (varies by service level)
2. **Mileage**: Per loaded mile (patient on board)
3. **Supply fees**: Additional supplies and equipment
4. **Adjustments**: Rural/infrequent transport adjustments

### Payment Amounts (Approximate)

| Service | Base Rate (approx) | Mileage Rate (approx) |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|
| BLS non-emergency | $350-$450 | $7-$10/mile |
| BLS emergency | $450-$550 | $7-$10/mile |
| ALS non-emergency | $400-$500 | $7-$10/mile |
| ALS emergency | $550-$700 | $7-$10/mile |
| ALS2 | $600-$800 | $7-$10/mile |
| SCT | $700-$900 | $7-$10/mile |
| Fixed wing (per mile) | N/A | $25-$60/mile |
| Rotary wing (per mile) | N/A | $80-$150/mile |

*Actual amounts vary by locality and are updated annually.*

### Geographic Adjustments

The ambulance fee schedule is adjusted by the **Geographic Adjustment Factor (GAF)**:
- **Rural areas**: 1.5x base rate for ground mileage (rural adjustment)
- **Super-rural areas**: Additional adjustments
- **Urban areas**: Standard rates

## Medical Necessity Documentation

Medicare requires comprehensive documentation to support the medical necessity of ambulance transport.

### Essential Documentation Elements

1. **Medical necessity**: Why ambulance transport was required (not a wheelchair van or private car)
2. **Patient condition**: The patient's condition at the time of transport
3. **Vital signs**: Documented at pickup and during transport
4. **Level of service**: Why ALS vs. BLS was needed
5. **Origin and destination**: Where the patient was picked up and dropped off
6. **Distance**: Loaded mileage (patient on board)
7. **Interventions**: Any ALS procedures performed
8. **Physician certification statement** (for non-emergency transports)

### Medical Necessity Criteria

Medicare will pay for ambulance transport when:
1. The patient is **bed-confined** (unable to sit, stand, or walk without assistance)
2. The patient requires **medical monitoring** during transport (IV, cardiac monitor, oxygen)
3. The patient requires **ALS-level interventions** (IV access, drug administration, EKG monitoring)
4. The patient is being transported to the **nearest appropriate facility**
5. The patient's condition is **contraindicated for non-emergency transport**

### When Ambulance Transport is NOT Covered

- Routine transport to/from physician appointments when the patient can use other means
- Transport to a facility not the nearest appropriate for the condition (patient/family choice)
- Transport that is primarily for the patient's or family's convenience
- Transport when the patient does not require medical monitoring or intervention
- Non-emergency transport without a valid physician certification

## Ambulance Billing Rules

### Loaded Mileage

Mileage is billed only for **loaded miles** (when the patient is on board). The ambulance does not bill for miles driven to the pickup location or returning to base.

- **Ground**: Use code A0425 for per-statute-mile billing
- **Multiple patients**: Some payers allow mileage to be split when multiple patients are transported on the same trip

### Time Units

For ground transport (A0426-A0434), services are billed per 15-minute unit:
- Report 1 unit for each 15-minute increment
- Round to nearest 15 minutes (Medicare: up to 30 minutes = 1 unit, 31-45 = 2 units, 46-60 = 3 units, etc.)

### Physician Certification Statement (PCS)

For non-emergency ambulance transport, Medicare requires a **Physician Certification Statement** (CMS Form 1554) that:
- Certifies the medical necessity of ambulance transport
- Is signed by the treating physician
- Is obtained within 60 days of the transport
- Is kept on file by the ambulance provider

### Advance Beneficiary Notice (ABN)

If an ambulance transport is likely not covered by Medicare, the ambulance provider should have the beneficiary sign an ABN. Use modifier GA if ABN is on file, GZ if no ABN.

## Air Ambulance Coding Specifics

### Medical Necessity for Air Transport

Air ambulance (rotary or fixed wing) is covered when:
1. The patient's condition requires rapid transport to a higher level of care
2. Ground transport time would exceed the time for air transport
3. Ground transport is not feasible due to terrain or road conditions
4. The patient requires the speed of air transport due to time-sensitive conditions (e.g., trauma, STEMI, stroke)

### Billing for Air Transport

- **Fixed wing (A0435)**: Per statute mile, requires distance calculation
- **Rotary wing (A0436)**: Per statute mile, requires distance calculation
- Both require origin and destination modifiers showing where pickup and dropoff occurred

## Common Denial Reasons for Ambulance Claims

| Denial Reason | Cause | Solution |
|---------------|-------|----------|
| Not medically necessary | Patient did not require ambulance level of care | Document specific medical necessity |
| Wrong level of service | BLS billed for ALS patient, or vice versa | Match service level to patient condition |
| No origin/destination modifier | Missing modifier | Always include modifier on all ambulance claims |
| Missing physician certification | Non-emergency transport without PCS | Obtain PCS within 60 days |
| Loaded mileage not documented | No odometer reading | Document loaded and unloaded mileage |
| Not nearest facility | Patient transported past closer appropriate facility | Document why (patient choice? clinical need?) |
| Duplicate transport | Multiple ambulance providers billing same trip | Coordinate billing with other providers |
| Non-covered mileage | A0888 used without proper documentation | Ensure mileage is for a non-covered reason |

## Q&A Pairs

**Q:** What is the difference between ALS and BLS ambulance transport?

**A:** 
- **BLS (Basic Life Support)**: Transport by ambulance staffed with EMTs. The patient is stable and does not require advanced interventions (IV, EKG monitoring, drug administration). Examples: routine hospital discharge, non-emergency transport.
- **ALS (Advanced Life Support)**: Transport by ambulance staffed with at least one paramedic. The patient requires or may require ALS-level interventions. Examples: cardiac monitoring, IV access, drug administration, cardiac arrest management, respiratory distress.

A paramedic's presence on the ambulance alone does not make the transport ALS. The patient's condition must warrant ALS-level care.

---

**Q:** A patient is transported from home to the ED via ambulance. The ambulance service provides BLS-level service. What codes should be used?

**A:** 
- A0428 (BLS non-emergency transport) -- if the transport was not an emergency (no lights/sirens, stable patient)
- A0429 (BLS emergency transport) -- if the transport was an emergency response
- A0425 (Ground mileage, per statute mile) -- for the loaded miles
- Modifier: RH (Residence to Hospital)

The origin/destination modifier is RH (Residence to Hospital). Submit with one unit for the transport base rate and multiple units for mileage.

---

**Q:** A patient requires interfacility transport from a rural hospital to a trauma center (75 miles). The patient is intubated and on a ventilator. What level of service is appropriate?

**A:** This is a Specialty Care Transport (SCT) case. Use A0430 (SCT) or A0434 (SCT, time based). The patient requires the constant attendance of a nurse or physician due to the mechanical ventilation. SCT is the appropriate level for patients who are critically ill and require specialized monitoring and interventions during transport. The transport team typically includes a critical care nurse or respiratory therapist.

---

**Q:** What is the correct way to bill for ambulance mileage?

**A:** Mileage is billed using A0425 (Ground mileage, per statute mile). Submit the total loaded miles (from pickup point to destination). Do NOT include unloaded miles (driving to pickup or returning to base). For air ambulance, use A0435 (fixed wing) or A0436 (rotary wing) per statute mile.

Example: 15 loaded miles from home to hospital: bill 15 units of A0425.

---

**Q:** When is air ambulance (rotary wing, A0436) appropriate vs. ground transport?

**A:** Rotary wing (helicopter) transport is appropriate when:
1. The patient has a time-sensitive condition (e.g., trauma, STEMI, stroke, severe burn)
2. Ground transport time would be greater than the air transport time
3. Ground access is limited (rural areas, mountains, islands)
4. The patient is at a location inaccessible by ground (wilderness, offshore)

Air transport should NOT be used when ground transport is clinically adequate. Medicare and private payers closely audit air ambulance medical necessity.

---

**Q:** What documentation is needed to support an ALS-level transport?

**A:** Documentation must show:
1. The patient's condition at pickup (vital signs, level of consciousness, symptoms)
2. The ALS interventions performed or required (e.g., IV started, EKG monitored, medication administered)
3. The patient's condition during transport
4. The presence of a paramedic or ALS provider
5. Why BLS would have been inadequate

Examples that support ALS: chest pain with abnormal EKG, respiratory distress requiring nebulizer treatment, altered mental status, cardiac monitoring for arrhythmia, IV medications.

---

**Q:** A patient is transported by ambulance from the hospital to a skilled nursing facility (SNF). The patient is medically stable. What code should be used?

**A:** Use A0428 (BLS non-emergency transport) with modifier HN (Hospital to SNF). This is a routine interfacility transfer of a stable patient. No ALS interventions are expected. The mileage is billed separately with A0425.

---

**Q:** What is the "two-mile" rule for non-emergency ambulance transport?

**A:** Some Medicare contractors have a "two-mile rule" for non-emergency ambulance transport. If the pick-up and drop-off locations are within 2 miles, Medicare may consider the transport not medically necessary unless the patient has a specific condition that requires ambulance transport (e.g., the patient is bed-confined, requires monitoring, or the distance itself creates a clinical risk). Always document the specific medical necessity regardless of distance.

---

**Q:** Can an ambulance provider bill for both the transport base rate AND mileage?

**A:** Yes. Every ambulance claim includes both:
1. The transport base rate code (A0426-A0430, A0428, etc.) -- 1 unit per transport
2. The mileage code (A0425, A0435, A0436) -- 1 unit per statute mile

Both are required for a complete ambulance claim. Do not submit one without the other (unless the transport was cancelled or response-only).

---

**Q:** What is the correct modifier for a patient picked up from a nursing home and transported to the hospital emergency department?

**A:** The modifier is EH (Nursing home to Hospital). E = residential/domiciliary/nursing home (origin), H = hospital (destination). This is one of the most common modifier combinations for nursing home-to-hospital emergency transports.

---

**Q:** A patient calls 911 and an ambulance responds, but the patient refuses transport. How should this be billed?

**A:** Use A0998 (Ambulance response and treatment, no transport). No mileage is billed since the patient was not loaded. No origin/destination modifier is needed (since there is no transport). Some payers may not cover response-only services, so check the specific payer policy.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between ALS and ALS2 (A0433)?

**A:** 
- **ALS (A0426/A0427)**: Transport with one ALS provider (paramedic) in attendance
- **ALS2 (A0433)**: Transport with TWO or more ALS providers (paramedics)

ALS2 is used for the most critically ill patients where one paramedic would be insufficient. Examples: cardiac arrest with ongoing CPR, severe trauma requiring multiple interventions simultaneously, multiple patients in the same transport. Most ambulance transports do not require ALS2.

---

**Q:** A patient is transported from their home to a dialysis center three times per week. The patient is mobile but has a medical condition that requires ambulance transport. What documentation is needed?

**A:** For regular, non-emergency ambulance transport (such as dialysis runs), Medicare requires:
1. A written order from the treating physician
2. A Physician Certification Statement (PCS) signed within 60 days
3. Documentation of medical necessity showing the patient cannot use other transport
4. An origin/destination modifier: RG (Residence to dialysis center)

The medical necessity must be specific to the patient's condition (e.g., the patient requires monitoring during transport due to risk of hypotension, the patient is completely bedridden).

---

**Q:** An ALS ambulance is requested for a patient who is transported from home to the hospital. The patient is stable but the treating physician ordered ALS due to the patient's history of cardiac arrhythmia. What code is appropriate?

**A:** If the patient requires cardiac monitoring during transport and has a history that justifies ALS-level care, use A0427 (ALS emergency transport) with modifier RH. The paramedic should document:
- The patient's history of arrhythmia
- Continuous cardiac monitoring during transport
- Any rhythm changes or management
- The ALS interventions performed

Even if no intervention is needed, the potential for deterioration supports ALS if clearly documented.

---

**Q:** What is the "Medicare Advantage" requirement for prior authorization of non-emergency ambulance transport?

**A:** Many Medicare Advantage plans require prior authorization for non-emergency ambulance transport. The ambulance provider should:
1. Check the patient's plan requirements before transport
2. Obtain prior authorization when required
3. Document the authorization number on the claim
4. If authorization was required but not obtained, the claim may be denied

Traditional Medicare (Part B) does not require prior authorization for ambulance transport, but medical necessity must still be documented.

---

**Q:** How is ambulance transport for a patient with a psychiatric emergency coded?

**A:** The same codes apply:
- A0429 (BLS emergency transport) or A0427 (ALS emergency transport) depending on the patient's condition
- Modifier: typically RH (Residence to Hospital) or SH (Scene to Hospital)
- Documentation should focus on the medical necessity of the patient's condition, including any risk of harm, need for restraint or monitoring, and the inability to use other transport

The key is the patient's condition and the reason ambulance transport was medically necessary, not the psychiatric diagnosis alone.

---

**Q:** What is the definition of "loaded mileage" and how is it calculated?

**A:** Loaded mileage is the distance from the location where the patient is first placed into the ambulance to the location where the patient is removed from the ambulance at the destination. It does NOT include:
- Unloaded miles driven to the pickup location
- Unloaded miles from the destination back to the base
- Miles for multiple stops without the patient on board

For ground transport, the crew typically documents odometer readings at the pickup and destination to calculate loaded miles.

---

**Q:** Can ambulance services be billed with modifier 59?

**A:** No. Modifier 59 (Distinct Procedural Service) is a CPT modifier used for surgical/procedural unbundling. It is not used with ambulance HCPCS codes. The appropriate modifiers for ambulance are the two-character origin/destination modifiers. If you need to indicate a distinct service (e.g., two separate transports on the same day), bill separate claim lines with separate modifiers.

---

**Q:** A patient is transported from one hospital to another for a higher level of care. The patient is on a ventilator. What codes should be used?

**A:** This is an ALS-level or SCT-level transport depending on the team composition:
- **ALS**: If transported by paramedic with ventilator monitoring: A0427 (ALS emergency) with modifier HH (Hospital to Hospital)
- **SCT**: If transported by a critical care nurse or respiratory therapist: A0430 (Specialty Care Transport) with modifier HH

Plus mileage: A0425 (ground mileage, per statute mile).

The critical distinction is the level of care required during transport and the skill set of the transport team.

---

**Q:** What is the "rural adjustment" for ambulance transports?

**A:** Medicare provides a rural adjustment for ground ambulance mileage when the point of pickup is in a rural area:
- Ground mileage is paid at 1.5x the standard rate for miles in rural areas
- This adjustment applies to both emergency and non-emergency transports
- Documentation should note the rural location
- Super-rural areas (lowest 25th percentile population density) receive an additional 25% adjustment

Air ambulance does not receive the rural adjustment.
