# Anesthesia Coding

## Overview

Anesthesia coding is a specialized area of medical coding with unique payment calculations. Unlike most medical services that are paid on a fee schedule or RVU basis, anesthesia services are paid using a **base unit + time unit** model, adjusted by geographic and physical status factors.

**Payment Formula**:

```
Anesthesia Payment = (Base Units + Time Units + Modifying Units) x Conversion Factor
```

## Base Units

**Base units** represent the relative complexity of the anesthetic management of a surgical procedure. They are assigned by CMS for each anesthesia CPT code.

| Procedure | Code | Base Units | Complexity |
|-----------|------|:----------:|:----------:|
| Cataract surgery | 00142 | 5 | Low |
| Lap cholecystectomy | 00790 | 6 | Moderate |
| Hip replacement | 01214 | 7 | Moderate |
| CABG | 00560 | 15 | High |
| Heart transplant | 00580 | 20 | Very high |

Base units are updated annually by CMS. They vary by geographic area (some localities have different base unit assignments from the standard CMS crosswalk).

---

## Time Units

**Time units** compensate the anesthesiologist for the duration of anesthesia care.

- 1 time unit = 15 minutes
- Time begins: When the anesthesia provider begins to prepare the patient for anesthesia care (pre-operative evaluation in the holding area)
- Time ends: When the patient is safely placed under post-anesthesia care (PACU handoff)

**Time documentation requirements**:
- Start time (anesthesia ready time)
- Stop time (anesthesia care complete)
- Total anesthesia minutes documented

**Example**: Anesthesia for hip replacement (01214):
- Base units: 7
- Anesthesia time: 120 minutes
- Time units: 120 / 15 = 8 units
- Total: 7 + 8 = 15 units

## Physical Status Modifiers (P1-P6)

Physical status modifiers indicate the patient's pre-anesthesia medical condition. They are informational (not payment) modifiers but affect the anesthesia conversion factor and payment calculation.

| Modifier | Description | ASA Class | Examples |
|----------|-------------|:---------:|----------|
| P1 | Normal healthy patient | ASA I | No medical problems, non-smoker |
| P2 | Patient with mild systemic disease | ASA II | Well-controlled DM, HTN, obesity, mild COPD |
| P3 | Patient with severe systemic disease | ASA III | Poorly controlled DM, COPD with limitation, CHF, morbid obesity |
| P4 | Patient with severe systemic disease that is a constant threat to life | ASA IV | Recent MI, severe COPD, ESRD, sepsis |
| P5 | Moribund patient not expected to survive without the operation | ASA V | Massive trauma, ruptured AAA, multiple organ failure |
| P6 | Declared brain-dead patient whose organs are being removed for donor purposes | ASA VI | Brain-dead organ donor |

**Impact on payment**: Physical status modifiers P3 and P4 typically add 1 unit to the base units. P5 adds 2 units. P1 and P2 add no additional units.

**Documentation requirement**: The physical status must be documented in the anesthesia record and supported by the patient's medical history.

## Qualifying Circumstances Codes (99100-99140)

Qualifying circumstances codes are **supplemental CPT codes** used to report special circumstances that increase the complexity of anesthesia care. They are billed in addition to the primary anesthesia code.

| Code | Description | Additional Units |
|------|-------------|:----------------:|
| 99100 | Anesthesia for patient of extreme age (under 1 year or over 70 years) | +1 |
| 99116 | Anesthesia complicated by utilization of total body hypothermia | +5 |
| 99135 | Anesthesia complicated by utilization of controlled hypotension | +5 |
| 99140 | Anesthesia complicated by emergency conditions (specify) | +2 |

### Code 99100 -- Extreme Age
- Patient under 1 year OR over 70 years
- The age must be documented
- Billed as add-on to the primary anesthesia code

### Code 99116 -- Total Body Hypothermia
- Used when the patient is intentionally cooled during surgery
- Common in cardiac surgery with CPB
- Document the temperature range and duration

### Code 99135 -- Controlled Hypotension
- Used when deliberate hypotension is induced to reduce bleeding
- Common in spine surgery, major joint replacement, sinus surgery
- Document the target MAP and duration

### Code 99140 -- Emergency Conditions
- A life-threatening condition that requires immediate surgical intervention
- Examples: ruptured AAA, trauma, acute appendicitis, ectopic pregnancy
- Do NOT use for elective procedures
- Document the emergency nature of the surgery
- Cannot be used with P5 (moribund patient)

## Conversion Factor

The **anesthesia conversion factor** is a dollar amount per unit, specific to each geographic locality. It is updated annually by CMS.

**Formula**: Conversion Factor (locality-specific) x Total Units = Payment

**2024 approximate conversion factors**: $22.00 - $25.00 per unit (varies by geographic locality)

| Geographic Locality | Example CF (approximate) |
|--------------------|--------------------------|
| National average | $22.50 |
| High cost (NYC, SF) | $25.00+ |
| Medium cost (Midwest) | $21.50-$23.50 |
| Low cost (Rural) | $20.00-$21.50 |

## Anesthesia Provider Modifiers

| Modifier | Provider Type | Payment |
|----------|---------------|---------|
| AA | Anesthesia services performed personally by an anesthesiologist | 100% |
| AD | Medical supervision by a physician: more than 4 concurrent cases | 50% of allowable |
| QK | Medical direction of 2-4 CRNAs by an anesthesiologist | 50% each |
| QX | CRNA service: medically directed by a physician | 50% (split with anesthesiologist) |
| QY | Medical direction of one CRNA by an anesthesiologist | 50% each |
| QZ | CRNA service: NOT medically directed by a physician | 100% to CRNA |
| GC | Service performed in part by a resident under the direction of a teaching physician | Varies |

### Medical Direction (QK, QY, QX)

An anesthesiologist can **medically direct** up to 4 concurrent anesthesia providers (CRNAs) for CMS to allow the full 50/50 split.

Requirements for medical direction (all must be met):
1. Pre-anesthetic evaluation and plan by the anesthesiologist
2. Personal participation in the most demanding aspects of the anesthesia plan
3. Remains immediately available during the procedure
4. Provides post-anesthesia care
5. Documents the medical direction

If any of these requirements are not met, the service is considered **medical supervision** (modifier AD), which pays less.

## Anesthesia Payment Calculation Examples

### Example 1: Personal Performance by Anesthesiologist

**Scenario**: Anesthesiologist personally performs anesthesia for a total hip replacement (01214). Patient is ASA III (P3). Anesthesia time: 105 minutes.

- CPT: 01214 (base units = 7)
- Physical status: P3 (+1 unit)
- Time: 105 minutes = 7 units (105/15 = 7)
- Qualifying circumstances: None
- **Total units**: 7 + 7 + 1 = 15 units
- Conversion factor: $23.00
- **Payment**: 15 x $23.00 = $345.00

### Example 2: Medical Direction

**Scenario**: Anesthesiologist medically directs a CRNA for a laparoscopic cholecystectomy (00790). Patient is ASA II (P2). Anesthesia time: 90 minutes.

- CPT: 00790 (base units = 6)
- Physical status: P2 (no addition)
- Time: 90 minutes = 6 units
- Medical direction: QK modifier
- **Total units**: 6 + 6 = 12 units
- Conversion factor: $23.00
- **Payment**: 12 x $23.00 = $276.00 (split $138 each between anesthesiologist and CRNA)

### Example 3: Emergency Surgery

**Scenario**: Anesthesia for emergency laparotomy for ruptured AAA (00790). Patient is ASA IVE (P4, emergency). Anesthesia time: 180 minutes.

- CPT: 00790 (base units = 7)
- Physical status: P4 (+1 unit for severe disease)
- Qualifying circumstances: 99140 (emergency, +2 units)
- Time: 180 minutes = 12 units
- **Total units**: 7 + 12 + 1 + 2 = 22 units
- Conversion factor: $23.00
- **Payment**: 22 x $23.00 = $506.00

## Monitored Anesthesia Care (MAC)

MAC is reported using the same anesthesia CPT codes as general anesthesia. The same base units, time units, and physical status modifiers apply.

**MAC requirement**: The anesthesia provider must be present and ready to convert to general anesthesia if necessary.

## Denial Reasons in Anesthesia Coding

| Denial Reason | Common Cause | Solution |
|---------------|--------------|----------|
| Missing time documentation | Anesthesia start/stop not recorded | Ensure all times are documented |
| Wrong physical status modifier | P2 documented but P3 warranted | Ensure ASA class is accurately documented |
| Incorrect base units | Wrong CPT code for the procedure | Verify base unit assignment |
| Modifier mismatch | AA vs. QK/QY for medical direction | Determine correct supervision level |
| Missing qualifying circumstances | Emergency not documented | Document emergency nature |
| Bundled services | Post-op pain management billed separately | Check if included in anesthesia code |
| Duplicate payment | Two providers billing same case | Coordinate with other anesthesia providers |
| Medical supervision (AD) used incorrectly | Should have been medical direction (QK) | Document medical direction requirements |

## Q&A Pairs

**Q:** How are anesthesia base units determined?

**A:** Base units are assigned by CMS for each CPT anesthesia code (00100-01999). They represent the relative complexity of the anesthetic management required for the procedure. For example, cataract surgery (00142) has 5 base units, while a coronary artery bypass graft (00560) has 15 base units. The base unit amounts are updated annually in the Medicare Physician Fee Schedule and can vary slightly by geographic locality.

---

**Q:** What is the calculation for anesthesia time units?

**A:** Time is calculated as:
1. **Start time**: When the anesthesia provider begins to prepare the patient (pre-op evaluation, IV access, monitors)
2. **Stop time**: When the anesthesia provider ends care (PACU handoff, patient stable)
3. **Duration**: Total minutes between start and stop
4. **Time units**: Total minutes divided by 15, rounded to nearest 15-minute increment

Example: 120 minutes of anesthesia = 8 time units (120/15 = 8).

---

**Q:** When should physical status modifier P3 vs. P4 be used?

**A:** 
- **P3 (ASA III)**: Severe systemic disease with substantial functional limitations. The patient has a condition that is not immediately life-threatening but significantly affects daily activity. Examples: poorly controlled diabetes, stable COPD with dyspnea on exertion, morbid obesity (BMI >40), stable CHF.
- **P4 (ASA IV)**: Severe systemic disease that is a CONSTANT threat to life. Examples: recent MI (< 30 days), severe COPD with hypercapnia, unstable angina, severe sepsis, end-stage renal disease on dialysis.

The key difference: P4 conditions are immediately life-threatening if not actively managed.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between medical direction (QK/QY) and medical supervision (AD)?

**A:** 
- **Medical direction (QK/QY)**: An anesthesiologist directs up to 4 concurrent CRNAs/AA. Requires all 5 conditions (pre-op evaluation, participation in demanding aspects, immediate availability, post-op care, documentation). Payment: 50% of allowable to anesthesiologist, 50% to CRNA.
- **Medical supervision (AD)**: An anesthesiologist supervises MORE than 4 concurrent cases. Does not require all 5 conditions. Payment: Only 50% of the amount that would have been paid under medical direction. The CRNA still receives 50%.

**Important**: If an anesthesiologist is directing fewer than 4 cases but does not meet all 5 conditions, the service SHOULD be billed as medical supervision (AD).

---

**Q:** Can a CRNA bill anesthesia services without medical direction?

**A:** Yes. Use modifier QZ (CRNA service: NOT medically directed by a physician). The CRNA bills 100% of the allowable amount (at the CRNA's rate, which is typically 85% of the physician fee schedule). The QZ modifier indicates anesthesiologist involvement is not present or not required.

---

**Q:** What qualifying circumstances code is used for emergency surgery?

**A:** Use 99140 (Anesthesia complicated by emergency conditions). This code adds 2 base units. An emergency condition is defined as a life-threatening situation requiring immediate surgical intervention. Do not use 99140 for:
- Elective surgeries
- Urgent but not immediately life-threatening conditions
- Procedures scheduled in advance

Documentation must specifically state the emergency nature of the surgery.

---

**Q:** How is anesthesia for a patient under 1 year of age coded?

**A:** Use:
1. The appropriate anesthesia code for the procedure (e.g., 00140 for eye surgery, 00820 for appendectomy)
2. Physical status modifier (P1-P6)
3. Qualifying circumstances code 99100 (Extreme age, patient under 1 year) -- adds 1 base unit

The extreme age code is used for both under 1 year and over 70 years. If the patient is both extreme age AND emergency, both 99100 and 99140 can be used.

---

**Q:** A patient undergoes a laparoscopic cholecystectomy that converts to open. How is the anesthesia coded?

**A:** Bill the anesthesia code for the most complex procedure performed. If the procedure started laparoscopically and converted to open:
- If the majority of the surgery was laparoscopic: 00790 (base 6 units)
- If the majority of the surgery was open: 00840 (base 6 units) or 00790
- Either way, the base units for these codes are similar

The key is the time documentation. The full anesthesia time (including both laparoscopic and open portions) is used to calculate time units.

---

**Q:** What is the correct code for Monitored Anesthesia Care (MAC)?

**A:** MAC is billed using the same CPT code as for general anesthesia for the same procedure (e.g., 00142 for cataract surgery MAC). The same base units, physical status modifier, and time units apply. Some payers may require modifier QS (Monitored Anesthesia Care) to indicate MAC rather than general anesthesia. Check payer-specific requirements.

---

**Q:** Can an anesthesiologist bill for both the anesthesia and the pain management (e.g., epidural for post-op pain)?

**A:** The initial placement of an epidural for surgical anesthesia is included in the anesthesia code (e.g., 01960 for labor epidural). Post-operative epidural management is separately reportable with 01996 (Daily management of epidural drug therapy). This code is used for each day of continued epidural management after the initial placement. The global surgical package for surgery does not include the daily management of the epidural.

**Q:** What is the correct way to handle anesthesia for a bilateral procedure?

**A:** For bilateral procedures, bill the anesthesia code with the appropriate modifier:
- If the CPT code for the bilateral procedure is reported with modifier 50: no change to anesthesia code (anesthesia has its own base units)
- Some anesthesia codes specifically address bilateral procedures (e.g., 01215 for bilateral knee replacement with base units of 9)
- For unilateral codes performed bilaterally: Use the same anesthesia code for both sides. The total base units are the same as for one side (anesthesia does not double-base for bilateral)

Time units account for the extra time required for bilateral procedures.

**Q:** Can an anesthesia provider bill for both an E/M service and anesthesia on the same day?

**A:** This is very rare and typically only appropriate when:
1. The E/M is for a completely separate, unrelated problem
2. The E/M is a significant, separately identifiable service

For most anesthesia encounters, the pre-operative evaluation is included in the anesthesia code and should NOT be billed separately. Append modifier 25 to the E/M code if appropriate. Most payers will deny same-day E/M with anesthesia.

**Q:** What is the definition of "immediately available" for medical direction?

**A:** "Immediately available" means the anesthesiologist is in the same building, on the same floor, and can reach the operating room within minutes. The anesthesiologist cannot be:
- In another building
- In a different hospital
- Performing a procedure themselves
- Unavailable for any significant period

If the anesthesiologist is not immediately available, the service should be billed as medical supervision (AD).

**Q:** Can anesthesia services be billed for radiology procedures (MRI, CT, radiation therapy)?

**A:** Yes. Use the appropriate anesthesia code:
- 01925 (Anesthesia for MRI)
- 01926 (Anesthesia for CT scan)
- 01924 (Anesthesia for radiation therapy)

These have low base units (3) since the procedures themselves are not surgically invasive. However, the anesthesia time can be lengthy, and the patient may have comorbidities that increase the complexity.

**Q:** What is the correct code for anesthesia for a transcatheter aortic valve replacement (TAVR)?

**A:** Use 00530 (Anesthesia for transcatheter aortic valve replacement [TAVR]). This code has 12 base units. TAVR is a complex, high-risk procedure often performed on elderly patients with multiple comorbidities. Qualifying circumstances for extreme age (99100) may also apply. The anesthesia provider must be prepared for potential complications including hemodynamic instability, vascular injury, and conversion to open surgery.

**Q:** What documentation is needed to support anesthesia time?

**A:** The anesthesia record must document:
1. **Anesthesia start time**: When the provider begins to prepare the patient
2. **Surgery start time**: When the incision is made
3. **Surgery end time**: When the procedure is complete
4. **Anesthesia end time**: When the provider turns over care to PACU
5. **Total anesthesia time**: Minutes and time units
6. **Type of anesthesia**: General, MAC, regional, etc.
7. **Medications**: All drugs administered
8. **Vital signs**: Throughout the procedure
9. **Physical status**: ASA classification
10. **Name and signature**: Of the anesthesia provider

**Q:** How is anesthesia for obstetrics (labor epidural) billed?

**A:** 
- **Initial epidural placement**: 01960 (Anesthesia for vaginal delivery). Base units = 5. Bill once for the initial placement.
- **Daily management**: 01996 (Daily management of epidural drug therapy). Base units = 2. Bill per day of continued management.
- **C-section**: 01961 (C-section, not otherwise specified) or 01962 (emergency C-section). Do NOT also bill 01960.
- **Postpartum tubal ligation**: 01967 (Postpartum tubal ligation)

**Q:** What is anesthesia "conversion" and how does it affect coding?

**A:** Anesthesia conversion refers to:
1. **Conversion from regional to general**: If regional anesthesia (e.g., spinal, epidural) is attempted but fails and general anesthesia is required, only the general anesthesia code is reported (since it is the most complex and definitive).
2. **Conversion from MAC to general**: Same principle. Report the general anesthesia code.
3. **Procedure conversion**: If the surgical procedure changes (e.g., laparoscopy converts to open), use the anesthesia code for the more complex/comprehensive surgical procedure.

Time units include the entire anesthesia time, including the period before the conversion.

---

**Q:** What is the correct modifier for anesthesia for ECT (electroconvulsive therapy)?

**A:** Use 01900 (Anesthesia for ECT). Base units = 4. No physical status modifier is required for basic cases. ECT is typically a short procedure, so time units are low. The ECT anesthesia code includes all services provided during the ECT session, including pre-ECT evaluation and post-ECT recovery.
